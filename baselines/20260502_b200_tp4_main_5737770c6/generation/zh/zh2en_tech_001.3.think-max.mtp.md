Bamboo papermaking originates in the south of China, but Fujian province is particularly famed for it. After bamboo shoots have grown, the best raw material is bamboo that is about to sprout branches and leaves – this is judged by examining the depth of the bamboo groves in the mountains. They cut it into lengths of five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.6–2.3 meters) and dig a soaking pit right there on the mountain. Water is channeled into the pit; in case of drying, they use bamboo pipes to feed a continuous stream of water into the pit. The bamboo soaks for over a hundred days. Then it is beaten and washed to remove the coarse outer skin and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber, now resembling ramie, is treated with a lime slurry (made from high-quality lime dissolved in water) and placed into a large vat for boiling. The boiling lasts exactly eight days and eight nights.

The boiling vessel is an iron pot about four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter. Its rim is sealed with a mixture of mud and lime, forming a high, wide edge similar to the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong. The pot can hold over ten *dan* (approx. 600 liters) of water. A large wooden vat (the *huangtong*) – about 1.5 *zhang* (5 meters) in circumference and over four *chi* (1.3 meters) in diameter – is placed over the pot as a lid. After eight days of boiling, the fire is stopped for one day. The vat is then opened, and the bamboo pulp (now called "bamboo hemp") is taken out and washed clean in a clean water tank. The bottom and sides of this tank are lined with wooden boards, tightly joined to prevent mud from contaminating the pulp (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is treated with wood-ash lye, then placed back into a pot, leveled, and covered with a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (3.3 cm) thick. Boiling water is poured over it, and the liquid is repeatedly drained and reapplied – if it cools, it is reheated. This process continues for about ten days, until the pulp naturally rots and softens.

The softened pulp is then taken out and pounded in a mortar (in mountainous areas, water-powered trip-hammers are used). It is beaten until it becomes a smooth, mud-like paste. This paste is then transferred into a papermaking vat.

The papermaking vat is rectangular, its dimensions adjusted according to the size of the mold (the *lian* frame) and the desired paper size. The bamboo pulp is placed in the vat, covered with about three *cun* (10 cm) of clear water. A "paper medicine" solution is added – a plant-based liquid (often made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with local names varying). This helps the water drain away, leaving the paper white and dry.

The paper mold (*lian*) is made of extremely fine, polished bamboo strips woven into a screen, mounted on a rectangular wooden frame that can be opened and closed. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and scoops up the pulp. By gently shaking the mold, the pulp spreads evenly. The thickness of the paper depends on the skill of the worker: a light shake yields thin paper, a heavy shake thick paper. As the bamboo fibers settle on the screen, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The mold is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a stack of paper. This is repeated until thousands of sheets are piled up.

When the stack is high enough, a board is placed on top, and a rope is tightened around it using a lever (like pressing wine) to squeeze out as much water as possible. The sheets are then peeled off one by one with fine copper tweezers and dried.

For drying, a brick chamber is built like a narrow hallway. The floor is paved with bricks, leaving gaps between some of them. A fire is lit at one end, and the heat travels through the gaps, heating the bricks. The damp paper sheets are pasted onto the hot bricks to dry. Once dry, they are collected into bundles.

In recent times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" became prized for writing and calligraphy. Waste paper, after washing off ink and colors, can be soaked, rotted, and re-pulped to make new paper – this saves much of the soaking and boiling effort, with little material loss. In southern regions where bamboo is cheap, this is not practiced much. But in the north, even small scraps are collected and recycled into "soul-returning paper." Whether bamboo or bark, fine or coarse, the principle is the same.

For "fire paper" (used as spirit money) and rough paper, the process of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, treating with ash lye, and rinsing is identical. However, after removal from the mold, the sheets are not dried on heated bricks. Instead, they are pressed to remove water and then sun-dried.

For festive red paper, the sheets are first dipped in alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

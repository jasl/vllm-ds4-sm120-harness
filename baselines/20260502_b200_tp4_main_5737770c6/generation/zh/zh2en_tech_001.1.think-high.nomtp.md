**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially famous for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one inspects the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow leaves is the best material. Around the time of the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), one goes up the mountain to cut the bamboo. It is cut into pieces five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.7–2.3 m) long. A pond is dug right there on the mountain, filled with water, and the bamboo is soaked in it. To prevent the pond water from drying up, bamboo pipes are used to channel a continuous flow of water into it. After soaking for over one hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the outer coarse shell and green bark (this step is called *sha qing*, "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber, which looks like ramie, is then coated with a lime paste made from high-quality lime dissolved in water, placed into a *huang* tub (a large wooden vat), and boiled for a full eight days and eight nights.

The boiling pot used is four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. The rim of the pot is built up with mud and lime, making it as tall and wide as the salt boiling pans used in Guangdong, capable of holding over ten *dan* (approx. 1,000 L) of water. The *huang* tub, which is placed on top, has a circumference of fifteen *chi* (approx. 5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m). After sealing it for boiling, the eight days are completed. The fire is stopped for one day. Then the tub is opened, and the bamboo pulp is taken out and washed clean in a clear-water pond. The bottom and four sides of this pond must be lined with tightly fitted planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is coated with wood-ash lye and put back into a pot. It is leveled flat, and a one-*cun* (approx. 3 cm) layer of rice straw ash is spread evenly on top. When the water in the tub boils, the pulp is taken out and placed into another tub, where the ash lye is poured over it again. If the liquid cools, it is boiled again and poured once more. This process is repeated for over ten days, until the bamboo naturally rots and becomes soft. The pulp is then taken out and put into a mortar to be beaten (water-powered trip-hammers are used in mountain regions), until it becomes as fine as mud. It is then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat is square at the top. Its dimensions are determined by the paper mold, and the paper mold's dimensions are determined by the desired paper size. The bamboo pulp is now ready. The vat is filled with clear water to a depth of about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. A papermaking drug solution (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with various local names) is added to the vat. This causes the water to drain away cleanly, leaving white paper.

The papermaking mold is made of extremely fine, polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. When opened and spread, it has a supporting wooden frame underneath. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts a layer of bamboo pulp onto the screen. The thickness of the paper sheet is controlled by hand technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the bamboo pulp lies on the screen, water drains from all four sides back into the vat. The mold is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is complete, a board is placed on top and pressed down with a lever and rope, in the same manner as pressing wine, to squeeze out all remaining water. After the water is drained, each sheet is lifted one by one with fine, light copper tweezers and dried on a heated wall.

To dry the paper, a brick wall is built with a hollow passage (flue) inside. The floor of the flue is covered with bricks, but every few bricks, one is left out to create an opening. A fire is lit in a hole at one end; the hot air and smoke pass through the flue and heat the outer bricks. The wet sheets are pasted onto the hot brick wall, dried, and then removed as finished quires.

In recent times, a wide format called "Big Four-Link" has become valued for writing and official documents. Waste paper, with its red ink (from vermilion) and black ink washed off, can be soaked, rotted, and re-processed in the vat. This saves all the previous steps of boiling and soaking, yet still produces paper with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, even small scraps of paper are picked up from the ground and re-processed. This is called "returned-soul paper." Bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, are all made this way.

For "fire paper" and coarse paper, the steps of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, applying ash lye, and water rinsing are all the same. The only difference is that after the paper leaves the mold, it is not heated on a wall. Instead, it is simply pressed to remove water and sun-dried.

During the High Tang period, religious and funerary practices were elaborate. Paper money was burned as a substitute for silk cloth (in the north, cut strips were used, called "board money"). Therefore, this type of paper is called "fire paper." In the regions of Jing and Chu (modern Hubei and Hunan), a single funeral may burn up to 1,000 *jin* (approx. 600 kg) of it. About 70% of this paper is used for burning as offerings to spirits; the remaining 30% is used for daily purposes. The coarsest and thickest kind is called "wrapping paper," which is made from bamboo pulp mixed with the straw of the previous year's late rice.

For letter paper, such as that made in Yanshan County, it is made entirely from fine bamboo material, formed into a thick sheet to command a high price. The highest grade is called "official letter paper." Wealthy and noble families use it for name cards and correspondence. It is thick and sturdy, with no coarse fibers. To make red paper for auspicious occasions (such as wedding invitations), the paper is first treated with an alum solution and then dyed with safflower juice.

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially renowned for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, the best material is bamboo that is about to sprout leaves, taken from deep in the mountains. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is beaten and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner fiber, now looking like ramie, is then coated with a paste made from high-quality lime dissolved in water. This is placed into a wooden vat and boiled over a fire for a full eight days and eight nights.

The boiling vat sits on a pot measuring four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. The rim of the pot is built up with clay and lime, forming a large, high-lipped vessel similar to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, capable of holding over ten *dan* (approx. 500–1000 L) of water. The wooden vat placed on top has a circumference of fifteen *chi* (approx. 5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m). After sealing, the material boils for eight full days. The fire is then allowed to go out for one day. After that, the vat is opened and the bamboo fiber (now called "bamboo hemp") is taken out and washed clean in a soaking pit filled with clear water. The bottom and sides of this pit must be lined with tightly joined wooden planks to prevent mud from contaminating the pulp (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the bamboo hemp is treated with a slurry of wood ash and then put back into a pot. It is leveled, and a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick is spread evenly on top. When the water in the pot boils, the liquid is ladled out and poured back over the material in another vat. If the liquid cools, it is reheated before being poured again. This process is repeated for over ten days, until the fibers naturally rot and become soft. The rotten mass is then taken out and placed into a mortar for pounding (in mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers are used). It is pounded until it has the consistency of mud, and then it is poured into a vat.

A papermaking vat is square at the top. The dimensions of the vat depend on the mold, and the mold's dimensions depend on the size of the paper being made. Once the bamboo hemp pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water until the pulp is submerged about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) deep. A "paper medicine" liquid (a plant extract, often from the leaves of certain shrubs like *Pteroceltis* or *Hibiscus*, known by various local names) is added. This makes the fibers bond evenly and turn white as the water drains.

The papermaking mold is made of extremely fine, polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. This screen can be rolled or spread flat and is supported by a wooden frame structure. The papermaker holds the mold with both hands and dips it into the vat, lifting up the bamboo pulp so it settles on the mold's screen. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by the worker's technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a vigorous one makes thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains through the gaps back into the vat. The mold is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is high enough, a board is placed on top, ropes are tightened, and a lever is used to press the stack, similar to pressing wine, to squeeze out all remaining water. After pressing, each sheet is lifted individually with fine copper tweezers and dried on a heated wall.

The drying wall is built from brick. It forms a long, narrow chamber (like a flue). The floor of the chamber is paved with bricks, with every few bricks having one missing to create air inlets. A fire is lit at one end; the hot air and smoke travel through the chamber and heat the brick wall on the outside. The wet paper sheets are pasted one by one onto this hot brick wall to dry. Once dry, they are peeled off and gathered into quires.

In recent times, wide-format paper, called "Big Four-Link," has been valued for writing and documents. Waste paper is washed to remove red ink and other stains, soaked until it falls apart, and added back into the vat for reuse. This process saves all the previous steps of soaking, boiling, and pounding, yet still produces usable paper with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. In the north, even scraps an inch or two in size are picked up and remade into so-called "returned-soul paper."

Then, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the coarse outer husk and green skin (this is called *sha qing*, "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber will now resemble ramie (a plant fiber). Prepare a lime solution (mix good-quality lime with water to make a paste), coat the bamboo with it, place it into a *huang* bucket (a large wooden vat used for boiling), and boil for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling bamboo, use a pot about 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. Build up a rim on the pot using clay mixed with lime, making it tall and wide, like the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong, able to hold over ten *shi* (approx. 1,000 L) of water. Cover this with an inverted *huang* bucket, which measures about 15 *chi* (approx. 5 m) in circumference and 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. Once sealed, boil for a full eight days. Let it rest for one day, then remove the lid and take out the cooked bamboo pulp. Wash it in a clean soaking pond (the bottom and sides of this pond must be lined with wooden boards, tightly joined, to prevent mud contamination – this step is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, apply a slurry of wood ash (柴灰) to the pulp, then put it back into a pot. Level the pulp, and spread about an inch (approx. 3 cm) of rice straw ash evenly over the top. When the water in the bucket boils, ladle the hot ash-water over the pulp, then drain into another bucket. If the liquid cools, reheat it to boiling and pour again. Repeat this for about ten days, until the natural rotting process makes the pulp soft and mushy. Then take it out and beat it in a mortar (in mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers (水碓) are used). Beat until the pulp becomes as fine as mud, then pour it into a vat.

For the papermaking vat (抄紙槽): its top opening is a rectangular frame, sized according to the mold. The mold's size determines the paper's dimensions. Add about three inches (approx. 10 cm) of clear water above the beaten bamboo pulp in the vat. Add papermaking drug liquor (紙藥水汁, made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves – local names vary). This additive makes the water film contract evenly so the paper becomes pure white. The papermaking mold (抄紙簾) is made from very fine, polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is mounted in a frame with vertical and horizontal supports. Hold the screen with both hands, dip it into the vat, and lift it so the bamboo pulp is suspended and deposited evenly on the screen. Thickness is controlled by the operator's technique: a gentle shake yields thin paper, a vigorous one yields thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains from all four sides back into the vat. Then, flip the screen to transfer the wet paper sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. Once the stack reaches the desired height, place a board on top, apply pressure, tie the stack with ropes, insert wedges, and tighten like a wine press, to squeeze out all excess water. After pressing, use fine copper tweezers to lift each sheet one by one, then dry them on a heated wall. For drying paper (焙紙), first build a double-walled chamber (or drying tunnel) with brick sides. The floor of the tunnel is made of bricks laid with gaps (every few bricks leave one missing). A fire is lit at one end; heat and smoke pass through the gaps, heating all the bricks. Damp paper sheets are pasted onto the hot brick walls to dry, then peeled off and gathered into quires (folded bundles).

In recent times, a wide-format size called "Da Si Lian" (Big Four-Joined) became prized for official documents and letters.

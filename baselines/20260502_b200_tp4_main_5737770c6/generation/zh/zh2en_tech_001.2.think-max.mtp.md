**Papermaking from Bamboo**
*(from *Tiangong Kaiwu*, translated for clarity)*

Bamboo paper is made in the south, especially in Fujian province. After bamboo shoots grow, look for bamboo in deep valleys—bamboo that is about to sprout branches and leaves is the best material. Around the *Mangzhong* solar term (early June), cut the bamboo on the mountain into sections 5–7 *chi* long (approx. 1.7–2.3 m). Dig a pit on the mountain, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo. If the pit might dry up, use bamboo pipes to channel a constant stream of water into it. Soak for over 100 days, then pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called *shaqing*, “killing the green”). The remaining bamboo fiber looks like ramie. Mix slaked lime into a paste, coat the bamboo with it, and place it in a large vat for boiling. Boil for exactly eight days and eight nights.

The boiling pot should be 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. Its rim is sealed with clay and lime, similar to the large salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong, and can hold over 10 *shi* (approx. 600 liters) of water. Cover it with a vat (circumference 15 *chi*, diameter approx. 4.5 *chi*). After boiling for eight days, let it rest for one day. Then open the vat, take out the bamboo pulp (called *zhuma*), and wash it in a clean soaking pit. The pit’s bottom and sides must be lined with tightly joined wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for coarse paper). After washing, mix the pulp with wood-ash lye and put it back into the pot. Level the surface and spread a layer of rice straw ash about 1 *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick on top. When the liquid in the pot boils, transfer the pulp to another vat, and pour the hot lye over it. If the lye cools, reheat it and pour again. Repeat for about ten days, until the pulp rots and becomes soft. Then take it out and pound it in a mortar (in mountainous areas, water-powered trip-hammers are used). Pound until it has the consistency of wet clay, then transfer it to a vat.

**The papermaking vat** is rectangular. Its dimensions depend on the size of the mold (called a *lian*, a screen), and the mold’s size depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water to about 3 *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp, and add a plant-based sizing liquid (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves; local names vary). This sizing helps the pulp drain and form clean, white paper.

**The paper mold** is woven from very fine, polished bamboo strips. It is stretched over a rectangular frame. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and scoops up a layer of pulp. The thickness of the paper depends on the technique: a gentle shake makes thin paper; a vigorous shake makes thick paper. As the pulp settles on the mold, water drains through the screen back into the vat. Then the mold is turned over to transfer the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is complete, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever-and-rope system (like a wine press) to squeeze out all the water.

After pressing, each sheet is lifted with fine copper tweezers and dried on a heated wall. The drying wall is built from brick, forming a narrow tunnel. The floor of the tunnel is tiled, with every few bricks replaced by a gap. A fire is lit at one end of the tunnel; the heat passes through the gaps and warms the bricks. Wet sheets are pasted onto the hot bricks to dry, then peeled off and bundled into quires.

**Large-format paper**, called *Dasilian* (Big Four-Sheet), was highly valued for writing and calligraphy. Waste paper (even with ink or vermilion stains) can be soaked, rotted, and repulped in the vat, skipping the boiling and soaking steps. This recycled paper loses little material. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not done. But in the north, even tiny scraps are collected and remade into “soul-returning paper” (*huanhun zhi*). The process is the same for bamboo and bark papers, whether fine or coarse.

**Fire paper** and **rough paper** follow the same steps: cutting bamboo, boiling pulp, lye treatment, and washing. But after the mold, they are not dried on a heated wall—they are simply pressed and sun-dried.

**Fire paper** (for burning as offerings) originated in the Tang dynasty, when paper money replaced burned silk as a spirit offering. (In the north, strips of this paper are called *banqian*, “board money.”) In parts of Hubei and Hunan, people sometimes burn up to 500 kg at a funeral. About 70% of this paper is used for spirit offerings; the rest is for daily use. The coarsest, thickest type is **wrapping paper**, made from bamboo pulp mixed with old rice straw.

**Letter paper** made in Yanshan and nearby counties uses only fine bamboo pulp, formed thickly on the mold, and commands a high price. The best grade is called *guanjian* (official letter paper), used by wealthy families for name cards. It is thick and free of fiber clumps. For red “auspicious” letter paper, the sheets are first dipped in alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

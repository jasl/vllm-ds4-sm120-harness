**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking originates in the south, but Fujian province is particularly renowned for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one examines the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow branches and leaves is the best material. On the mountain itself, a pond is dug, filled with water, and the bamboo is soaked in it. To prevent the pond from drying out, bamboo pipes are used to channel a continuous stream of flowing water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The remaining bamboo fiber resembles ramie in appearance. A paste is made from high-quality lime dissolved in water, which is then applied to the fibers. This is placed into a *huang* vat (a large covered pot) and boiled over a fire for exactly eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron with a diameter of four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) is used. The rim of the cauldron is sealed with clay and lime, making it tall and wide, similar in shape to the large salt-boiling pans of Guangdong. It can hold over ten *dan* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. The *huang* vat, which serves as a lid, has a circumference of 1.5 *zhang* (approx. 5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m). After covering, the material boils for eight full days. The fire is then stopped for one day. The *huang* vat is opened, and the bamboo pulp (called "bamboo hemp") is removed. It is then washed clean in a clear-water soaking pond. The bottom and all four sides of this pond must be sealed with wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is treated with a wood-ash lye. It is put back into a pot, smoothed flat on top, and covered with a one-cun-thick (approx. 3.3 cm) layer of rice straw ash. When the liquid in the pot boils, the pulp is removed and placed into a separate vat, where the ash lye liquid is poured over it again. If the lye cools, it is reheated to a boil before pouring. This process is repeated for over ten days, until the material naturally rots and becomes soft. The pulp is then taken out and placed in a mortar for pounding (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers). It is pounded until it has the consistency of mud or paste, then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat is square, with dimensions matching the paper size. The vat's size depends on the paper mold, and the mold's size depends on the paper. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to a depth of about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp's surface. A "paper medicine" liquid (a plant extract, often from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name) is added. This causes the water to drain away, leaving the paper clean and white. The papermaking mold is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven together. When unrolled, it is supported by a horizontal and vertical frame underneath. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts the bamboo pulp, spreading it evenly onto the mold surface. The paper's thickness depends on the worker's technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a vigorous lift makes thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the mold, water drains from all four sides back into the vat. The mold is then turned over to deposit the wet sheet onto a board. This is repeated, stacking up thousands of sheets. When the stack reaches the desired height, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed down and tightened with ropes and wedges, similar to pressing wine in a winepress, until all water is squeezed out and drained away. Then, each sheet is carefully lifted with fine copper tweezers and dried over a heater.

The drying kiln is made by building a narrow, double-walled flue from earthen bricks. The floor of the flue is also covered with bricks, leaving a gap for one brick every few bricks. A fire is lit in a hole at one end of the flue. The hot air and flames travel through the gaps between the bricks, heating the entire outer brick wall. Wet paper sheets are then pasted onto this hot brick wall to dry. Once dry, they are lifted off and gathered into a stack (a *zhi*—a unit of paper).

In recent times, a wide format called "Big Four-Linked" has become valuable for writing and documents. Waste paper, after washing off red ink and dirt, is soaked, rotted, and put back into the vat for remanufacturing. This process entirely saves the earlier steps of boiling and soaking, yet it still produces usable paper with little material loss. In southern regions where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered worthwhile. In the north, however, even a small piece or scrap on the ground is picked up and recycled.

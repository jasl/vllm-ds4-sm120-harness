**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. When bamboo shoots have grown, choose bamboos from deep mountain valleys that are about to sprout branches and leaves—this is the best material. Around the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), go up the mountain to cut them down, cut the stems into sections 1.5–2 meters (5–7 *chi*) long, dig a pit on the mountain, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo in it. To keep the pit from drying out, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over 100 days, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner fiber, now resembling ramie, is then coated with a paste made from quicklime dissolved in water, placed in a wooden vat over a cauldron, and boiled for exactly eight days and eight nights.

The cauldron used for boiling is about 1.3 meters (4 *chi*) in diameter. Its rim is sealed with clay and lime, built up high and wide like the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, and can hold over 130 liters (10+ *shi*) of water. A wooden vat, about 5 meters (15 *chi*) in circumference and 1.3 meters (4 *chi*) in diameter, is placed on top and sealed. After eight days of boiling, let it rest for one day, then open the vat, take out the bamboo pulp, and wash it in a clean soaking pit. This pit must have its bottom and all four sides lined with tightly fitted planks to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, mix the pulp with wood-ash lye, put it back into the cauldron, level it, and spread a layer of rice straw ash about 3 cm (1 *cun*) thick on top. When the water in the cauldron boils, transfer the pulp to another vat and pour the ash lye over it again. If the lye cools, reheat it before pouring. Repeat for over ten days, until the pulp naturally rots and becomes soft. Then take it out, put it into a mortar, and pound it (in mountainous areas, water-powered trip-hammers are used) until it has the consistency of mud. Pour this into a vat.

The papermaking vat is a rectangular trough. The width of the trough is determined by the mold (screen), and the mold is sized for the paper. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the trough with clean water to about 10 cm (3 *cun*) above the pulp, and add a "paper drug" liquid (made from leaves like peach or bamboo—local names vary). This makes the water drain away cleanly, leaving white paper.

The papermaking mold is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is stretched over a rectangular frame. Hold the mold with both hands, dip it into the vat, and lift it so that the bamboo fibers float onto the screen. The thickness of the paper depends on the operator's technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper; a heavy lift makes thick paper. Water drains back into the trough through the edges of the screen. Then, turn the mold over to deposit the sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. When the stack is complete, press it with a board on top, tighten it with ropes and a lever (like pressing wine), to squeeze out all the water. Then, use fine copper tweezers to lift each sheet one by one and dry them on a heated wall.

For drying, first build a narrow chamber with brick walls, and lay bricks on the floor with one brick missing every few bricks. Light a fire at one end; the flames and heat pass through the gaps in the floor bricks, heating the walls. Wet sheets are pasted onto the hot walls, dried, and then removed to form quires.

In recent times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" has been valued for writing and documents. Waste paper (with ink and vermilion washed off) can be soaked, rotted, put back into the vat, and remade into paper, saving the entire boiling and soaking steps—the loss is small. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered worthwhile. In the north, even scraps of paper are collected, recycled, and made into "returned-soul paper." Bamboo paper and bark paper, fine and coarse, are all made this way.

For "fire paper" and coarse paper, the steps are the same: cut bamboo, boil it, treat it with ash and lye. But after removing the sheet from the mold, they are not heated on a wall—instead, they are pressed and then sun-dried.

**Making Bamboo Paper**
*(From *Tiangong Kaiwu*, by Song Yingxing)*

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots have grown, choose bamboos that are about to sprout branches and leaves – these are the best material. Around the time of *Mangzhong* (Grain in Ear, early June), go up the mountain and cut them down. Cut the stems into pieces five to seven *chi* long (roughly 1.7–2.3 m). On the mountain itself, dig a pond, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo pieces. If the pond might dry up, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. Soak for over a hundred days. Then pound and wash the bamboo to remove the coarse shell and green bark (this step is called *shaqing*, “killing the green”). The inner fiber will then look like ramie.

Mix high-quality lime into a paste and coat the bamboo with it. Place it in a vat (*huangtong*) and boil for eight days and eight nights.

The boiling vat has a cauldron about four *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. Its rim is sealed with clay and lime, making it wide and tall, like the salt-boiling pans in Guangdong; it can hold over ten *dan* of water (roughly 1,000 liters). The vat is covered with a *huangtong* (a large tub-like cover), about 1.5 *zhang* (5 m) in circumference and over four *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. After covering, boil for eight full days. Let it rest for one day, then open the cover and take out the bamboo pulp. Rinse it in a clean pond lined on all sides and the bottom with wooden planks (to prevent mud contamination – not necessary for coarse paper).

After washing, mix the pulp with wood-ash slurry and put it back into a cauldron. Level the surface and spread about one *cun* (3 cm) of rice-straw ash on top. When the water in the vat boils, take the pulp out and put it into another vat, then pour the ash water over it again. If the water cools, reheat it and pour again. Repeat this for over ten days, until the material naturally rots and becomes soft.

Then take it out and beat it in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers, *shuidui*). Beat until it is as fine as mud, then pour it into a vat.

**The papermaking vat** (*chao zhi cao*) is rectangular. Its width depends on the mould (*lian*, a bamboo screen), and the mould in turn depends on the size of the paper sheet. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. Add a “paper medicine” (*zhi yao*) – a liquid made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves (local names vary). This makes the fibers separate and the paper turn white when dried.

The paper mould is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven into a screen, mounted on a rectangular frame. Hold the mould with both hands, dip it into the vat, and lift a layer of bamboo pulp onto the screen. The thickness depends on the operator’s skill: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the pulp floats on the screen, water drains through the gaps back into the vat. Then flip the mould onto a board to deposit the wet sheet. Stack thousands of sheets this way.

When the stack is thick enough, place a board on top, tie it with ropes, and insert a lever (like squeezing wine) to press out all the water. Then use fine copper tweezers to lift each sheet one by one and dry it on a heated wall.

**Drying the paper**: Build a narrow brick chamber with an open fire channel underneath. Several bricks are left out of the floor at intervals. Light a fire at one end; the heat passes through the gaps between the bricks, heating the entire brick wall. Paste the wet sheets onto the hot bricks to dry, then peel them off into stacks.

In recent times, wide sheets called *da si lian* (“Big Four-Link”) have been used for important writing.

Waste paper (with ink and vermilion washed off) can be soaked, rotted, and put back into the vat to make paper again, saving the boiling and soaking steps. The loss is small. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, people don’t bother; but in the north, every scrap of paper is collected and remade into “returned-soul paper” (*huanhun zhi*). The process is the same whether the material is bamboo or bark, fine or coarse.

**Fire paper** (*huo zhi*) and rough paper (*cao zhi*) follow the same steps – cutting bamboo, boiling pulp, ash-water treatment – but after removal from the mould, they are not baked. Instead, they are pressed to remove water and dried in the sun.

Paper made for this purpose is called *huo zhi*. In the Jing-Chu region (modern Hubei/Hunan), it is not unusual to burn over 500 kg at one funeral. About 70% of this paper is for funeral use; 30% for daily use.

The coarsest, thickest paper is called *baoguo zhi* (wrapping paper). It is made by mixing bamboo pulp with old rice straw from the late-season crop.

**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots grow, the best material is bamboo that is about to sprout branches and leaves, taken from deep mountain valleys. Around the Mangzhong solar term (early June), workers go into the mountains to cut the bamboo, chopping it into sections five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.7–2.3 meters) long. They dig a pond right there on the mountain and soak the bamboo in it. To prevent the pond from drying up, they use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous flow of water into it.

After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is beaten and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called *sha qing*, "killing the green"). The remaining bamboo fiber looks like ramie. This fiber is then coated with a paste made from high-quality lime dissolved in water, placed into a *huang* vat (a large cooking vessel), and boiled over a fire for eight days and eight nights.

The boiling pot is four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. Its rim is sealed with clay and lime, shaped wide and tall like the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong, and can hold over ten *dan* (approx. 600–1000 liters) of water. The *huang* vat covering it is 1.5 *zhang* (approx. 5 m) in circumference and over four *chi* in diameter. After eight days of boiling, the fire is stopped for one day. The vat is then opened, and the bamboo pulp (called *zhuma*) is taken out and washed clean in a clear-water pond. The pond's bottom and sides must be lined with tightly fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper).

After washing, the pulp is mixed with wood-ash lye and put back into a pot. It is leveled flat, and a one-*cun* (approx. 3.2 cm) layer of rice straw ash is spread on top. When the water in the pot boils, the liquid is ladled out and poured back over the pulp in another vat. If the liquid cools, it is reheated and poured again. This is repeated for over ten days, until the pulp naturally rots and becomes soft. It is then taken out and beaten in a mortar (mountain regions have water-powered trip-hammers) until it has the consistency of mud. This paste is then poured into a vat.

**The Papermaking Vat and Mould:** The vat for sheet-forming is square and half as deep as its width. The size of the vat depends on the mould, and the mould depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. A "paper medicine" (a plant extract, often from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name) is added. This makes the water drain away quickly, leaving clean, white paper.

**Forming the Sheets:** The papermaking mould is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo wires woven into a mat. It is stretched flat on a rectangular frame. The worker holds the mould with both hands and dips it into the vat, lifting up some bamboo pulp and spreading it evenly on the mould. The thickness of the sheet depends on the worker's technique: a light shake makes thin paper, a heavy shake makes thick paper. As the pulp floats on the mould, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The mould is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is high enough, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed using ropes and a lever (like pressing wine), to squeeze out all the water.

**Drying the Paper:** After pressing, each sheet is lifted with fine, light copper tweezers and dried on a wall. To build the drying wall, a narrow corridor is made with brick walls. The floor of the corridor is covered with bricks, with every few bricks leaving one missing as a vent. A fire is lit at one end; hot air and smoke travel through the vents, heating the bricks from inside. The wet sheets are pasted onto the hot outer brick surface to dry. When dry, they are peeled off and gathered into stacks.

In recent times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Lian" was highly valued for writing and official documents. Waste paper, after washing away ink and vermilion stains, can be soaked, rotted, and re-used in the vat. This saves all the previous cooking and soaking steps, and produces usable paper again with little material loss. In southern regions where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, even tiny scraps of paper found on the ground are collected and remade into "returned-soul paper" (*huan hun zhi*). This applies to both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse.

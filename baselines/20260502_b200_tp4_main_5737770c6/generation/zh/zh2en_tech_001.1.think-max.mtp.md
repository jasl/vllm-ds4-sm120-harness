All bamboo papermaking occurs in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots grow, one observes the depth of mountain valleys. To prevent the pit from drying out, they use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous flow of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, they pound and wash the bamboo to remove the outer shell and green bark (called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber looks like ramie. They dissolve high-quality lime in water to make a slurry, coat the bamboo with it, and cook it in a vat. The fire burns for eight days and eight nights.

To cook the bamboo, use a cauldron four feet in diameter. Build a rim around the cauldron using mud mixed with lime, making it tall and wide like the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong. This setup holds over ten *dan* (approximately 1,000 liters) of water. Place a large vat (15 feet in circumference, about four feet in diameter) on top as a lid. Seal it and cook for exactly eight days. Let it rest for one day, then open the vat and remove the bamboo pulp. Wash it in a clean soaking pit. The bottom and sides of this pit must be lined with tightly-fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, soak the pulp in wood-ash slurry, then put it back into the pot. Level the surface, and spread a layer of rice-straw ash about one inch thick on top. When the water in the vat boils, transfer the pulp to another vat and continue pouring the ash-water mixture over it. If the liquid cools, reheat it before pouring. Repeat this for over ten days, until the material naturally rots and becomes soft. Remove it and pound it in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers). Pound until it becomes like mud. Then pour it into a vat.

The papermaking vat has a rectangular frame on top, sized to match the screen (*lian*), which in turn matches the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water to about three inches above the pulp surface. Add a liquid made from a plant (like peach-bamboo leaves; local names vary). The papermaking screen is woven from extremely fine, polished bamboo strips. It is stretched over a frame with cross-supports.

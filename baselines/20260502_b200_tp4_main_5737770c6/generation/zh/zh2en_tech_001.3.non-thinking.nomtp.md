The bamboo is cut into lengths of five to seven chi (approx. 1.7–2.3 meters). On the mountain itself, a pit is dug, filled with water, and the bamboo is soaked in it. To prevent the pit water from drying up, bamboo pipes are used to channel a continuous stream of flowing water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo pulp then looks like ramie fiber.

A lime solution is made from high-quality lime and used to coat the pulp. This is placed into a *huang* tub (a large cooking vessel) and boiled. The fire is maintained for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron with a diameter of four chi (approx. 1.3 meters) is used. The rim of the cauldron is sealed with clay and lime, making it tall and wide, similar in shape to the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong, able to hold over ten *dan* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. The *huang* tub is placed on top; its circumference is 1.5 *zhang* (approx. 5 meters) and its diameter is over four chi (approx. 1.3 meters). After covering and boiling steadily for eight full days, the fire is stopped for one day. Then the *huang* tub is uncovered, and the cooked bamboo pulp (called "bamboo hemp") is taken out. It is washed clean in a clear-water soaking pit. The bottom and all four sides of this soaking pit are made of boards joined tightly together, to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is coated with plant ash slurry and then put back into a cauldron. It is leveled, and a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3.2 cm) thick is spread evenly on top. When the water in the tub comes to a rolling boil, the pulp is taken out and placed into another tub, where the ash-water liquid is poured over it again. If the liquid gets cold, it is reheated to a boil before being poured again. This is done for over ten days. The pulp will naturally rot and become foul-smelling. It is then taken out and put into a mortar for pounding (mountain regions all have water-powered trip-hammers). It is pounded until it has the consistency of mud, then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat (*chao zhi cao*) is square at the top. The dimensions of the vat depend on the size of the screen (*lian*), and the screen depends on the size of the paper. The bamboo pulp is put into the vat, and clear water covers its surface to a depth of about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm). A liquid made from "paper medicine" (a plant, possibly a type of hibiscus or similar; local names vary) is added. This causes the water to drain away, leaving the paper white. The papermaking screen is made from finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven together. When opened and spread, it has a supporting frame of crossbars underneath. Grasping the screen with both hands, it is dipped into the vat and lifted, causing the bamboo pulp to float onto the screen. The thickness of the paper depends on the skill of the maker: a gentle lift makes thin paper; a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the layer of bamboo fibers floats on the screen, water drains from all sides back into the vat. Then the screen is turned over, depositing the sheet of paper onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked up this way. When the stack is high enough, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever and rope, like pressing wine, to squeeze out all the moisture. Then, using fine copper tweezers, each sheet is lifted up one by one and dried over heat.

For drying the paper, a narrow passage is first built from earthen bricks. The floor of the passage is also made of bricks, with every few bricks leaving one missing. A fire is lit at one end; the hot air and flames pass through the gaps in the bricks, heating the entire brick wall. The wet sheets of paper are pasted one by one onto this hot brick wall to dry. Once dry, they are lifted off and gathered into quires.

In recent times, wide-format paper, called "Big Four-Lian," has been prized for writing and books.

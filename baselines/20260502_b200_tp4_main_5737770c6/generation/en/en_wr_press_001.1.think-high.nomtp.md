**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New Pollinator Path, a Hands-On Educational Trail for Habitat Conservation**

**HARBORLEAF, MN – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the launch of its newest public education initiative, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through practical, small-scale planting choices. The 0.8-mile accessible loop, located in the garden’s east meadow, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

Responding to growing visitor interest in actionable guidance for backyard and community gardening, the Pollinator Path transforms a scenic meadow loop into an interactive classroom. The trail features native plant demonstration beds that showcase species proven to attract and sustain pollinators native to the Upper Midwest, as well as a specialized night-blooming flower station highlighting plants that support nocturnal moths. Tactile signage along the route provides information in both English and braille, ensuring the experience is accessible to visitors with visual impairments. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, encourage children to document the insects and flowers they encounter.

“Our visitors have consistently asked for more practical, take-home knowledge about what they can plant to help pollinators,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “People want to move beyond general awareness. They want to know which specific flowers will feed a bumblebee in late summer, or how a small patch of milkweed in a city yard can connect to larger habitats. The Pollinator Path is our answer to that request.”

The trail is designed to be self-guided and family-friendly, with shaded resting points interspersed along the loop. In addition to the permanent features, the garden will host a volunteer-led weekend walk each Saturday and Sunday through the end of the growing season. Trained docents will lead small groups, offering deeper context on plant-pollinator relationships and answering individual questions. No registration is required for the weekend walks, which will begin at 10 a.m. at the east meadow trailhead.

The Pollinator Path emphasizes that even small, deliberate planting decisions can create a network of connected habitats across neighborhoods. The demonstration beds are planted exclusively with regionally appropriate, non-invasive native species, including bee balm, purple coneflower, butterfly weed, and evening primrose. Each bed is labeled with the plant’s common and scientific names, bloom period, and the specific pollinator species it supports.

“One of the most powerful messages we can share is that a single flowerpot on an apartment balcony or a strip of native shrubs along a sidewalk can be part of a larger pollinator corridor,” Ortiz added. “When neighbors coordinate even modest efforts—choosing a milkweed instead of an ornamental, or leaving a few fallen leaves for overwintering insects—the cumulative effect is profound. We hope the Pollinator Path inspires visitors to see their own outdoor spaces as extensions of this garden.”

The night-blooming flower station, a highlight of the trail, features plantings such as moonflower, evening stock, and night-blooming jasmine, paired with interpretive signage explaining how moths and other nocturnal pollinators navigate in darkness. The station is also lit with low-level, pollinator-safe LED lighting to allow evening viewing without disrupting insect behavior.

Youth observation cards, available free of charge, prompt children to record observations such as the color of a flower, the type of pollinator visiting it, and the time of day. Completed cards can be returned to the garden’s visitor center for a small, nature-themed sticker.

The east meadow loop itself is a previously underutilized section of the garden that has been renovated to meet accessibility standards. The trail surface is composed of compacted, fine-grained gravel suitable for wheelchairs and strollers. Rest areas are positioned at roughly quarter-mile intervals, each with a bench and interpretive panel.

The opening on July 12 will be marked by a brief introductory program at 9:30 a.m. at the trailhead, followed by the first official volunteer-led walk. The garden encourages early arrival, as parking is limited. The Pollinator Path is included with general garden admission, and no additional ticket is required.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a private, nonprofit organization dedicated to plant conservation, education, and community engagement. The garden is open year-round, with seasonal hours.

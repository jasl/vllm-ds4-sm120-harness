**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New “Pollinator Path” Educational Trail to Help Visitors Create Habitat Connections**

**PORTSMOUTH, NH – June 23, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its newest public education attraction, the “Pollinator Path,” on July 12, 2026, offering visitors an accessible, immersive experience designed to teach practical ways to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through thoughtful home gardening.

Located along the east meadow loop, the 0.8-mile trail is fully accessible and features a series of interactive learning stations intended to address a growing demand from Garden visitors for actionable guidance on planting for local pollinators. The trail includes native plant demonstration beds, a dedicated night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for all ages and abilities, youth observation cards, shaded resting points, and a volunteer-led weekend walk program.

“For several seasons, our visitors have consistently asked for more than just a list of recommended plants—they want to understand how small choices in their own yards can connect to a larger network of habitat,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that even a window box, a patch of milkweed, or a cluster of evening primrose can help create connected corridors across neighborhoods.”

The trail is designed to be self-guided, with interpretive signage placed at key intervals. The tactile signage, developed in consultation with accessibility specialists, allows visitors with visual impairments to feel representations of flower shapes, leaf textures, and pollinator body forms. Youth observation cards, available at the Garden entrance and along the trail, encourage children to record sightings of specific pollinators and compare notes with the Garden’s seasonal species lists.

One of the trail’s most distinctive features is the night-blooming flower station, positioned near a shaded resting area. This station highlights plants such as moonflower, evening primrose, and four-o’clocks, which open in the late afternoon or evening to attract moths and other nocturnal pollinators. Interpretive panels explain the role of scent, color, and timing in pollination ecology, and the station will be illuminated with low-intensity amber lighting during evening Garden hours to allow safe viewing without disrupting wildlife.

The native plant demonstration beds showcase regional species that support local pollinator populations at different times of the growing season. Each bed is labeled with the plant’s common and scientific names, bloom period, and the specific pollinators it attracts. The beds are arranged to illustrate principles of succession planting, ensuring nectar and pollen sources are available from early spring through late fall.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will begin on July 13, the first Saturday after the trail opens. These walks, limited to 15 participants per session, will be led by trained Garden volunteers who will provide context on plant-insect interactions, answer questions, and offer take-home tips for creating pollinator-friendly gardens of any size. Advance registration will be available through the Garden’s website.

The Pollinator Path was designed and installed entirely by Harborleaf Botanical Garden staff and volunteers, using plant materials grown in the Garden’s own nursery. The trail surface is compacted crushed stone suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility devices, and the 0.8-mile loop includes several benches and shaded pavilions for rest.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has long emphasized environmental education and community engagement. The Pollinator Path represents the organization’s latest effort to bridge the gap between public garden displays and everyday conservation action. According to Ortiz, the trail’s core message is that individual choices matter.

“We are not asking anyone to redesign their entire yard,” Ortiz said. “We are showing that a single pot of lavender on an apartment balcony, a corner of clover in a suburban lawn, or a patch of wild bergamot in a community garden can become a stepping stone in a larger habitat network. When neighbors make these choices together, the effect multiplies.”

The Garden expects the trail to serve as an ongoing resource for schools, community groups, and individual visitors seeking hands-on learning opportunities. The youth observation cards are designed to be reusable, and the Garden plans to collect data from returned cards to track the most frequently observed pollinator species along the trail.

The Pollinator Path opens to the public at 9 a.m. on July 12, 2026. Admission to the trail is included with general Garden entry. No reservations are required for self-guided visits.

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New “Pollinator Path” to Educate Public on Habitat Creation**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the official opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile public education trail designed to provide visitors with practical, hands-on guidance on supporting bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. Located along the east meadow loop, the accessible trail is now open to the public and features a series of interactive demonstration stations, tactile signage, and shaded resting points.

The Pollinator Path was developed in direct response to increasing visitor inquiries about how to make individual planting choices—from balcony pots to suburban yards—that collectively create essential habitat for local pollinators. The trail offers a sequential, self-guided experience that translates botanical science into actionable steps for home gardeners and community stewards.

“When visitors tell us they want to help pollinators but don’t know where to start, we hear a real need for practical, non-intimidating guidance,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. It shows that small planting decisions—whether it’s choosing one native milkweed plant or leaving a patch of bare soil for ground-nesting bees—can add up to connected habitats across neighborhoods.”

The trail begins at the east meadow loop entrance and winds through a series of carefully curated stations. Native plant demonstration beds feature locally adapted species selected for their nectar and larval host value, including California lilac, yarrow, and buckwheat varieties. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights species such as evening primrose and jasmine tobacco, which open at dusk to serve moths and other crepuscular pollinators. Each station is accompanied by tactile signage—with raised illustrations and braille text—ensuring accessibility for visitors with visual impairments.

Younger visitors can use newly designed youth observation cards, which prompt children to record pollinator sightings, draw flower shapes, and note color preferences. The cards are available free of charge at the trailhead. Shaded resting points, constructed from reclaimed wood and native stone, are spaced every 400 feet, providing quiet spots for observation or reflection. On weekends, trained volunteer naturalists lead guided walks (Saturday and Sunday mornings at 10:00 AM through October), offering informal Q&A and hands-on demonstrations.

The trail is entirely accessible, with a smooth, compacted surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility devices. Directional signs at key junctions provide clear wayfinding, and the entire loop is designed to be completed in 30 to 45 minutes at a relaxed pace.

The Pollinator Path is part of Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s broader commitment to environmental education. The garden has offered public programs for more than three decades, but the new trail represents a shift toward more actionable, visitor-driven learning. “People are eager to move beyond theory,” Ortiz added. “They want to know, ‘Should I plant this or that? Does it matter if I plant in a pot instead of the ground?’ The answer is yes, it matters, and we show them how.”

While the garden does not advocate for specific political or regulatory approaches, the Pollinator Path underscores a growing public interest in biodiversity and habitat connectivity at the grassroots level. “This isn’t about policy,” Ortiz said. “It’s about a person standing in their backyard and deciding to plant one more native flower. That single act, multiplied by thousands of neighbors, creates a corridor.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden does not disclose funding amounts for specific projects, and the Pollinator Path was developed using internal resources and existing garden staff expertise. No celebrity endorsers, political supporters, or external partners were involved in the trail’s creation.

The trail is open daily from 8:00 AM to dusk, free with garden admission. Weekend guided walks require no advance reservation and are included with admission. Youth observation cards are available at the front desk and the trailhead kiosk.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including a downloadable trail map and schedule of weekend walks, visit harborleafgarden.org/pollinator-path, or contact the garden’s communications office.

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New “Pollinator Path” to Educate Public on Habitat Creation**

**PORTLAND, OR – June 25, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through small, practical planting choices. The 0.8-mile accessible trail, located on the east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

Developed in direct response to visitor feedback requesting more actionable guidance on pollinator-friendly gardening, the Pollinator Path combines hands-on learning stations with permanent interpretive elements. The trail is designed to be fully accessible, with a smooth, compacted surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. It features shaded resting points at regular intervals, ensuring comfort for all visitors.

The path serves as a living classroom, showcasing native plant demonstration beds that highlight species naturally adapted to the local ecosystem. These beds are arranged to illustrate how different plant shapes, bloom times, and colors attract specific pollinators. A dedicated night-blooming flower station, for example, will feature plants such as evening primrose and moonflower, which open at dusk to accommodate nocturnal moths. Tactile signage, engraved with raised text and botanical illustrations, allows visually impaired visitors to engage with the content. In addition, the garden will distribute free youth observation cards at the trailhead, encouraging children to record the pollinators they spot along the route.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will begin the weekend following the opening, led by trained docents who can answer questions about plant selection, garden layout, and seasonal care. These walks are included with general garden admission and require no advance registration.

The inspiration for the Pollinator Path came from repeated inquiries from garden visitors. “Over the past two years, we’ve heard from countless guests who want to help pollinators but aren’t sure where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “They ask, ‘What should I plant? When should I plant it? Will one small garden actually make a difference?’ This trail answers those questions directly. We’re showing that even a single container of milkweed, a cluster of asters, or a patch of wild bergamot can become a vital resource. When many neighbors make these small planting decisions, the result is a connected habitat that stretches across entire communities.”

Ortiz emphasized that the trail is intended as an educational tool, not a political statement. “We are focused purely on ecology and horticulture. The message here is that anyone—whether they have a balcony, a backyard, or a shared community plot—can contribute to a healthier environment for these animals,” she added.

The east meadow loop, which previously served as a general walking path, has been transformed with interpretive stations and planting beds installed over the past four months. The trail begins near the garden’s main visitor center and winds through a restored meadow area. Each station features a specific theme: early-season bloomers for emerging bumblebee queens, mid-summer nectar sources for butterflies, late-season plants for migrating monarchs, and host plants for caterpillars. The night-blooming station is accompanied by low-level, pollinator-safe lighting to guide visitors after hours during special evening programs.

The Pollinator Path also includes a “Take-Home Guide” kiosk, where visitors can pick up free printed cards listing recommended native plants for each season, along with simple tips for reducing pesticide use and providing water sources. Digital versions will be available on the garden’s website.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, established in 1982, is a nonprofit organization dedicated to plant conservation, education, and community engagement. The garden maintains over 12 acres of themed collections, including a woodland garden, a rose garden, and a conservatory. The new Pollinator Path represents the garden’s first major trail project focused exclusively on wildlife gardening.

The official opening ceremony on July 12 will begin at 10:00 a.m. at the east meadow trailhead. The event is free with garden admission. Visitors are encouraged to bring water, sun protection, and comfortable walking shoes. The garden expects the trail to be a year-round attraction, with seasonal rotations of plantings and signage.

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden to Open “Pollinator Path” Education Trail on July 12**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – June 9, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will debut a new public education trail designed to teach visitors how to create habitat for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The “Pollinator Path” opens July 12 along the garden’s east meadow loop, a 0.8-mile accessible trail featuring native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, and interpretive signage designed for all ages.

The trail was developed in direct response to visitor requests. In recent years, Harborleaf staff have heard a recurring question from home gardeners and community groups: “What should I plant to actually help pollinators?” The Pollinator Path aims to answer that question with hands-on, visible examples that visitors can replicate in their own yards, school gardens, or neighborhood green spaces.

“People want to help, but they often don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf. “With this trail, they can see exactly which plants work for specific pollinators, how to arrange them, and why even a small planting patch matters. Those small decisions, when made across a neighborhood, can create a connected chain of habitats that sustains entire populations.”

The trail is fully accessible, with a smooth, packed-gravel surface suitable for wheelchairs and strollers. It winds through the east meadow loop, a shaded corridor of mature oaks and open grassland. Along the route, visitors will encounter:

- **Native plant demonstration beds**, each labeled with the pollinator species it supports—such as milkweed for monarch caterpillars, goldenrod for native bees, and salvia for hummingbirds.
- **A night-blooming flower station**, featuring moonflowers, evening primrose, and night-blooming jasmine, with interpretive signs explaining how moths and bats navigate after dark.
- **Tactile signage**, with raised text, braille, and embossed plant shapes, designed for visitors with visual impairments.
- **Youth observation cards**, available at a trailhead kiosk, that encourage children to track which pollinators they see at each station.
- **Shaded resting points**, with benches placed every quarter-mile under pergolas draped with pollinator-friendly vines.
- **Volunteer-led weekend walks**, offered every Saturday and Sunday morning through August, led by trained garden docents.

The trail is not a commercial exhibit or a political statement. Its sole focus is education—providing practical, science-based guidance on the ecological role of pollinators and the plants that support them. No funding partners, celebrity endorsers, or political supporters are associated with the project. Harborleaf is a private nonprofit operating solely on admissions, memberships, and individual donations.

Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is designed for all skill levels. “You don’t need to be a master gardener. You don’t need a big yard. You can start with a single pot of lavender on a balcony,” she said. “The trail shows you how.”

The opening coincides with peak bloom season for many of the meadow’s native wildflowers. Harborleaf expects the trail to remain active year-round, with volunteer-led walks continuing through October and winter programming focused on seed identification and dormant plant habitats.

The Pollinator Path will open to the public at 9:00 a.m. on July 12, 2026. Admission to Harborleaf Botanical Garden is included with standard entry. No special tickets or reservations are required for the trail, though weekend walk groups are limited to 20 participants and fill on a first-come basis.

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ to Educate Visitors on Habitat-Friendly Planting**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its newest public education trail, the “Pollinator Path,” on Sunday, July 12, 2026. Designed to address growing visitor interest in supporting local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds, the 0.8-mile accessible loop in the east meadow features interactive learning stations, native plant demonstration beds, and tactile signage aimed at gardeners of all skill levels.

The trail, which is fully wheelchair- and stroller-accessible, offers a self-guided experience that blends botanical beauty with practical instruction. Along the loop, visitors will encounter a night-blooming flower station—highlighting species that support nocturnal pollinators such as moths—alongside shaded resting points where guests can sit and observe insect activity. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, encourage children to record species they see, while volunteer-led weekend walks will begin on the first Saturday following the opening.

“Our visitors have consistently asked for more hands-on, practical guidance on how to plant for pollinators in their own yards and community spaces,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path was created to answer that call by showing, not just telling, what a habitat-friendly garden can look like. Small planting decisions—choosing milkweed over a non-native shrub, for instance—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods and help sustain these essential species.”

The trail’s design emphasizes regional native plants that provide nectar, host leaves, and nesting materials for a range of pollinators. Demonstration beds are clearly labeled with common names, scientific names, and blooming seasons, and tactile signage—featuring raised text and botanical illustrations—ensures accessibility for visitors with visual impairments. Stations include examples of plants attractive to specific pollinator groups: bright tubular flowers for hummingbirds, flat-topped blooms for butterflies, and fragrant, pale-colored blossoms for moths.

Harborleaf staff developed the Pollinator Path after noting a sharp increase in questions from garden visitors about how to reverse declines in local pollinator populations. Garden surveys conducted over the past two years showed that more than 60 percent of respondents wanted clearer examples of plants that serve as both nectar sources and larval host plants. The trail directly addresses these concerns without promoting specific political or regulatory positions, focusing solely on voluntary, educational gardening practices.

The night-blooming flower station, a highlight of the trail, includes moonflower, evening primrose, and night-blooming jessamine. Informational panels explain how these plants rely on scent and pale coloration to attract moths and bats. During the summer months, the garden plans to schedule occasional evening hours so that visitors can experience the station when it is most active.

The east meadow loop is located at the southern edge of the garden property, adjacent to the existing woodland trail network. All paths are surfaced with compacted gravel suitable for wheelchairs and strollers. Benches and shaded awnings built from sustainably sourced wood are placed at intervals along the route, offering rest stops with clear sightlines to the demonstration beds.

Youth observation cards, provided at no charge, guide children through activities such as identifying butterfly species, counting the number of bees on a single flower, and noting differences between daytime and nighttime bloomers. Cards can be returned to the garden’s visitor center for a small reward sticker, and completed cards are shared with the garden’s education team to help track informal observations.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will run every Saturday and Sunday at 10 a.m. and 1 p.m., beginning July 18, 2026. Each walk lasts approximately 45 minutes and covers the full loop, with guides offering tips on plant selection, garden layout, and pesticide-free pest management. No registration is required, though groups larger than ten are asked to contact the garden in advance.

Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is designed to be a year-round resource, not a seasonal exhibit. Many of the demonstration plants have been selected for multi-season interest—spring blooms, summer foliage, autumn seed heads, and winter structure—so that visitors can see how a pollinator-friendly garden evolves throughout the year.

“We hope that when visitors leave, they feel empowered to make even one small change in their own garden,” Ortiz added. “Whether it’s planting a patch of native aster or leaving a fallen log for ground-nesting bees, every action contributes to a larger patchwork of habitat. The Pollinator Path is a place to start that journey.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is located at 1000 Meadow Lane, Harborleaf, CA. The garden is open daily from 9 a.m. to sunset. Admission is free for members and children under 5; standard adult admission is $12. The Pollinator Path is included with general admission and requires no separate ticket.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including downloadable observation cards and a preview of the night-blooming flower station, visit www.harborleafgarden.org/pollinator-path or contact the garden’s media office.

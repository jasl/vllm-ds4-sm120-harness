**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New “Pollinator Path” to Teach Public About Native Habitats**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the official opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile accessible public education trail designed to help visitors understand how simple planting choices can support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The trail opens to the public on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. in the Garden’s east meadow loop.

Developed in response to frequent visitor requests for practical, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly spaces at home, the Pollinator Path is the Garden’s first dedicated habitat-education trail. It features a series of native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, youth observation cards, shaded resting points, and a volunteer-led weekend walk program that will begin the day of the opening.

“Our visitors have been asking for more than just a list of plants—they want to see how a garden can work as a real habitat,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “Small planting decisions, such as choosing a single native wildflower or leaving a patch of bare soil for ground-nesting bees, can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. The Pollinator Path is designed to show visitors exactly how to make those choices, step by step.”

The trail is fully accessible and navigable by wheelchair and stroller. It loops through the east meadow, a previously undeveloped section of the Garden that has been transformed into a living classroom. The path itself is surfaced with compacted decomposed granite to ensure stable footing, and all signage is positioned at multiple heights for ease of viewing. Resting points are spaced at regular intervals and shaded by existing oak canopy, offering visitors a place to sit, observe, and record their findings using the free youth observation cards available at the trailhead.

The native plant demonstration beds are the trail’s centerpiece. Each bed highlights a different pollinator group: one bed is planted with milkweed, goldenrod, and asters to attract monarch butterflies and native bees; another features trumpet vine, cardinal flower, and penstemon for hummingbirds; a third includes evening primrose, moonflower, and blazing star for night-flying moths. All plants are species native to the region, chosen for their proven value to local pollinators and their low water requirements.

Adjacent to the demonstration beds, the night-blooming flower station is a dedicated area where visitors can observe plants that open only after dusk. The station will be illuminated with low-level, amber-colored lighting designed not to disturb nocturnal pollinators. Garden staff will offer scheduled evening viewing sessions beginning in late July, timed to coincide with peak bloom periods.

Tactile signage is installed at each stop along the trail. The signs feature raised text and embossed illustrations of key plants and pollinators, allowing visitors who are blind or have low vision to explore the habitat through touch. Audio descriptions will also be available via a free mobile guide that visitors can access by scanning a QR code at the trailhead.

Youth observation cards, designed for children ages 5 to 12, provide guided prompts for noting which pollinators visit each bed, what colors and shapes they prefer, and what time of day they appear. The cards are free and can be collected at the Garden’s welcome center or at the trailhead kiosk. Garden educators will also lead a weekly drop-in program on Saturday mornings, starting July 12, where families can use the cards together and share their observations.

“We want the Pollinator Path to be a place where people of all ages and abilities can slow down, look closely, and learn,” Ortiz said. “The goal is not just to display beautiful plants, but to show how each of us—even with a small balcony or a front yard patch—can take part in rebuilding the network of food and shelter that pollinators depend on.”

The Pollinator Path is a permanent addition to Harborleaf Botanical Garden. There is no additional admission fee beyond standard Garden entry. Weekend walks, led by trained Garden volunteers, will be offered free of charge from 10:00 a.m. to 11:30 a.m. every Saturday through October. The walks will cover a different theme each week, including plant identification, pollinator life cycles, and seasonal garden care.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a nonprofit public garden dedicated to the conservation and display of native plants. The Garden is open daily from 8:00 a.m. to sunset. For more information about the Pollinator Path, including accessibility details, scheduled events, and the free mobile guide, visitors are encouraged to contact the Garden directly.

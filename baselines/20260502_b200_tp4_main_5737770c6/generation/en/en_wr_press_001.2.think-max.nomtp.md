**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New Pollinator Path Education Trail**

**HARBORLEAF, MD – June 5, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated 0.8-mile public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through native planting and habitat design. The trail, located along the garden’s east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

The self-guided, fully accessible trail is the result of increasing visitor demand for practical, hands-on guidance about pollinator conservation. Over the past two years, garden staff noted a sharp rise in questions from guests seeking specific advice on which plants to choose, how to arrange them, and what small changes in home landscaping could make a difference for local pollinator populations.

“People come to us wanting to help, but they often feel overwhelmed or unsure where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path answers that need directly. It shows that small, intentional planting decisions in a backyard, a balcony container, or a community plot can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. You don’t need a large property to make a meaningful impact.”

The trail features a series of native plant demonstration beds, each highlighting species that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host support for specific pollinator groups. Plants were selected based on regional research and consultation with local entomologists and horticulturists. Visitors will see milkweed for monarch butterflies, wild bergamot for bees, and columbine for hummingbirds, among many others.

A notable stop along the path is the night-blooming flower station, designed to attract moths and other nocturnal pollinators. This section is planted with evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jasmine, and includes interpretive signage explaining the ecological role of pollinators that work after dark.

Throughout the trail, tactile signage provides information in raised text and simple diagrams, making the educational content accessible to visitors with visual impairments or varying reading levels. Each sign includes descriptions of the plants, the pollinators they support, and easy-to-follow tips for replicating similar plantings at home.

Youth observation cards are available at the trailhead and at each station. These laminated cards guide children and families through simple activities such as counting the number of bee species spotted, identifying leaf shapes, and recording the time of day certain flowers open. The cards encourage repeat visits and help young visitors develop observation skills while engaging directly with the garden environment.

Shaded resting points are spaced at regular intervals along the 0.8-mile loop, offering benches under shade trees and small gazebos. These areas allow visitors to pause, reflect, and observe pollinator activity without disturbing the plant beds. Garden staff designed the trail to be fully navigable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids, with a compacted gravel surface and gentle grades throughout.

Beginning the weekend after the official opening, Harborleaf Botanical Garden will offer volunteer-led weekend walks along the Pollinator Path. These guided walks, led by trained garden educators and volunteer docents, will provide deeper context about plant ecology, seasonal changes, and identification techniques. Walks are free with garden admission and will run on Saturday and Sunday mornings through the end of the growing season.

The garden will mark the trail’s launch with a public opening event on July 12 at 10:00 a.m. at the east meadow loop trailhead. The event will include brief remarks from staff, a ribbon cutting, and guided preview walks. No special registration is required for the opening day event, though regular garden admission fees apply.

The Pollinator Path is a permanent addition to Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s educational offerings, and its content will be updated seasonally to reflect bloom cycles and pollinator activity. Foundation support for the trail’s development came from sources focused on environmental education, though funding amounts and partner organizations are not being disclosed at this time.

Ortiz emphasized that the project remains strictly educational in focus. “This is about giving people tools and knowledge,” she said. “We are not advocating for any policy or position. We are simply showing what is possible when you plant with purpose. A butterfly doesn’t need a grand plan—it needs a place to land. The Pollinator Path helps people provide that place.”

For more information about the trail, including a downloadable map and the full schedule of volunteer-led weekend walks, visit the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website or contact the garden’s visitor services office.

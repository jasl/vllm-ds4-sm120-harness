**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New Pollinator Path Educational Trail**

**HARBORLEAF, MA – June 10, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its newest public education initiative, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated nature trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through practical, small-scale planting choices. The trail officially opens to the public on July 12, 2026, along the garden’s east meadow loop.

The 0.8-mile accessible trail will feature a series of immersive, hands-on learning stations aimed at bridging the gap between casual observation and actionable home gardening. Responding to increasing requests from visitors for guidance on creating pollinator-friendly spaces, the garden has transformed a portion of its east meadow into a living classroom. The path is fully wheelchair accessible and includes paved surfaces, gentle grades, and shaded resting points to accommodate visitors of all ages and abilities.

“Over the past two seasons, our guest surveys consistently showed that people leave inspired but unsure how to replicate what they see here in their own backyards or community plots,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path was designed to answer that specific need. Our core message is that small planting decisions—choosing one native aster over a non-native variety, or leaving a patch of bare ground for ground-nesting bees—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. Every footprint of garden matters.”

Key features of the trail include native plant demonstration beds, which display a curated selection of regional wildflowers, grasses, and shrubs known to support local pollinator life cycles. Each bed is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom periods, and the specific pollinators it attracts. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights plants that open after dusk, tailored to support nocturnal moths and bats. The station will be lit with low-intensity, amber-colored lights that minimize disruption to wildlife while allowing evening visitors to observe blooms such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-scented stock.

To enhance accessibility for visitors with visual impairments, tactile signage is installed at key stops along the route. These signs combine raised illustrations of pollinators, plant textures, and braille descriptions, allowing guests to experience the trail through touch. Youth observation cards, available at the garden’s welcome kiosk, encourage younger visitors to document species they spot, track bloom changes, and record behavioral notes. The cards include simple prompts such as “Which flower did the moth visit first?” and “Count how many different bee species you see in five minutes.”

The shaded resting points, constructed from sustainably sourced wood and native stone, are placed at intervals of approximately every quarter-mile. Each rest area includes interpretive panels that explain the ecological role of the adjacent plants and provide tips for incorporating similar elements into home landscapes. The garden will also offer a volunteer-led weekend walk program, beginning on July 12 and continuing every Saturday and Sunday through October. Trained docents will lead small groups, answering questions and offering demonstrations on seed collection, soil preparation, and water source placement.

The east meadow loop was chosen for the Pollinator Path in part because of its existing diversity of native flora and its proximity to an established monarch butterfly waystation. Garden staff have worked with local horticulturists and entomologists to select plant species that provide sequential bloom throughout the growing season, ensuring a continuous supply of nectar and pollen from early spring through late autumn. Species include milkweed, goldenrod, purple coneflower, wild bergamot, and butterfly weed, among others.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a private, nonprofit organization dedicated to the conservation of native plants and the education of the public about sustainable horticulture. The garden’s mission focuses on science-based, nonpolitical approaches to environmental stewardship, emphasizing personal action and community engagement over advocacy. The Pollinator Path is its first dedicated trail specifically designed around pollinator education.

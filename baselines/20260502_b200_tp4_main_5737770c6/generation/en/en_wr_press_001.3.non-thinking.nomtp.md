**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” to Teach Visitors How to Build Backyard Habitat**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – June 20, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public education trail designed to give visitors practical, hands-on knowledge about planting for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The trail will open on **July 12, 2026**, offering a free, accessible, self-guided experience for gardeners, families, and nature enthusiasts.

The 0.8-mile loop, located within the garden’s east meadow, is fully accessible and features a series of demonstration areas intended to show how small, deliberate plant choices can support local pollinator populations. Garden staff developed the trail in response to recurring questions from visitors seeking clear, actionable guidance on what to plant and how to arrange their home landscapes to benefit pollinators.

“Our visitors have consistently asked for more practical, step-by-step advice about choosing plants that genuinely help bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds survive and thrive,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “With the Pollinator Path, we are translating research into real-world examples. A single window box or a small patch of native perennials may seem modest, but when many households make these same small decisions, they create connected habitats that stretch across entire neighborhoods.”

The trail’s design emphasizes education through direct observation. Among its key features are:

- **Native plant demonstration beds** planted with regionally appropriate species known to serve as host plants and nectar sources for local pollinators.
- **A night-blooming flower station** highlighting plants that open in the evening to attract moths and other nocturnal species, complete with timed interpretive lighting.
- **Tactile signage** with raised text and plant-material samples, allowing visitors with visual impairments to feel leaf textures, seed heads, and bark.
- **Youth observation cards** that guide children and school groups through identifying pollinator species, recording visitation times, and understanding flower shapes.
- **Shaded resting points** placed at intervals along the loop, offering benches and interpretive panels that explain how microclimates affect pollinator activity.
- **Volunteer-led weekend walks** beginning the first Saturday after opening, led by trained Garden docents who will answer questions and demonstrate plant selection and garden layout strategies.

The east meadow loop was selected for its existing mix of open grassland, partial shade, and seasonal wildflowers, providing a realistic backdrop for the demonstration beds. The Garden’s horticulture team worked over the past year to install the plantings and signage, ensuring that each station models conditions that homeowners can replicate in typical yard, balcony, or community garden spaces.

The Pollinator Path is designed to be a self-guided experience with no additional cost beyond regular Garden admission. The trail circles back to the main visitor center, allowing guests to purchase starter plants, seeds, and reference materials from the Garden’s nursery. An accompanying digital guide will also be available via the Garden’s mobile site, providing audio descriptions and seasonal bloom calendars.

Ortiz emphasized that the trail is meant to be an educational resource, not a political statement. “We are not advocating for any specific policy or legislative agenda,” she said. “Our mission is to share science-backed information about the role of native plants in supporting biodiversity. Whether a visitor has a large suburban yard, a small urban patio, or a school garden, we want them to leave with one new idea they can try tomorrow.”

The Garden plans to host a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at 10 a.m. on July 12 at the trailhead near the east meadow entrance. The event is open to the public and will include demonstrations of the youth observation cards and tactile signage. No speeches by elected officials or celebrity appearances are scheduled.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a nonprofit organization dedicated to plant conservation, education, and community engagement. The Garden maintains more than 200 acres of themed gardens, natural areas, and research collections and welcomes over 300,000 visitors annually.

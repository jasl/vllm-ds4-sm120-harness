**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils “Pollinator Path” Trail to Inspire Community-Based Habitat Conservation**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – June 10, 2026** – The Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on July 12, 2026, offering visitors a dedicated, accessible route designed to demonstrate how individual planting choices can support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile trail, located on the garden’s east meadow loop, will feature native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage, youth observation cards, and shaded resting points, along with a volunteer-led weekend walk program.

The trail’s development responds to a growing number of visitor inquiries seeking practical, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly spaces. Garden staff observed that many guests leave the main gardens inspired but unsure how to translate botanical beauty into their own yards, balconies, or community plots. The Pollinator Path directly addresses that gap by providing clear, hands-on learning opportunities at every stop.

“We heard repeatedly from visitors that they want to help pollinators, but they don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “This trail shows that small planting decisions—choosing a native milkweed over an ornamental, or leaving a patch of bare soil for ground-nesting bees—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. It’s not about grand gestures; it’s about informed, consistent choices.”

The trail begins at the east meadow loop entrance, where a welcome kiosk introduces the concept of pollinator corridors. From there, visitors will encounter a series of themed stations. The native plant demonstration beds feature locally adapted species such as California fuchsia, yarrow, and goldenrod, each labeled with information about which pollinators they attract and their bloom cycles. A dedicated “night-blooming flower station” highlights plants like evening primrose and yucca that support moths and bats after dusk.

Accessibility was a central design consideration. The entire 0.8-mile loop uses compacted, stable surfacing suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers. Tactile signage incorporating raised text, braille, and embossed illustrations allows visitors with visual impairments to identify plants and learn about their pollinator relationships. Benches and shaded resting points are spaced at regular intervals, and the path maintains a gentle grade throughout.

Youth engagement is a priority. At the start of the trail, children and families can pick up a set of laminated “Youth Observation Cards” that guide them through simple activities: counting the number of different bee species at a flower patch, drawing a hummingbird’s feeding pattern, or identifying a night-blooming flower’s scent. Completed cards can be returned at the garden’s visitor center for a small plantable seed packet.

Starting July 12, a volunteer-led weekend walk program will operate every Saturday and Sunday morning from 10 a.m. to 11:30 a.m. Trained docents will lead groups along the path, offering deeper explanations of plant-pollinator relationships, habitat requirements, and seasonal changes. The walks are free with garden admission and do not require advance registration.

The Pollinator Path is the result of a year-long planning process that included input from garden horticulturists, educators, and accessibility consultants. It aligns with the garden’s broader mission to provide environmental education without political advocacy. The trail does not address pesticide policy, land-use regulation, or agricultural practices; instead, it focuses solely on botanical and ecological information that individuals can apply at home.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, founded in 1972, maintains 35 acres of curated landscapes, including a woodland garden, desert collection, and seasonal bloom displays. It hosts approximately 150,000 visitors annually and offers year-round educational programs for all ages. The east meadow loop previously served as a seasonal wildflower viewing area; the new trail repurposes and enhances that space with permanent interpretive features.

The Pollinator Path officially opens on July 12, 2026, with a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at 9:30 a.m. at the east meadow loop entrance. The garden opens to the public at 10 a.m. that day. Admission prices remain unchanged: $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under 5. Garden members enter free year-round.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including a detailed trail map, seasonal bloom calendars, and volunteer opportunities for the weekend walk program, visit the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website or contact the communications office.

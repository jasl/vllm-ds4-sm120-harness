**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” to Teach Public How to Build Backyard Habitats**

**PORT HARBOR, CA – July 2, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will launch its newest public education trail, the “Pollinator Path,” on July 12, 2026, offering visitors a hands-on, accessible guide to supporting local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through native plant choices. The trail opens at 9:00 a.m. on the east meadow loop, a 0.8-mile accessible route designed to accommodate visitors of all ages and mobility levels.

The Pollinator Path responds directly to a growing demand from Harborleaf’s 180,000 annual visitors: practical, actionable guidance on what to plant and how to maintain small-scale habitats. Garden staff developed the trail over 18 months after reviewing visitor comment cards and survey responses that consistently asked for “more help turning my yard into a pollinator-friendly space.”

“People come to our garden to see beautiful flowers, but they often leave asking, ‘What can I actually do at home?’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. It shows that even a few intentional planting decisions, like swapping a non-native shrub for a native aster or leaving a patch of bare ground for ground-nesting bees, can create connected habitats across an entire neighborhood.”

The trail’s centerpiece is a series of native plant demonstration beds arranged by ecological function. Each bed features species native to the coastal region, including California buckwheat, goldenrod, purple needlegrass, and showy milkweed. Interpretive signs explain which pollinators rely on each plant and what time of year they bloom. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights plants such as evening primrose and yucca, whose pale blossoms and strong scents attract moths and bats after dark.

Tactile signage—embossed text and raised botanical illustrations—ensures the trail is fully accessible to visitors with visual impairments. Youth observation cards, available free at the trailhead, prompt children to search for specific pollinators, record observations, and draw the flowers they see. Shaded resting points with benches and low tables are spaced every quarter-mile, allowing families and older adults to pause, sketch, or read.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will begin the weekend of July 12, with trained docents leading small groups along the path every Saturday and Sunday at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. through October. No registration is required; groups will gather at the east meadow entrance.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has prioritized low-maintenance, drought-tolerant plantings for the demonstration beds, using only species that thrive with minimal supplemental water once established. The organization’s horticulture team selected plants that offer overlapping bloom times from early spring through late fall, ensuring a continuous nectar and pollen supply.

“We hear from visitors who want to help but feel overwhelmed by all the information online,” Ortiz added. “They do not need to turn their entire property into a meadow. A container of lavender on an apartment balcony, a window box of annual phlox, a small patch of clover instead of a perfect lawn—each choice becomes a steppingstone. When enough neighbors make those small choices, you get a corridor of habitat.”

The garden plans to use the path as a living classroom for school field trips, community workshops, and a planned “pollinator pledge” program in which participants commit to adding at least one native plant per season.

The east meadow loop’s existing features—a small wooden bridge over a seasonal stream and views of the surrounding coastal hills—remain intact, with the Pollinator Path installations complementing rather than replacing the natural landscape.

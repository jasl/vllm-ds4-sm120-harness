**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” to Educate Public on Habitat-Friendly Planting**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public education trail designed to help visitors understand how small-scale planting choices can support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile accessible trail will open to the public on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. in the garden’s east meadow loop.

The Pollinator Path addresses a growing demand from Harborleaf visitors for practical, hands-on guidance on creating habitat for native pollinators. According to garden staff, frequent questions from guests have centered on which plants to choose, how to arrange them, and what simple changes in yard care can make a difference. The new trail aims to answer those questions directly through a series of interactive and educational features.

“The most common thing we hear from visitors is, ‘I want to help the bees and butterflies, but I don’t know where to start,’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that small planting decisions—whether on a balcony, a suburban lot, or a community plot—can add up to create connected habitats across neighborhoods. Our goal is to make that knowledge accessible and actionable for everyone.”

The trail is fully accessible by wheelchair and stroller, winding through the east meadow loop with a level, compacted surface. Along the route, visitors will encounter several dedicated stations designed to demonstrate key pollinator-supporting principles.

Native plant demonstration beds anchor the experience, featuring species that have co-evolved with local pollinators. The beds are grouped by function: early-season bloomers for emerging bees, high-nectar flowers for migrating butterflies, and host plants for moth and butterfly larvae. Labels at each bed specify which pollinators benefit from each plant, and the installation dates are staggered to show how a continuous bloom sequence can support insects from spring through fall.

A night-blooming flower station introduces the lesser-known world of nocturnal pollination. Planted with evening primrose, moonflower, and other species that open after dusk, the station includes subtle lighting designed to reveal blossoms without disrupting local wildlife. Information panels explain how moths and bats rely on these plants, and how visitors can incorporate night-blooming species into their own landscapes.

Touch is a central component of the learning experience. Tactile signage with raised text, plant texture panels, and embossed illustrations of pollinators allow visually impaired visitors to explore the same content as sighted guests. Braille descriptions accompany each sign, and audio guides are available at the trailhead for those who prefer listening.

Youth observation cards, available free at the garden entrance, turn the trail into a self-guided field activity. Each card includes prompts to identify flower shapes, count visiting insects, and note the time of day different pollinators appear. The cards are designed for elementary and middle school-aged visitors, with a stamp station at the trail’s end where completed cards earn a small Pollinator Path badge.

For those who want a guided experience, volunteer-led weekend walks will begin on July 13 and continue every Saturday and Sunday through October. Harborleaf garden volunteers have completed a training program on pollinator biology and interpretation, and each walk lasts approximately 45 minutes. Walks are free with garden admission, and groups of 10 or more are encouraged to register in advance.

Shaded resting points are spaced at intervals along the trail, with benches set beneath existing oak and maple trees. Each rest point features a short interpretive panel on a nearby topic, such as the role of fallen leaves as insect overwintering habitat or the importance of leaving dead stems for stem-nesting bees. These panels reinforce the message that even low-maintenance practices can support pollinator life cycles.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has developed the Pollinator Path in response to visitor feedback collected over the past two years. The garden’s education team surveyed guests during regular programming and noted a consistent request for more detailed, practical guidance on pollinator-friendly gardening. The trail’s launch date in midsummer coincides with peak bloom in the meadow and high pollinator activity, allowing visitors to see the principles in action.

“The Pollinator Path is not a one-time exhibit,” Ortiz added. “It will evolve as the plants mature and as we gather more input from the community. We intend it to be a living classroom that changes with the seasons and with what our visitors tell us they need.”

The trail is located off the main garden path in the east meadow, accessible from the Harborleaf Visitor Center. Garden admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students ages 13–17, and free for children 12 and under and Harborleaf members. The Pollinator Path is included with general admission. No additional ticket is required.

For more information, including a downloadable trail map and the weekend walk schedule, visit Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s website or stop by the Visitor Center.

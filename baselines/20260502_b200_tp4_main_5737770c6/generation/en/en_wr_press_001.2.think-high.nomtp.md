**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New Pollinator Path to Inspire Community-Wide Habitat Connection**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its newest public amenity, the “Pollinator Path,” an educational trail designed to demonstrate how small-scale planting choices can support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile accessible loop, located in the garden’s east meadow, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

Developed in response to frequent visitor inquiries about practical, at-home strategies for supporting pollinator species, the Pollinator Path combines native plant demonstration beds, interpretive signage, and interactive learning tools along a fully accessible route. The trail offers shaded resting points and features a dedicated night-blooming flower station, highlighting plants that support nocturnal pollinators such as moths. Tactile signage, designed for visitors with visual impairments, and youth observation cards intended for school groups and families will further enhance the educational experience.

“We consistently hear from visitors who want to help pollinators but aren’t sure where to begin,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that even small planting decisions—choosing one native species, adding a night-bloomer, or leaving a patch of bare ground—can create connected habitats that stretch across yards, parks, and neighborhoods.”

The trail’s east meadow location was selected for its existing diversity of wildflowers and its ability to accommodate the garden’s signature demonstration beds without disturbing sensitive native plant communities. The loop is paved and meets Americans with Disabilities Act accessibility standards, ensuring that all visitors can navigate the route comfortably.

According to Ortiz, the programming for the Path extends beyond the physical trail. Starting on opening weekend and continuing each Saturday through October, volunteer-led weekend walks will guide visitors through the loop, explaining the ecological role of each plant species and offering tips for home planting. The youth observation cards, which are laminated for repeated use, prompt children to record pollinator sightings and identify flower shapes, colors, and bloom times. Educators and group leaders may request free card sets through the garden’s education office.

The night-blooming flower station, a feature that garden staff say is unique among regional botanical gardens, showcases species such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jasmine. Interpretive panels explain the adaptations that allow these flowers to attract moths and other nocturnal visitors, and a low-light viewing area allows safe observation after dusk. The garden plans to extend evening hours for one weekend each month during peak blooming season.

Native plant demonstration beds along the trail include milkweed for monarch butterflies, purple coneflower for bees, trumpet honeysuckle for hummingbirds, and butterfly weed for a variety of pollinators. Each bed is labeled with the plant’s common and scientific names, bloom period, preferred soil type, and sunlight requirements, providing visitors with a take-home reference for their own gardens.

The Pollinator Path is the latest addition to Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s public education initiatives, which also include seasonal workshops, a seed library, and a school field trip program. The garden’s staff emphasize that the project is entirely nonpartisan and focused on ecological education.

“The goal is not to prescribe a specific gardening style, but to show the science behind plant-pollinator relationships in a way that people can apply to any yard, balcony, or community space,” Ortiz said. “When one household plants a native milkweed, and the neighbor plants lavender, and the next block has a patch of clover, pollinators can move freely and find the resources they need.”

The garden will host a soft opening for members and donors on July 11, with the general public welcome starting July 12. Admission to the Pollinator Path is included with regular garden entry. The garden is open daily from 9 a.m. to 6 p.m., with extended evening hours during scheduled night-bloom viewing dates.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is located at 2400 Arbor Lane, Harborleaf, CA. For more information about the Pollinator Path, including program schedules, youth card availability, and volunteer opportunities, contact the Garden’s Education Office at (555) 019-8347 or education@harborleafgarden.org.

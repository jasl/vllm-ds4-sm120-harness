**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ Education Trail to Inspire Community-Wide Habitat Creation**

**HARBORLEAF, NJ – June 25, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the July 12 opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile public education trail designed to teach visitors how small, intentional planting choices can support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. Located on the garden’s east meadow loop, the trail offers an accessible, interactive experience for all ages, featuring native plant demonstration beds, tactile signage, and a dedicated night-blooming flower station.

The trail addresses a recurring request from Harborleaf visitors who have sought practical, actionable guidance on supporting pollinators in their own yards and neighborhoods. According to garden officials, guests commonly ask which plants are most beneficial and how to arrange them. The Pollinator Path translates that curiosity into a living classroom.

“We hear from visitors who want to help but feel overwhelmed by where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that even small planting decisions—a cluster of milkweed here, a few late-blooming asters there—can, when connected across a neighborhood, create a network of vital habitats. We hope people leave the trail not just inspired but equipped to act.”

The trail’s design prioritizes accessibility and sensory learning. Paved and graded to meet ADA standards, the path includes shaded resting points at intervals, allowing visitors to pause and observe. At stops along the route, tactile signage provides raised graphics and braille descriptions of featured plants and their pollinators, making the experience inclusive for visually impaired guests.

Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, guide younger visitors through activities such as identifying flower shapes, counting insect visits, and sketching leaf patterns. Garden staff designed the cards to encourage repeat visits and seasonal comparisons, reinforcing the concept that a pollinator-friendly garden evolves throughout the year.

A key highlight is the night-blooming flower station, where plants such as evening primrose and moonflower open at dusk to attract moths. The garden plans to offer evening viewing hours during peak bloom periods, with signage explaining the role of night-flying pollinators in the ecosystem.

The trail also integrates demonstration beds showcasing regionally appropriate native plants. Each bed is labeled with the target pollinator it serves: butterfly milkweed for monarch caterpillars, beebalm for bees, and trumpet honeysuckle for hummingbirds. Garden educators will refresh the plantings seasonally to demonstrate a succession of blooms from early spring through late fall.

To reinforce learning, Harborleaf will launch a volunteer-led weekend walk series beginning July 13. Trained docents will lead small groups along the Pollinator Path, offering on-site interpretation and answering questions. The walks are free with garden admission and require no advance registration.

The Pollinator Path is the latest addition to Harborleaf’s broader education strategy, which aims to bridge the gap between formal horticultural knowledge and everyday home gardening. The garden’s leadership emphasized that the project is nonpolitical and focused solely on ecological literacy.

“We are not taking sides in broader debates,” Ortiz said. “Our mission is simple: show people what works in the ground, and let the plants and pollinators speak for themselves. A hummingbird doesn’t care about politics—it just needs nectar.”

Community response has been positive in early previews. Harborleaf members who tested the trail noted the clear, visual demonstrations of companion planting and the calming design of the path. One member commented that seeing a patch of wild bergamot alive with bumblebees made the concept of “connected habitats” tangible in a way a pamphlet never could.

The garden expects the Pollinator Path to become a flagship educational destination, attracting school groups, master gardener classes, and casual visitors alike. The east meadow loop, previously underutilized, now offers a focused experience that dovetails with the garden’s seasonal flower displays and greenhouse collections.

“We invite everyone to come walk the path, touch the plants, and see the pollinators in action,” Ortiz said. “And then—if they plant just one milkweed in their front yard, or hang a hummingbird feeder in a window box—they’ll be part of something larger than themselves.”

**Opening Day Details:**
The Pollinator Path officially opens on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. Regular garden admission applies. No ceremonial events are planned; the trail will simply be open for self-guided exploration.

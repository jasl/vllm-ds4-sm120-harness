**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” to Educate Public on Habitat-Friendly Planting**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the launch of its new “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through simple, intentional planting choices. The 0.8-mile accessible trail, located along the east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

The Pollinator Path was developed in direct response to a growing number of visitor inquiries about practical, science-based guidance for creating pollinator-friendly spaces at home. Garden staff noted that many guests left the main gardens inspired but unsure how to translate what they saw into their own yards, balconies, or community plots.

“Visitors frequently tell us they want to help pollinators, but they don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path turns that curiosity into clear, actionable information. Our goal is to show that even small planting decisions—choosing one native shrub over a non-native one, or adding a night-blooming flower near a porch light—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods.”

The trail is fully accessible and designed for visitors of all ages and physical abilities. It features a series of native plant demonstration beds that highlight species specifically selected to support local pollinators throughout the year. Interpretive signs along the route identify each plant by common and scientific names, bloom season, and which pollinator species it serves. A dedicated night-blooming flower station, illuminated in the evenings, showcases plants that attract nocturnal moths and bats, offering a rare opportunity for evening visitors to observe these often-overlooked creatures.

Tactile signage is installed at key stops along the path, providing text in braille and raised illustrations of flowers, insects, and hummingbirds. This feature ensures that visitors with visual impairments can engage with the educational content through touch. At the start of the trail, a small kiosk offers free youth observation cards—laminated, reusable cards that prompt children to identify pollinator species, note flower colors, and sketch what they see. Garden staff expect the cards to be particularly popular with school groups and family visitors.

For those seeking a more guided experience, the garden will host volunteer-led weekend walks beginning the Saturday after the opening. These free, drop-in walks will be led by trained docents who can answer questions, demonstrate plant identification techniques, and discuss the ecological role of each species featured along the path. No registration is required, and walks will accommodate groups of up to 20 participants per session.

The trail surface is composed of compacted, permeable gravel suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. Shaded resting points, including benches placed beneath existing oak and sycamore trees, are spaced at regular intervals to allow visitors to pause and observe without strain. Water stations are located at the trailhead and at the halfway point.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s commitment to nonpolitical, science-based education is central to the Pollinator Path’s design. The trail avoids advocacy or policy positions, focusing instead on observable ecological relationships and practical horticulture. Interpretive content was reviewed by the garden’s horticulture and education teams, as well as by a volunteer entomologist and a certified master gardener.

Maya Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is intended to bridge the gap between public enthusiasm and everyday action. “We often hear, ‘I want to help the bees, but I don’t have a lot of space,’ ” Ortiz said. “This trail shows that a pot on a balcony, a strip of lawn converted to wildflowers, or a single night-blooming plant in a garden bed can make a measurable difference. When many people make those small choices, the result is a network of stepping-stone habitats that support pollinators across the entire region.”

The opening on July 12 will begin with brief remarks from garden staff at 10:00 a.m. at the trailhead, followed by self-guided exploration. Volunteer-led walks will commence the following day, July 13, and will continue each Saturday and Sunday through the end of the growing season.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is open daily from 8:00 a.m. to sunset. Admission to the Pollinator Path is included with general garden entry. Garden members and children under 12 enter free of charge.

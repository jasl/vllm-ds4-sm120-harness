**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Debuts “Pollinator Path” Educational Trail to Inspire Community-Wide Habitat Connections**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – June 2, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through accessible, science-based gardening practices. The trail will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026, along the east meadow loop.

The Pollinator Path transforms a section of the garden’s existing east meadow into an immersive, hands-on learning experience. Developed in direct response to frequent visitor inquiries about practical steps for attracting and sustaining pollinators, the trail features native plant demonstration beds, a dedicated night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, youth observation cards, and shaded resting points. The garden will also launch a volunteer-led weekend walk program to accompany the trail.

The initiative aligns with Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s broader mission to connect people with the natural world through accessible education. According to garden staff, the past two years have seen a marked increase in questions from home gardeners, school groups, and community members seeking clear, actionable guidance on planting for local pollinators.

“Our visitors have told us repeatedly that they want to know exactly which plants to put in the ground and how to arrange them to make a real difference for pollinators,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path gives them a living classroom. Small planting decisions—choosing a single native milkweed over an ornamental shrub, or leaving a patch of leaf litter for moths—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods, linking backyards, schoolyards, and public spaces into a network of refuge.”

The trail’s design emphasizes inclusivity and experiential learning. Tactile signage along the route features raised illustrations and braille descriptions of key plant species and pollinator life cycles, allowing visitors with low vision to engage fully with the content. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead and at the garden’s welcome center, guide children and families through scavenger-hunt-style tasks that encourage close observation of flower shapes, insect behavior, and plant-animal interactions. The cards include prompts such as “Find a flower that is open only at night” and “Count how many bees visit a purple coneflower in two minutes.”

Shaded resting points, constructed from sustainably harvested wood and positioned at intervals along the loop, offer seating and interpretive panels that explain the role of pollinators in food production, native plant ecology, and local biodiversity. The night-blooming flower station, a highlight of the trail, features species such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jasmine, accompanied by signage that explains the adaptations of nocturnal pollinators, including moths and bats.

The east meadow loop, chosen for its gentle terrain and existing pollinator habitat, is fully accessible via paved and compacted surfaces, ensuring that visitors using wheelchairs, strollers, or mobility aids can navigate the entire route. The garden’s volunteer-led weekend walks, scheduled to begin on July 13, will be led by trained docents who will provide deeper context on plant selection, seasonal bloom cycles, and habitat design.

The development of the Pollinator Path was guided by input from Harborleaf’s horticulture and education teams, as well as feedback collected through visitor surveys and community listening sessions. The garden has emphasized that the trail is a noncommercial, educational resource and does not involve external partners, celebrity endorsers, or political affiliations. Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a privately supported nonprofit organization.

“This trail reflects what our community has been asking for: practical, joyful, and evidence-based ways to help pollinators thrive where they live,” Ortiz added. “We hope every person who walks the path leaves with a seed of an idea—literally and figuratively—that they can plant in their own backyard.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden will host a public opening event on July 12 from 9 a.m. to 4 p.m., with docent-led previews of the Pollinator Path offered at 10 a.m., 12 p.m., and 2 p.m. Regular garden admission applies. The volunteer-led weekend walks will commence the following day, July 13, and will continue every Saturday and Sunday through October 31, 2026, weather permitting.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, the opening event, or the weekend walk program, members of the media are invited to contact the garden’s communications office.

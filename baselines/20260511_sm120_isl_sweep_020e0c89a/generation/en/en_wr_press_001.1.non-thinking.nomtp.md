**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New “Pollinator Path” Education Trail**
*Interactive, accessible trail aims to connect visitors with practical habitat gardening for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds.*

**HARBORLEAF, PA – June 15, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden announced today the opening of its newest public education initiative, the “Pollinator Path,” an immersive 0.8-mile accessible trail designed to teach visitors how to support local pollinator species through simple, actionable planting choices. The trail, located on the east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

Developed in direct response to increasing visitor inquiries about supporting wildlife in home gardens, the Pollinator Path features a series of native plant demonstration beds, a dedicated night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, youth observation cards, shaded resting points, and a volunteer-led weekend walk program. The trail is fully wheelchair and stroller accessible, with a packed-earth and boardwalk surface that meets current accessibility standards.

“We’ve heard consistently from our visitors that they want to help bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds, but they aren’t always sure where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is designed to bridge that gap. It shows that small, thoughtful planting decisions on a single property can, when repeated across neighborhoods, create a connected network of habitats. You don’t need a large garden to make a difference.”

The trail’s demonstration beds showcase plant species native to the region that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host material for key pollinators. Plants such as milkweed, bee balm, purple coneflower, and goldenrod are arranged in clusters that replicate how they might appear in a typical home garden or community green space. Each bed is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom season, and the specific pollinators most likely to visit.

A highlight of the new trail is the night-blooming flower station, which opens near dusk. This area features evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jessamine, which attract moths and other nocturnal pollinators. Interpretive panels explain the role of scent, white or pale coloration, and large flower shapes in guiding night-flying species to food sources.

Tactile signage along the route includes raised text, botanical illustrations, and braille descriptions, allowing visitors with vision loss to explore the trail independently. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, encourage children to document pollinators they see, note flower shapes and colors, and record weather conditions—turning a simple walk into a citizen science activity.

Shaded resting points equipped with benches and small water stations are positioned at regular intervals, providing respite for families, seniors, and visitors with mobility challenges. These rest stops also host informational panels that summarize each section of the trail.

The park’s volunteer-led weekend walk program will launch the same day. Trained garden docents will lead groups through the trail every Saturday and Sunday at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. through the end of October, with seasonal adjustments thereafter. The walks are free with garden admission and require no advance registration.

Ortiz emphasized that the trail is explicitly educational and nonpolitical in its approach. “Our focus is purely on the biology and ecology of pollinators and the plants that support them,” she said. “We want people to leave feeling empowered, not overwhelmed. Whether you have a balcony container or a quarter-acre yard, there’s something here for you.”

The Pollinator Path joins Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s existing network of themed gardens and educational facilities, including the native woodland trail, the sensory garden, and the children’s discovery plaza. The garden is open daily from 8:00 a.m. to sunset, with extended evening hours during the summer solstice season to accommodate the night-blooming station.

A ribbon-cutting ceremony will be held at the trailhead on the east meadow loop on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. The event is open to the public. Media representatives are encouraged to attend and to schedule advance interviews with garden staff.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including trail maps, plant lists, and volunteer walk schedules, visit the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website or contact the media relations office.

**Paper from Bamboo (Zào Zhú Zhǐ)**
This process is carried out in the south, especially in Fujian province. After bamboo shoots grow, select bamboo from deep mountain valleys where branches and leaves are about to appear—this is the best material. Around the *Mangzhong* (Grain in Ear) solar term (early June), cut the bamboo on the mountain into sections 5–7 *chi* (roughly 1.7–2.3 m) long. Dig a pit on the mountain, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo. To prevent the pit from drying out, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over 100 days, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (called *sha qing*, "killing the green"). The inner fiber looks like ramie. Mix high-quality lime into a slurry, coat the bamboo with it, then place it in a *huang tong* (a large cooking vessel) and boil for exactly eight days and eight nights.

The boiling vessel has a diameter of 4 *chi* (about 1.3 m). Its rim is sealed with a mixture of mud and lime, similar in size and shape to a salt-boiling pan used in Guangdong, and can hold over 10 *dan* (approx. 500–600 L) of water. The *huang tong* cover is 15 *chi* (5 m) in circumference and 4 *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. After sealing, boil for eight full days. Let it rest for one day, then open the cover and remove the bamboo pulp. Wash it in a clean soaking tank whose bottom and sides are lined with tightly fitted planks to prevent mud contamination (not necessary for coarse paper). After washing, treat the pulp with wood-ash lye, then put it back into a cauldron. Level the pulp and spread a layer of rice straw ash about 1 *cun* (3 cm) thick on top. When the water in the cauldron boils, transfer the pulp to another vessel and pour the ash lye over it again. If the liquid cools, reheat it and repeat. After about ten days of this, the bamboo fibers will naturally rot and soften. Then remove and pound them in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers) until the pulp becomes like mud. Pour it into a vat.

**The paper-making vat** is rectangular, its width matching the size of the screen (*lian*), which in turn matches the desired paper size. The bamboo pulp is placed in the vat, covered by about 3 *cun* (10 cm) of clean water. Add "paper medicine" (a plant juice, often from leaves resembling peach or bamboo—no fixed name) so that the water drains cleanly, leaving white paper. The screen is made from finely scraped bamboo strips woven into a mat, stretched over a removable frame. Holding the screen by both hands, dip it into the vat and lift a layer of pulp onto it. Paper thickness depends on skill: a light lift gives thin paper; a heavy lift gives thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains back into the vat from all sides. Then turn the screen over onto a board to deposit the sheet. Stack thousands of sheets. When the stack is tall enough, press it with a board and tighten ropes with a lever (like pressing wine) to squeeze out all water. Then use fine copper tweezers to lift each sheet and dry it.

**Drying:** Build a drying chamber with brick walls in parallel, leaving a gap. Cover the floor with bricks, leaving one brick missing every few bricks. Light a fire in the first opening; hot air flows through the gaps between bricks, heating the outer wall. Paste each wet sheet onto the hot wall to dry, then remove and stack into quires.

In recent times, wide sheets called "Big Four-Link" have been prized for writing and documents. Waste paper—after washing off red ink or dirt—can be soaked, rotted, and reused in the vat, saving all the earlier cooking and soaking steps. It still makes usable paper with little loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. In the north, even scraps of paper are picked up and recycled into "soul-returning paper" (*huan hun zhi*). Bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, all follow similar methods.

For **fire paper** and **rough paper**, the bamboo is cut, cooked, treated with ash lye, and washed the same way, but after removing from the screen, they are not dried on a hot wall. Instead, they are pressed to remove water and then sun-dried.

During the Tang dynasty, because of many rituals for ghosts and gods, paper money (paper burned to replace silk cloth) became common. In the north, cut strips were called *ban qian* (board money). This type is called fire paper. In the Jing-Chu region (modern Hubei/Hunan), some burn as much as 1,000 *jin* (600 kg) in a single ceremony. About 70% of fire paper is used for burning to spirits; 30% for daily use. The coarsest, thickest kind is called wrapping paper, made by mixing bamboo pulp with leftover rice straw from old fields.

For **note paper** made in Yanshan and other counties, the pulp is entirely fine bamboo material, formed thickly on the screen to fetch a high price. The best is called *guan jian* (official note paper), used by wealthy families for name cards. It is thick and free of fiber clumps. For red auspicious paper, the sheets are first coated with alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

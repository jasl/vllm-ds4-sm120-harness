All bamboo paper is made in the south, but Fujian province specializes in it and produces the most. When the bamboo shoots have grown, inspect the remote mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to sprout leaves is the best material. Around the time of the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), go into the mountains to cut it down. Cut the stems into lengths of five to seven *chi* (about 1.7–2.3 meters). On the mountain itself, dig a pond, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo in it. To prevent the pond from drying out, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over one hundred days, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The remaining bamboo fiber looks like raw hemp. Make a paste from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the bamboo fibers with it, put them into a wooden vat, and cook them over a fire for exactly eight days and eight nights.

For cooking the bamboo, use a pot about four *chi* (1.3 meters) in diameter. Seal the rim of the pot with a mixture of mud and lime, making it tall and wide, similar to the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong. The pot can hold over ten *shi* (roughly 600+ liters) of water. Cover the pot with a large wooden vat (楻桶) that is one *zhang* five *chi* (about 5 meters) in circumference and four *chi* (1.3 meters) in diameter. After sealing it, cook for the full eight days. Then, let the fire go out for one day, open the vat, and take out the cooked bamboo pulp. Wash it clean in a clean soaking pond. The floor and all four sides of this pond must be lined with tongue-and-groove wooden planks to prevent mud from getting in (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, coat the pulp with wood-ash slurry and put it back into a pot, spreading it flat. On top, spread an even layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (3.3 cm) thick. When the water in the pot boils, remove the pulp and place it into another vat, then continue to pour the ash-water mixture over it. If the mixture gets cold, re-boil it and pour it again. Repeat this process for about ten days, until the pulp naturally rots and becomes soft. Then, take the pulp out and put it into a mortar for pounding (in mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers are used). Pound it until it has the consistency of mud, then pour it into a vat (the papermaking vat).

The papermaking vat (抄紙槽) is a rectangular container. Its width depends on the size of the paper mold (簾), and the size of the mold depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. Add a plant-based sizing liquid (called "paper medicine water") to the vat. This liquid comes from a plant that looks like peach or bamboo leaves (its local name varies). It helps the water drain away, leaving the paper white and clean. The paper mold (抄紙簾) is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven together. It is held in a rectangular wooden frame that supports it when open or rolled up. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts up the bamboo pulp so it spreads evenly over the mold. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by the worker's technique: a gentle shake makes a thin sheet, a stronger shake makes a thick one. While the bamboo fibers float on the mold, water drains out through the mesh on all sides back into the vat. Then, the mold is turned over to deposit the wet sheet onto a board. This is repeated, stacking thousands of sheets on top of each other. Once the stack reaches the desired height, a board is placed on top, a bar is tied to it with ropes, and it is pressed like a wine press to squeeze out all the remaining water. After pressing, each sheet is lifted individually with light, fine copper tweezers and dried over a fire.

For drying paper, first build a drying wall: a narrow passageway between two walls made of earth bricks. The floor of this passage is also paved with bricks, but with gaps left every few bricks. A fire is lit and fed from a hole at one end. The hot air and flames pass through the gaps between the bricks, heating the entire brick wall. Wet sheets of paper are pasted onto the hot brick wall one by one to dry. When dry, they are peeled off and gathered into bundles.

In recent times, a wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" (大四連) became valued for writing and painting. Waste paper is cleaned of any red ink or dirt, soaked until it breaks down, added to the vat, and remade into paper. This process saves all the work of the original cooking and soaking, and it still produces usable paper with little loss of material. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, any scrap of paper, even a small piece, is picked up and remade. This recycled paper is called "returned-soul paper" (還魂紙). The same method applies to both bamboo and bark papers, fine or coarse.

As for spirit paper (火紙) and rough paper (糙紙), the steps of cutting bamboo, cooking the fibers, and using ash-lime water washing are all the same as above. However, after the sheet is removed from the mold, it is not dried on a hot wall. Instead, it is pressed to remove water and then sun-dried.

In the modern custom of the Jing-Chu region (Hunan/Hubei area), it is not uncommon to burn up to a thousand *jin* (about 600 kg) in a single funeral ceremony.

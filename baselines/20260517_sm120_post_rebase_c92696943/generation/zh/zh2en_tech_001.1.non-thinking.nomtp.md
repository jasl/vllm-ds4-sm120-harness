**Making Bamboo Paper**
(from *Tiangong Kaiwu*)

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots appear, choose bamboos growing in mountain valleys—those about to sprout branches and leaves are best. Around the Mangzhong solar term (early June), go into the mountains to cut them. Chop them into lengths of 5–7 *chi* (about 1.7–2.3 m). Dig a pit near where they were cut, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo in it. To keep the pit from drying out, use bamboo pipes to bring a steady flow of water. Soak for over 100 days, then pound and wash off the outer shell and green skin (this step is called *shaqing*, "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber looks like ramie. Mix slaked lime into a paste, coat the fibers with it, and put them into a wooden vat (called a *hongtong*) for boiling. Heat for eight days and eight nights.

The boiling pot is about 4 *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. Its rim is sealed with clay and lime, similar in shape and size to the large salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong. It can hold over ten *dan* (about 600 liters) of water. On top sits the vat, about 15 *chi* (5 m) in circumference and over 4 *chi* in diameter. After eight days of boiling, let it rest for one day. Then open the vat, take out the bamboo pulp (called *zhuma*), and wash it in a clean soaking pit. The pit's bottom and sides must be lined with tightly fitted planks to prevent mud from getting in (not necessary for coarse paper). After washing, add wood-ash slurry to the pulp and put it back into the pot. Spread a layer of rice-straw ash about 1 *cun* (3.3 cm) thick over the surface. When the water in the pot boils, transfer the pulp to another vat, and pour the ash-water over it again. If the water cools, reheat and pour again. Repeat for over ten days until the pulp rots and becomes soft. Then take it out and pound it in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip hammers) until it becomes like mud. Pour it into a vat.

**The Papermaking Vat**
The vat is about half a square *zhang* (~5.6 m²) in area. Its size depends on the paper mold (*lian*), which in turn depends on the paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clean water to about 3 *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. Add a "paper drug" liquid (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves; the name varies locally). This makes the water drain away and leaves white paper.

**The Paper Mold**
The mold is made of very fine, smooth bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is mounted on a rectangular frame that can be rolled up or stretched flat. Hold the mold with both hands and dip it into the vat, lifting the pulp onto the screen. Thickness depends on the maker's skill—a gentle shake gives thin paper; a heavy shake gives thick paper. Water drains through the screen and back into the vat. Then flip the mold over to transfer the wet sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. When the stack is tall enough, press it with a board and tighten ropes around it (like pressing wine) to squeeze out water. After drying, lift each sheet carefully with fine copper tweezers and dry it over heat.

**Drying Paper**
Build a narrow corridor with brick walls. Cover the floor with bricks, leaving one brick missing every few tiles. Light a fire at one end; heat passes through the gaps and warms the bricks. Paste wet sheets onto the warm brick walls to dry, then remove them as finished sheets.

**Modern Wide Paper**
Recently, wide paper called "Big Four-Link" is valued for writing and documents. Waste paper (with ink or red seals washed off) can be soaked, rotted, and remade into new paper, saving the steps of soaking and boiling. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not common. In the north, even small scraps are collected and remade into "soul-returning paper." Bamboo and bark fibers, fine or coarse, are treated the same way.

**Fire Paper and Rough Paper**
For fire paper (cheap paper used for burning at funerals) and rough paper, the process is the same: cut bamboo, boil pulp, add ash-water, and wash. But after lifting from the mold, they skip drying over heat; instead, press out water and sun-dry.

**History**
In the Tang dynasty, paper money replaced silk for burning in religious rites (northerners use cut strips called *banqian*). This paper is called "fire paper." In Hubei and Hunan, some families burn up to a thousand *jin* (~600 kg) at once. About 70% of this paper is used for funerary burning, 30% for daily use. The coarsest, thickest kind is "wrapping paper," made from bamboo pulp mixed with old rice straw. Letter paper from Yanshan (in Jiangxi) uses fine bamboo pulp, thickly formed, and sells for high prices. The best is "official letter paper," thick and smooth, used by the wealthy for name cards. For auspicious red paper, the sheets are first treated with alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking originates in the south, with Fujian province being particularly renowned. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one examines the deep mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow branches and leaves is the best raw material. Around the time of the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), workers go into the mountains to cut it down. The bamboo is cut into pieces five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.7–2.3 meters) long. Right there on the mountain, a pit is dug and filled with water to soak the bamboo. To prevent the pit water from drying out, bamboo pipes (aqueducts) are used to channel a continuous stream of waterfall into it.

After soaking for over 100 days, the bamboo is beaten and washed to remove the coarse outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green" / *shaqing*). What remains is the bamboo fiber, which looks like ramie. A lime slurry is made from high-quality lime and mixed into the fibers. This mixture is then placed into an *huang* bucket (a large, cauldron-like vessel) and boiled over a fire continuously for eight days and eight nights.

The boiling pot is four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter. Its rim is built up with clay and lime mortar, forming a deep, wide basin similar to salt-boiling pans in Guangdong, capable of holding over ten *shi* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. The *huang* bucket, which is placed over this pot, has a circumference of 15 *chi* (approx. 5 meters) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters). After sealing it in place and boiling for the full eight days, the fire is stopped for one day. Then the bucket is opened, and the bamboo pulp (*zhuma*) is taken out and washed clean in a clear-water washing tank. The bottom and all four sides of this tank are lined with tightly fitted planks to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is sized with a wood-ash slurry and then put back into a pot. The pulp surface is leveled, and a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick is spread on top. When the water in the pot boils, the hot, alkaline liquid is ladled out and poured back over the pulp in another vessel. If the liquid cools, it is boiled again before being poured. This process is repeated for over ten days, until the fibers naturally rot and become soft. The pulp is then taken out and put into a mortar for beating (mountain regions all use water-powered trip-hammers / *shuidui*). It is beaten until it has the consistency of mud, then poured into a vat.

The paper-making vat is square and shallow. Its size is determined by the mold (*lian*), and the mold's size is determined by the desired paper size. After the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. A "paper medicine" (*zhiyao*) liquid is added (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves; local names vary). This liquid helps the fibers disperse and the water to drain, resulting in white paper once dry. The paper mold is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a mat. It is supported by a rectangular frame when used. Holding the mold by its frame, the worker dips it into the vat and lifts it, catching a layer of bamboo fibers on the mat. The thickness of the paper is controlled by the worker's technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the fibers float on the mold, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The mold is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet of paper onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. Once the stack reaches a certain height, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever and rope, similar to pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water. The damp paper sheets are then individually lifted with lightweight copper tweezers and dried over heat.

For drying, a double-walled chamber is built from ordinary bricks. The floor of the chamber has gaps left by removing every few bricks. A fire is lit at one end; the hot air and flames pass through the gaps, heating the entire brick wall. The wet paper sheets are pasted onto the hot brick wall one by one to dry, then removed as finished sheets.

In recent times, a wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" (*da silian*) became valued for calligraphy and books. Waste paper can have its ink and vermilion washed off, then be soaked until rotted and put back into the vat to remake paper, saving the entire initial boiling and soaking process. The paper loss is minimal. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not commonly done. In the north, any scrap of paper, even an inch long, is picked up and remade; this is called "returned-soul paper" (*huanhun zhi*). The principle is the same for both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse.

As for fire paper (*huozhi*, for burning at funerals) and rough paper (*caozhi*), the process of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and treating it with lime and wood-ash water is the same. The only difference is that after the sheets are lifted from the mold, they are not dried on a hot wall; instead, they are pressed to remove water and then sun-dried.

During the Tang Dynasty, ghost and spirit worship was prevalent. Paper money replaced silk as offerings (in the north, cut strips were used, called "board money" / *banqian*). So, this type of paper was called fire paper. A common custom in the Jing-Chu region is to burn as much as a thousand *jin* (approx. 600 kg) at once. 70% of this paper is for offerings to the dead, and 30% for daily use. The coarsest and thickest kind is called wrapping paper (*baoguozhi*), made from bamboo pulp mixed with stalks from last year's late rice.

Regarding the letter paper (*jianzhi*) made in Yanshan and other counties: it is entirely made from fine bamboo material formed into a heavy, thick sheet, aiming for a high price. The finest quality is called "official note paper" (*guanjian*). Wealthy families use it for name cards. It is thick and strong, without fibers or knots. To make red "lucky note" paper, the sheets are first treated with an alum solution, then dyed with safflower juice.

Bark paper is made from nanmu (*Phoebe*), camphor, or mulberry bark. The process includes a key difference: after the first boiling with lime and the beating step, the coarse outer bark of the white bark (sandarac) must be scraped off. The finest papers, such as "Kaihua paper" and "Kongqing paper," are made this way.

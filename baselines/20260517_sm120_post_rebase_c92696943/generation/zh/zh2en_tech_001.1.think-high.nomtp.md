**Making Bamboo Paper** (from *Tiangong Kaiwu*)

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, and Fujian province is especially renowned for it. After bamboo shoots have emerged, examine the depth of the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow branches and leaves is the best raw material. Around the time of Grain in Ear (芒种, early June), go into the mountains to cut the bamboo, saw it into pieces five to seven *chi* (about 1.7–2.3 m) long, and on that same mountain dig a pond, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo in it. Fearing the pond water might dry up, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over 100 days, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the coarse shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green" [杀青]). The remaining bamboo fiber looks like ramie. Make a paste from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the bamboo with it, place it in a large vat (楻桶), and cook it over a fire for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling bamboo, use a cauldron four *chi* (about 1.3 m) in diameter. Build a rim around the cauldron with clay and lime, similar in height and width to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, so it can hold more than ten *shi* (about 600 L) of water. Cover it with the large vat (楻桶), which has a circumference of fifteen *chi* (about 5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi* (about 1.3 m). Once covered and boiling, eight days are sufficient. Let the fire rest for one day, then remove the vat and take out the cooked bamboo pulp (called "bamboo hemp" [竹麻]). Wash it in a clean soaking pond. The bottom and sides of this pond should be lined with wooden planks joined tightly to prevent mud contamination (for making coarse paper, this lining is unnecessary). After washing, soak the fiber in wood-ash slurry, then put it back into the cauldron, level the surface, and spread a layer of rice-straw ash about one *cun* (3.3 cm) thick on top. When the water in the cauldron boils, ladle the hot ash-water over the fiber, transferring it to another vat. Keep pouring the ash-water (reheat it if it cools). Repeat this for ten or more days, until the fiber naturally rots and becomes soft. Then take it out and pound it in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers [水碓]) until it has the consistency of mud. Pour this into a vat.

The papermaking vat (抄纸槽) is rectangular, with dimensions matching the mold (帘), which in turn matches the desired paper size. When the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clean water until it stands about three *cun* (10 cm) above the fiber. Add a "paper medicine" liquid (纸药水汁) made from a plant (resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name). This additive makes the water drain away leaving clean white sheets. The paper mold (抄纸帘) is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven together. When opened and spread, it rests on a rectangular frame. Hold the mold with both hands, dip it into the vat, and lift it so that bamboo fiber floats onto the mold. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by the operator's technique: a gentle lift gives thin paper, a heavy lift gives thick paper. As the fiber is lifted, water drains from the sides back into the vat. Then turn the mold over and deposit the wet sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. When the stack reaches the desired height, place a board on top, tighten a rope around it with a lever (like pressing wine), and press out as much water as possible. After draining, use fine copper tweezers to pick up each sheet one by one and dry it over heat.

For drying paper, first build a long narrow chamber with brick walls. Cover the floor of the chamber with bricks, leaving gaps every few bricks. Light a fire at one end of the chamber; the hot air and flames pass through the brick gaps, heating the outer wall bricks. While the bricks are hot, paste each wet sheet onto the wall to dry. When dry, peel off and gather into bundles.

In recent times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" (大四连) has been prized for writing. Waste paper – after washing off red ink and dirt, soaking until rotten, and re-entering the vat – can be remade into paper, saving all the previous steps of cooking and soaking, with little loss. In southern regions where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, even scraps of paper are picked up and remade into so-called "returned-soul paper" (还魂纸). Bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, are all made the same way. As for "fire paper" (火纸) and coarse paper (糙纸), the steps of cutting bamboo, boiling the fiber, and applying ash slurry are the same as above; only after removing from the mold, instead of drying over heat, they are pressed to remove water and sun-dried.

During the High Tang period, ceremonies for gods and spirits became numerous, and paper money replaced burned silk (in the north they use cut strips called "board money" [板钱]), so this paper is called "fire paper." In the region of Jing-Chu (modern Hubei/Hunan), it is common to burn up to a thousand *jin* (about 600 kg) in one ceremony. About 70% of this paper is for sacrificial burning, 30% for daily use. The coarsest and thickest kind, called "wrapping paper" (包裹纸), is made by mixing bamboo pulp with old rice straw from the previous year's late crop. As for the letter paper (柬纸) made in Qianshan and other counties, it is entirely made from fine bamboo material, formed thick, and sold at high prices. The finest grade is called "official letter paper" (官柬). Wealthy families use it for visiting cards. It is thick and free of fiber lumps. To make red auspicious letter paper, first treat the sheet with alum water, then apply safflower juice.

**Making Bamboo Paper**

The making of bamboo paper comes from the south, and Fujian province is particularly famous for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one examines the depths of the mountain valleys. The best material is bamboo that is just about to sprout leaves. Harvesting happens around the Grain in Ear solar term (early June). Workers go into the mountains to cut the bamboo, chopping it into lengths of five to seven *chi* (roughly 1.7–2.3 meters). On the mountain itself, they dig a pit and fill it with water to soak the bamboo. To prevent the pit from drying up, they use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it.

After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called *sha qing*, "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber, which looks like ramie, remains. This fiber is coated with a paste made from high-quality lime dissolved in water, then placed in a *huang* bucket (a large cooking vessel) and boiled over a fire for eight days and eight nights.

The boiling pot is about four *chi* (1.3 meters) in diameter. Its rim is built up with mud and lime to form a high edge, similar in shape and size to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, and it can hold over ten *dan* (about 600 liters) of water. The *huang* bucket, which is placed on top, has a circumference of fifteen *chi* (5 meters) and a diameter of over four *chi* (1.3 meters). After eight days of boiling, the fire is stopped for one day. The bucket is then opened, and the bamboo pulp (now called "bamboo hemp") is taken out. It is washed clean in a clear-water soaking pit. The bottom and all four sides of this pit are lined with tightly-fitted wooden planks to prevent mud from contaminating the pulp (this is not needed for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is mixed with a wood-ash paste and put back into a pot. It is leveled, and a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (3 cm) thick is spread on top. When the water in the pot boils, the liquid (now an alkaline lye) is ladled out and poured over the pulp again, which has been moved to a different bucket. If the liquid cools, it is re-boiled before pouring. This process is repeated for over ten days, until the pulp naturally rots and becomes soft. It is then taken out and beaten in a mortar (in mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers are used). The beating continues until the pulp has the consistency of mud. It is then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat is rectangular in its upper half. Its width is determined by the paper mold (*lian*), and the mold's size is determined by the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to a depth of about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. A "paper medicine" liquid (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with various local names) is added. This causes the water to drain away cleanly, leaving white paper.

The paper mold (*lian*) is made of very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven together. It is supported by a frame stretched over vertical and horizontal bars. The worker holds the mold with both hands and dips it into the vat, lifting the bamboo pulp up and onto the mold's surface. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by the worker's technique: a gentle lift makes a thin sheet, a heavy lift makes a thick one. As the bamboo fibers float on the mold, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The mold is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is complete, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever rope system (like pressing wine) to squeeze out all the water. After pressing, each sheet is lifted individually with light, fine copper tweezers and then dried.

To dry the paper, a chamber is first built with brick walls, forming an inner flue. The floor of the flue is made of bricks with gaps left open to allow heat to rise. A fire is lit in a firebox at one end; the heat travels through the flue and heats the outer brick wall. Wet sheets are pasted onto this hot wall to dry. Once dry, they are removed and gathered into quires. In recent times, a wide format called *Da Si Lian* (Large Four-fold) was valued for writing and documents.

Waste paper (with ink and vermilion washed off) can be soaked, rotted, and added back to the vat to be made into new paper. This saves all the previous steps of boiling and soaking. The loss is small. In southern regions where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, even small scraps are collected and remade into so-called "Returned Soul Paper." This applies to both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse.

As for "fire paper" and coarse paper, the steps of cutting the bamboo, boiling the fiber, and treating it with ash lye and water are the same. But after being lifted from the mold, these are not dried on a hot wall. Instead, they are simply pressed to remove water and then sun-dried.

During the High Tang period (8th century), offerings to spirits and gods were common. Paper money replaced burned silk cloth (in the north, cut strips called "board money" were used). The paper for this purpose is called "fire paper." In the modern custom of the Chu region (Hubei/Hunan), a single funeral might burn up to 500 kg of it. 70% of this fire paper is used for burning as offerings; 30% is used for daily purposes. The coarsest and thickest kind, called "wrapping paper," is made by mixing bamboo pulp with old rice straw from the late-season crop.

As for the fine letter paper (*Jian zhi*) made in Yanshan County and other places, it is made entirely from fine bamboo material, formed into a thick sheet on the mold, and sold at a high price. The best grade is called "Official Letter Paper," used for name cards by wealthy and noble families. It is thick and strong, without any fibers showing. To make red auspicious letter paper, the sheets are first treated with alum water and then dyed with safflower juice.

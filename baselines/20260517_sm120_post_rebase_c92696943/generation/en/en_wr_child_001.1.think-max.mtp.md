Pip tumbled free. She spread her wings and fluttered down safely onto the grass beside Sylvester.

"Thank you," she whispered. "I was so scared."

"You are safe now," Sylvester smiled. "And look."

The sun was higher now. It was drinking the water. The perfect puddle by the fairy steps was half the size it had been. The Mirror of the Morning was almost gone.

"Where did the water go?" Pip asked.

"The sun is sipping it, drop by drop," Sylvester said. "Everything changes. A map helps me remember what was, so I can understand what is. Patience lets me see the changes that rushing would miss."

Pip watched the air, feeling the breeze on her feathers. "Show me the steady wind," she said.

Sylvester pointed with his horn. "See the long feathers of grass bending together in one direction? That is a steady breeze. It is your friend. It will carry you gently. But you have to wait for it."

Pip waited. She watched the grass. She felt the air. Instead of leaping, she listened.

A warm, gentle breeze rose. It lifted the smells of mint and mud. It was steady.

Pip spread her wings. The wind caught her. She did not fight it. She rose slowly, sailed in a wide, calm circle, and then tilted her wings perfectly. Her feet came down like two tiny leaves—perfectly, silently, on the edge of the remaining puddle.

She didn't even make a ripple.

"I did it!" she breathed. "I landed!"

"You were brave to fly before," Sylvester said. "But today you were brave to wait. And you caught the wind perfectly."

Pip hopped next to him. "Will you teach me to map? I can be your eyes up high. I can tell you where the new puddles are forming, and you can tell me which winds are the safest."

From that day on, the sparrow who couldn't land became the snail's best friend and wind-scout. And Sylvester's map of the garden grew to be the most wonderful map of all—filled with puddles, and winds, and patience.

# Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens Pollinator Path, a New Educational Trail Focused on Native Habitats

**CHARLESTON, SC – June 10, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will launch its newest public education initiative, the “Pollinator Path,” on **July 12, 2026**. The dedicated trail, located on the east meadow loop of the garden’s 60-acre campus, spans a 0.8-mile accessible route designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through thoughtful planting and habitat stewardship.

The Pollinator Path is a direct response to recurring requests from garden visitors seeking practical, actionable guidance on attracting and sustaining pollinators in their own yards. Harborleaf staff noted that while general interest in pollinator conservation has grown, many guests expressed a need for clear, hands-on demonstrations of which native plants work best and how to arrange them for maximum ecological benefit.

“Our visitors have been asking for more than just labels on flowers—they want to know exactly what to plant, where to plant it, and why it matters,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path translates complex ecological science into simple, observable choices. When one person decides to plant a patch of milkweed or a night-blooming moonflower, that small decision becomes part of a connected habitat that stretches across neighborhoods and even cities.”

The trail features **native plant demonstration beds** arranged in replicable garden layouts—such as a small urban front yard, a suburban side strip, and a shaded backyard corner—each planted with species that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host material for local pollinators. Interpretive signage explains the life cycles of key pollinators and identifies the plants they rely on at each stage.

A highlight of the route is the **night-blooming flower station**, which showcases species like evening primrose, night-blooming jasmine, and moonflower that open after dusk to attract moths and other nocturnal pollinators. The station includes low-level lighting and seating to encourage evening visits.

All signage along the Pollinator Path uses **tactile elements**—including raised graphics and Braille descriptions—to ensure accessibility for visitors with visual impairments. In addition, **youth observation cards** are available at the trailhead, allowing children to document pollinator sightings and engage in simple citizen science activities. Shaded resting points with benches are placed at regular intervals, making the trail comfortable for visitors of all ages and abilities.

The path is fully wheelchair and stroller accessible, following the same grade as the existing east meadow loop, which is paved and gently sloping.

To deepen public engagement, Harborleaf will host a **volunteer-led weekend walk** every Saturday and Sunday morning from 9:00 a.m. to 10:30 a.m., beginning July 12 and continuing through October. Trained garden volunteers will lead small groups along the trail, pointing out active pollinators, explaining plant-pollinator relationships, and answering questions about starting a pollinator garden at home. No pre-registration is required for the walks, which are included with regular garden admission.

“The weekend walks are designed to be conversational and low-barrier,” Ortiz added. “A volunteer might stop at the butterfly milkweed bed and explain why it’s a host plant for monarch caterpillars, or show how a cluster of native asters can support late-season bees. We want people to leave feeling confident, not overwhelmed.”

The Pollinator Path project was developed in-house by Harborleaf’s horticulture and education teams, drawing on research from regional native plant societies and university cooperative extension programs. The garden emphasizes that the trail is strictly educational and nonpolitical, focusing on ecological literacy and personal action rather than advocacy or policy.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a private, nonprofit institution that has operated since 1984. It maintains over 2,000 species of plants, with a particular emphasis on Southeastern U.S. natives. The garden offers year-round programming for schools, families, and adult learners.

The Pollinator Path opens to the public at 9:00 a.m. on July 12, 2026. Regular garden hours are Tuesday through Sunday, 9:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m. Admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under six and Harborleaf members. For more information, visit the garden’s website or contact the media office.

# Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens ‘Pollinator Path’ to Teach Practical Habitat Gardening

**HARBORLEAF, Mass. – June 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the July 12, 2026 opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile accessible public education trail designed to show visitors how their own yards and balconies can become vital havens for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. Located on the garden’s east meadow loop, the trail will feature native plant demonstration beds, a dedicated night-blooming flower station, tactile interpretive signage, youth observation cards, shaded resting points, and volunteer-led weekend walks.

The Pollinator Path was developed in direct response to a growing number of visitor inquiries about simple, effective ways to support local pollinators through everyday planting choices. Over the past two years, Harborleaf staff noticed that guests—ranging from suburban homeowners to urban apartment dwellers—were increasingly asking for practical, non-commercial guidance on selecting flowers, arranging garden beds, and managing outdoor spaces to attract and sustain pollinator species.

“Visitors kept telling us, ‘I want to help, but I don’t know where to start,’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “That feedback shaped every element of the Pollinator Path. We designed it to demonstrate that small planting decisions—even a single pot of native milkweed on a balcony or a patch of wild bergamot in a community plot—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. When people understand that, they become part of a much larger network of conservation.”

The trail meanders through the garden’s east meadow, a site already rich in native grasses and wildflowers. The loop is fully accessible, with a compacted stone surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids, and gentle grades that allow visitors of all ages to explore at their own pace.

**Key features of the Pollinator Path include:**

- **Native Plant Demonstration Beds:** Five distinct planting zones illustrate how to layer flowers for continuous bloom from early spring through late fall. Each bed is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom times, and the specific pollinator groups each plant supports—such as bumblebees, swallowtail butterflies, clearwing moths, or ruby-throated hummingbirds. The beds use only regionally native species, including bee balm, goldenrod, New England aster, and butterfly weed.

- **Night-Blooming Flower Station:** A dedicated section showcases plants that open after dusk, such as evening primrose, moonflower, and four o’clocks. Interpretive panels explain how these flowers rely on nocturnal pollinators like hawk moths and bats, and offer tips for including night-blooming species in home gardens. Small solar-powered lights allow the station to remain visible after sunset for evening visitors.

- **Tactile Signage:** All interpretive signs along the path are designed with raised text, Braille, and textured graphics—such as embossed leaf shapes and flower silhouettes—so that visitors with visual impairments can feel the contours of different plants and pollinators. Signs are placed at a height accessible to children and wheelchair users.

- **Youth Observation Cards:** Children ages 5–12 can pick up a free, laminated “Pollinator Bingo” card at the trail entrance. The card encourages them to spot and identify eight common pollinators, from honeybees to monarch butterflies, with a small reward—a native wildflower seed packet—upon completion. The activity is self-guided and available during all garden hours.

- **Shaded Resting Points:** Four benches and two small pavilions with roof covers are spaced along the loop, providing places to sit, observe, and sketch. Each rest point is oriented toward a demonstration bed or a natural meadow vista, and includes a small wooden table with built-in plant identification guides.

- **Volunteer-Led Weekend Walks:** Starting July 19, trained Harborleaf docents will lead free, 45-minute guided walks on Saturdays and Sundays at 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. The walks will focus on seasonal highlights, such as peak butterfly activity in late summer and the appearance of night-blooming flowers in midsummer. No registration is required; groups gather at the trailhead near the east meadow parking lot.

The Pollinator Path is intended as a living classroom that evolves with the seasons. Garden staff plan to rotate demonstration plantings annually, introduce new species based on visitor feedback, and add digital resources—including a downloadable audio tour—by spring 2027.

“We are not telling people what to plant or how to manage their property; we are showing them what works in this region and why it matters,” Ortiz added. “The goal is to build confidence. When a visitor leaves the Pollinator Path and puts a native plant in the ground, that plant becomes part of a corridor of habitat that might connect to a neighbor’s garden, a schoolyard, or a local park. That connection—that sense of being part of something larger—is what we hope everyone takes away.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, founded in 1954, is a 200-acre public garden dedicated to plant conservation, education, and community engagement. The garden hosts more than 150,000 visitors annually and offers year-round programming for all ages. The Pollinator Path is funded entirely through the garden’s operating budget and volunteer contributions; no external grants or corporate sponsorships were used.

The Pollinator Path officially opens to the public on Sunday, July 12, 2026, at 9 a.m. with a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at the east meadow trailhead. Light refreshments will be provided, and docents will be on hand to answer questions. Regular garden admission applies; members and children under 12 are admitted free.

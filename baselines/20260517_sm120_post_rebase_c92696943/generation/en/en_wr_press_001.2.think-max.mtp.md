**SEASIDE, CA — July 12, 2026 —** The Harborleaf Botanical Garden today celebrated the grand opening of the Pollinator Path, a dedicated 0.8-mile public education trail designed to transform inspiration into action. Winding through the east meadow loop, the accessible trail is the institution’s direct and comprehensive response to a growing community desire for practical guidance. Over the last two years, garden surveys and program evaluations consistently revealed a specific gap: visitors were captivated by the curated gardens but wanted concrete, step-by-step information on how to replicate these vibrant ecosystems in their own backyards. The Pollinator Path is purpose-built to bridge that gap, providing clear, replicable models for supporting local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds.

“The most powerful feedback we receive is, ‘I want to help, but I don’t know how.’ The Pollinator Path is our answer to that call,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs. “It demonstrates that small planting decisions have a remarkable cumulative power. A pot of native milkweed on an apartment balcony, a dense patch of asters in a suburban front yard, a carefully maintained brush pile in a community garden—these seemingly minor acts, when multiplied across a neighborhood, create connected habitats. We want every visitor to leave feeling equipped and inspired to make a tangible contribution to the local ecosystem, right from their own doorstep.”

The trail serves as a living laboratory for ecological stewardship. Upon entering, visitors encounter a series of carefully designed native plant demonstration beds. These are organized by habitat type to show replicable models for different garden conditions. The “Meadow in Miniature” packs native grasses and forbs into a compact space, while the “Shade Garden for Moths” showcases the host plants nocturnal larvae require. Each bed connects the plants directly to the specific pollinators they serve, emphasizing the critical distinction between nectar sources for adults and host plants for caterpillars.

A distinctive feature of the path is the night-blooming flower station. Recognizing that a significant portion of pollination occurs after dark, this area is planted exclusively with pale, fragrant species such as evening primrose and moonflower. These plants have evolved specific cues to attract moths and beetles. The station utilizes low-level, red-spectrum lighting, allowing visitors to safely observe these nocturnal blooms without disturbing the sensitive vision of the insects they attract. Special twilight visiting hours are planned for this section to accommodate public interest in this hidden world.

Universal access and multisensory learning are core to the design. The entire 0.8-mile loop is paved with gentle grades, making it navigable for wheelchairs, strollers, and those with mobility aids. At every major station, tactile signage provides an alternative way to engage. Featuring high-contrast text, Braille, and three-dimensional reliefs of plant structures and insects, the signs allow visually impaired visitors to experience the content through touch. Shaded resting points constructed from native materials are strategically placed along the route to encourage quiet observation.

Youth engagement is a primary focus of the Pollinator Path. At the trailhead, families can pick up free, laminated youth observation cards developed in partnership with local science educators. These cards are designed to transform a family walk into an active field investigation. They prompt children to search for specific pollinator species, time the duration of flower visits, and sketch the varied shapes of native plants. This structured observation builds foundational scientific skills of inquiry and data collection. Upon completing their observations, young visitors can present their findings at the visitor center to earn an official “Junior Pollinator Protector” badge, a tangible reward for their contribution to understanding the garden’s ecosystem.

To deepen the educational impact of the trail, the Harborleaf Botanical Garden has launched a comprehensive volunteer-led weekend walk program. Scheduled every Saturday and Sunday throughout the peak growing season, these walks are intentionally limited to 15 participants. This small group format fosters an intimate, conversational learning environment where trained docents can address the specific questions and gardening challenges of each participant. The docents provide direct mentorship on topics such as sourcing local native plants, designing for continuous seasonal bloom, providing water sources, and implementing responsible maintenance practices that support wildlife.

The launch of the Pollinator Path reaffirms the Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s mission to provide accessible, nonpartisan environmental education. The project is rooted squarely in the science of botany and ecology, offering evidence-based recommendations for individual action. By focusing on practical, achievable steps, the garden empowers the community to build a network of healthy habitats extending far beyond the garden’s own boundaries.

“It sounds cowardly.”

“Yes.”

The apology hung in the air, raw and unfinished. She could have stopped there. She should have.

“What did you say?” she asked. “In the message.”

He looked at her then. Really looked. The regret in his eyes was a physical weight, something she could almost feel pressing against her chest.

“I said I was sorry. I said I was scared.” He paused. The rain hammered the roof. “And I said I never thought a place could be a person. But you were. You were my home.”

The words settled between them, as solid and heavy as the wet towels in his hands.

“I’m not asking you to forgive me,” he said.

“Good. Because I don’t know if I can.”

“That’s fair.”

The fluorescent lights above them buzzed. They flickered once, twice, a pale imitation of life. The world was about to rush back in.

The lights held, humming steadily. The lantern was suddenly just a cheap plastic thing on a battered table.

He pulled the damp towels from the dryer, stuffed them into a bag, and walked to the door. The bell chimed as he pushed it open, letting in a gust of salt-sweet air.

“I’m at the Anchor Motel. Room eight,” he said. “Take care of yourself, Nora.”

The door swung shut.

She was alone in the harsh fluorescent light.

She finished folding the sheet. Her hands were steady. She folded it with neat, precise creases, a small act of control. Then she looked at the lantern.

She reached out and clicked the switch. **Off.**

The world became a bright, sterile box. The crack in the window. The empty detergent shelves. The space where he had been standing.

She clicked the switch again. **On.**

The small, warm circle of light returned. It didn’t fill the room—it defined it. A quiet, unresolved gesture. A door left open, just a crack.

She picked up the lantern, left the laundry basket on the table, and pushed the door open into the heavy, salt-fed rain.

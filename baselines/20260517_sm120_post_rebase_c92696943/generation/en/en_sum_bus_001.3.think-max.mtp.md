A letter passes through the hands of several postal clerks before it reaches the person to whom it is addressed, and if each one of them has to stop to play with it awhile an appreciable amount of time is lost, not to mention the strain it puts on their respective tempers. The paper of which an envelope is made should always be opaque enough to conceal the contents of the letter.

Practically all business letters are typewritten. Occasionally a "Help Wanted" advertisement requests that the answer be in the applicant's own handwriting, but even this is rare. The letter should be arranged symmetrically on the page with ample margins all around. Nothing but experience in copying her own notes will teach a stenographer to estimate them correctly so that she will not have to rewrite badly placed letters. It is a little point, but an important one.

Each subject considered in a letter should be treated in a separate paragraph, and each paragraph should be set off from the others by a wider space than that between the lines, double space between the paragraphs when there is single space between the lines, triple space between the paragraphs when there is a double space between the lines, and so on.

Honorary titles should be used in the address when they take the place of "Mr.," such titles as Reverend, Doctor, Honorable (abbreviated to Rev., Dr., Hon.,) and the like. Titles should not be dropped except in the case of personal letters.

A letter on which there is two cents due has placed itself under a pretty severe handicap before it is opened.

It is astonishing how many letters go out every day unsigned, lacking enclosures, carrying the wrong addresses, bearing insufficient postage, and showing other evidences of carelessness and thoughtlessness. ---

### 1. Executive Summary

The author argues that poor planning causes the bulk of business correspondence to be wasteful, incomplete, or incoherent. Effective writing requires a clear purpose—to convey an idea and to build goodwill—executed with maximum courtesy and minimum length. The message must always overshadow the medium; self-conscious style or flashy formatting undermines credibility. Standardization (plain design, single-subject structure, correct addressing) creates professionalism and efficiency. Though the tools have evolved from paper to digital, the core failures remain: too many messages, muddled goals, and careless errors. Careful planning, concision, and a relentless focus on the reader define lasting business writing.

### 2. Five Principles of Effective Business Writing

1. **Intentionality:** *“There should be a reason back of every letter.”* Never write without a clear purpose, even if it is merely to express thanks.
2. **Message over Medium:** *“No letter… is any good which calls attention to itself instead of the message.”* Cleverness and style must never distract from the content.
3. **Maximum Courtesy, Minimum Length:** The best letter *“accomplishes this most courteously and most completely in the briefest space of time (and paper).”* Respect the reader’s time.
4. **Standardization is Professionalism:** *“Startling variations from the conventional format indicate freakishness rather than originality.”* Conform to professional norms to build trust.
5. **Meticulous Accuracy:** Faults like unsigned letters, missing enclosures, and wrong addresses betray carelessness that destroys credibility before the message is read.

### 3. Advice Then and Now

| Original-Era Advice | Modern Equivalent |
|---|---|
| “Half the business letters… should never be written at all.” | Before writing an email or Slack message, ask if the communication is truly necessary or if a quick call solves the problem. |
| “A business letter should handle only one subject.” | Keep emails and digital threads focused on a single topic for easy searching, filing, and replying. |
| “Address an individual… rather than ‘Dear Sir’.” | Personalize every message with the recipient’s name and specific context. |
| “Plain white paper… gaudy colors, flashy designs… are taboo.” | Use clean, minimalist templates and standard fonts; avoid distracting backgrounds or excessive branding. |
| “The letter should be arranged symmetrically on the page with ample margins.” | Use short paragraphs, white space, and bullet points for easy scanning on screen. |
| “It is astonishing how many letters go out… lacking enclosures… bearing insufficient postage.” | Implement a pre-send checklist: verify attachments, links, recipient fields, and subject lines before clicking send. |

### 4. Updating Dated Advice

**Dated Advice (Gender Roles):** *“In most places the typing is taken care of by girls… it is necessary for the men and women who dictate the letters to know what constitutes a pleasing make-up.”*
- **Underlying Principle:** The sender is ultimately responsible for the final product, even when delegating the mechanical preparation.
- **Modern Update:** Today, professionals often type their own communications or rely on AI tools. The principle remains unchanged: the author must oversee quality by proofreading, using proper templates, and ensuring polished output. Responsibility cannot be outsourced.

**Dated Advice (Postal Specifics):** *“State abbreviations should be used sparingly… ‘City’ is not sufficient… and postage should be complete.”*
- **Underlying Principle:** Ambiguous or incomplete information creates friction and frustrates the recipient.
- **Modern Update:** For physical mail, the two-letter USPS code is standard. For digital communications, the principle translates to using explicit subject lines, full hyperlinks (not “click here”), clear meeting titles, and complete CRM data to prevent confusion and delay.

**Dated Advice (Design Rules):** *“Plain white paper of good quality is always in good taste… Hotels are often pictured… there is a very good reason for it… Some factories carry their own picture.”*
- **Underlying Principle:** Design should serve function and context, never personal taste.
- **Modern Update:** Images are standard in marketing, slide decks, and newsletters, but they must serve the core message. For transactional emails or reports, default to no-frills formatting. Let the context dictate the layout, not the desire to be “distinctive.”

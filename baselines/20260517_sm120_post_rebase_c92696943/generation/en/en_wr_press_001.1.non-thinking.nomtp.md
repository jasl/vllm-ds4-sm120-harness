**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ Education Trail to Inspire Neighborhood Habitat Creation**

**HARBORLEAF, MA – June 10, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open a new outdoor education trail, the “Pollinator Path,” on July 12, 2026, offering visitors a dedicated, accessible resource for learning how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through small-scale planting decisions.

Located on the east meadow loop of the garden’s 65-acre grounds, the 0.8-mile trail is fully accessible and designed to address a growing demand from visitors for practical, hands-on guidance on creating pollinator-friendly habitats. The trail will feature native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, youth observation cards for guided exploration, and shaded resting points with interpretive panels. Weekend walks led by trained volunteers will begin the weekend following the opening.

“Our visitors have repeatedly told us they want clear, actionable advice on how to turn their own yards, balconies, or community spaces into stopovers for pollinators,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer to that request. By showcasing native plants that thrive in our region and explaining the science behind bloom times, flower shapes, and nectar availability, we aim to show that even small planting decisions can create connected habitats across neighborhoods.”

The trail’s layout follows a natural progression from day-active pollinators to species that are active after dusk. The native plant demonstration beds highlight plants such as milkweed, goldenrod, bee balm, and asters, chosen for their proven value to local pollinator species. Each bed is paired with signage explaining which pollinators benefit from that plant, what time of year it blooms, and how to establish similar plants at home.

A dedicated night-blooming flower station will introduce visitors to plants such as evening primrose, moonflower, and garden phlox, which provide nectar for moths and nocturnal beetles. Tactile signage, including raised text and plant-texture panels, ensures that visitors with low vision can engage with the content. Youth observation cards—available at the trailhead—encourage children to identify species and record observations, turning each visit into a self-guided science activity.

Throughout the trail, shaded resting points offer benches and interpretive panels that delve into topics such as the life cycle of the monarch butterfly, the foraging range of a single honeybee, and the role of dead wood and leaf litter in overwintering insects. These features are designed for visitors who wish to linger and study without time pressure.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will be offered every Saturday and Sunday at 10 a.m. and 1 p.m., beginning July 18, 2026. These walks, led by trained garden docents, will focus on seasonal bloom variations and identification of common pollinator visitors. No registration is required.

The garden’s decision to create the Pollinator Path follows a survey conducted in 2025, in which more than 70 percent of respondents indicated they wanted more specific guidance on planting for pollinators. Harborleaf Botanical Garden staff designed the trail to bridge the gap between general ecological awareness and practical, at-home implementation.

“We are not telling people what to plant everywhere—we are showing them what works here, and why it matters,” Ortiz added. “When a homeowner plants a single patch of milkweed, it becomes a refueling station for monarchs traveling hundreds of miles. When a block of neighbors plants a corridor of goldenrod and aster, it creates a network. The Pollinator Path helps people see that they are part of a much larger system.”

The east meadow loop was selected for its gentle terrain and existing proximity to open meadow habitat, which already attracts a range of beneficial insects and birds. The trail surface is packed stone dust, suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers. Parking is available in the main lot, with a marked accessible entrance near the garden’s visitor center.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a nonprofit public garden dedicated to education, conservation, and community engagement.

# Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ Educational Trail to Teach Public How to Support Bees, Butterflies, and Hummingbirds

**HARBORLEAF, [State] – June 15, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile public education trail designed to give visitors practical, hands-on guidance for attracting and sustaining native pollinators. The trail, located on the east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026, and is fully accessible to guests of all ages and abilities.

The Pollinator Path responds directly to a growing demand from garden visitors who have asked for more concrete, actionable advice on how to create habitats for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds in their own yards and neighborhoods. The trail blends demonstration gardens with interactive learning stations, tactile signage, and structured observation activities for children and families.

“Our visitors repeatedly told us they want to help pollinators but don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path turns that curiosity into confidence. It shows that even small planting decisions—a single pot of native milkweed on a balcony or a patch of bee balm in a community garden—can create connected, thriving habitats across entire neighborhoods.”

The trail’s design emphasizes both beauty and education. Along the loop, visitors will encounter several distinct features:

- **Native Plant Demonstration Beds** – These curated plots showcase a variety of regionally native flowers, grasses, and shrubs that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host material for key pollinator species. Plants are labeled with common and scientific names, bloom seasons, and pollinator preferences.

- **Night-Blooming Flower Station** – A dedicated section of the trail highlights plants that open or release fragrance after dusk, such as evening primrose and moonflower, to attract nocturnal pollinators including moths and bats. Interpretive signage explains the unique adaptations of night-active species.

- **Tactile Signage** – Each interpretive panel along the path includes raised text, embossed plant shapes, and textured surfaces so that visitors with low vision or blindness can experience the trail through touch. Braille translations accompany all written content.

- **Youth Observation Cards** – Young visitors can pick up free laminated cards at the trailhead, which guide them through simple observation exercises—counting the number of bees on a flower, sketching a butterfly’s wing pattern, or noting the time of day a hummingbird visits. Cards include prompts for discussion with parents or group leaders.

- **Shaded Resting Points** – Benches and pergolas covered with native climbing vines are spaced every 200 feet along the trail, offering respite for older adults, families with small children, or anyone needing a break. Each rest area is positioned near a particularly rich pollinator activity zone.

- **Volunteer-Led Weekend Walks** – Starting the first weekend after opening, trained garden volunteers will lead free, guided walks along the Pollinator Path every Saturday and Sunday morning at 10 a.m. and 1 p.m. The walks are designed for small groups (up to 15 people) and will focus on identifying pollinators, understanding plant-pollinator relationships, and discussing how to replicate habitat features at home.

The Pollinator Path is permanently installed and will be open during the garden’s regular hours year-round. No advance reservation is needed for self-guided visits, though weekend walk participants are encouraged to sign up at the garden’s welcome center to guarantee a spot.

The trail’s location on the east meadow loop was chosen for its natural diversity and gentle terrain. The entire 0.8-mile route is surfaced with compacted crushed stone suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. Wide turnouts allow for passing and group gatherings.

The project was entirely conceived and executed by Harborleaf Botanical Garden staff and volunteers. No external funding, celebrity endorsements, or political partnerships were involved. The garden’s mission remains focused on fostering ecological literacy and community stewardship through direct, nonpartisan engagement with the natural world.

“We are not advocating policy; we are advocating action,” Ortiz added. “Every plant that a person puts in the ground is a vote for life. And when thousands of people make those small choices, the collective impact is enormous. We hope the Pollinator Path inspires people to see their own gardens, balconies, and park strips as part of a larger network—a living corridor for creatures that sustain our food systems and ecosystems.”

The opening date of July 12 will be marked with a community celebration from 9 a.m. to 12 p.m., including a ribbon-cutting ceremony, pollinator-themed crafts for children, and opportunities to meet the volunteer docents who will lead the weekend walks. Regular garden admission applies, but all Pollinator Path features are included at no extra charge.

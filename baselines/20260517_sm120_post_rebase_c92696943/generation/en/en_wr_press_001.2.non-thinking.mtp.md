**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New Pollinator Path to Inspire Community-Wide Habitat Creation**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – June 2, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its newest public education feature, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through practical, small-scale planting choices. The accessible, 0.8-mile loop will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026, in the garden’s east meadow.

Developed in direct response to visitor requests for more actionable guidance on pollinator-friendly gardening, the Pollinator Path transforms a scenic meadow loop into an immersive outdoor classroom. The trail addresses a growing public interest in reversing habitat fragmentation by demonstrating how individual yards, balconies, and community spaces can serve as vital stepping stones for pollinators.

“Over the past several seasons, our visitors have consistently asked for more than just a list of plants—they want to see exactly how to design a space that works for pollinators throughout the entire growing season,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path gives them that blueprint. It shows that small, deliberate planting decisions—whether in a window box, a front yard, or a school garden—can collectively create a connected network of habitats across an entire neighborhood.”

The trail features a series of carefully curated demonstration areas. Native plant demonstration beds showcase species that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host material, arranged by bloom time to ensure food sources from early spring through late fall. A night-blooming flower station highlights plants that support moths and other crepuscular pollinators, with interpretive panels explaining the role of scent and pale coloration in attracting nighttime visitors.

To ensure accessibility for all visitors, the trail includes tactile signage with raised illustrations and Braille text, allowing visually impaired guests to engage with the content independently. Youth observation cards—available at the trailhead—encourage children to document pollinator sightings, note flower preferences, and track seasonal changes during repeat visits. Shaded resting points with seating are placed at regular intervals, offering opportunities for quiet observation and reflection.

The Pollinator Path is fully wheelchair-accessible, with a compacted stone surface that meets Americans with Disabilities Act (ADA) guidelines. The east meadow loop was chosen for its gentle grade and existing biodiversity, which required minimal alteration to the natural landscape.

Beginning the weekend after the opening, the garden will host a volunteer-led weekend walk along the trail. These guided experiences, led by trained docents and community volunteers, will provide deeper insights into the biology of local pollinators and the specific habitat requirements of each species. The walks are free with garden admission and will run on Saturdays and Sundays through the peak growing season.

The development of the Pollinator Path reflects a broader shift in public horticulture toward actionable conservation education. Rather than simply observing nature from a distance, visitors are increasingly seeking knowledge they can apply at home. Harborleaf’s new trail bridges that gap by offering clear, replicable examples of plant groupings, spacing, and seasonal management.

“One of the most powerful things we hear from guests is that after a visit, they feel equipped to make their own outdoor spaces more welcoming to wildlife,” Ortiz added. “When a homeowner plants a single cluster of milkweed, or a renter adds a pot of native salvia to a balcony, they are not just decorating—they are inserting a critical resource into a larger ecological map. Those small acts add up.”

The garden plans to update the trail’s interpretive content annually to reflect changing pollinator populations and emerging research. A digital companion guide will also be made available on the garden’s website, allowing visitors to access plant lists and observation prompts from home.

The Pollinator Path opens to the public on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. Harborleaf Botanical Garden is located at 1420 Arboretum Drive, Harborleaf, California. Regular garden admission applies.

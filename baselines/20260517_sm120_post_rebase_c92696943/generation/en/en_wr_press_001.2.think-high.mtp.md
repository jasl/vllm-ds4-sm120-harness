**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Launches New ‘Pollinator Path’ Public Education Trail**

HARBORLEAF, [State] – July 1, 2026 – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its newest educational attraction, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through thoughtful planting and habitat stewardship. “We kept hearing the same thing from guests: ‘I want to help the bees and butterflies, but I don’t know where to start,’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer to that. It shows that even small planting decisions—like adding a patch of milkweed or a night-blooming jasmine—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods and support entire pollinator populations. You don’t need a large garden to make a difference.”

The new trail winds through the east meadow loop, a gently sloping path designed for visitors of all ages and abilities. Key features include:

- **Native plant demonstration beds** that showcase local wildflowers, grasses, and shrubs proven to attract a range of pollinators. Each bed is organized by bloom season—spring, summer, and fall—so visitors can see year-round planting strategies in action.
- A **night-blooming flower station** that highlights plants such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming cereus. This area will remain open after dusk on select evenings, with interpretive signage explaining how moths and other nocturnal pollinators rely on pale, fragrant flowers.
- **Tactile signage** with raised text, Braille, and sensory elements such as textured leaf replicas and seed pod samples, ensuring that the trail experience is accessible to visitors with visual impairments.
- **Youth observation cards** available at the trailhead. - **Shaded resting points** with benches placed at regular intervals, each one positioned to offer a view of a different demonstration bed. Interpretive panels at these stops provide deeper context about the lifecycle of pollinators and the ecological role of native plants.
- A **volunteer-led weekend walk** program that will run every Saturday and Sunday from 10 a.m. to 11 a.m. beginning July 13. Trained garden volunteers will lead small groups along the trail, sharing hands-on tips for selecting plants, preparing soil, and reducing pesticide use without sacrificing garden aesthetics.

The east meadow loop, already one of the garden’s most popular walking routes, was chosen for its natural transition from open meadow to shaded woodland edge—a microhabitat mix that mirrors the varied conditions many home gardeners face. The entire 0.8-mile loop is paved with a smooth, stable surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers, and grade changes are minimal to ensure ease of use.

Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is not only an educational resource but also a call to action that transcends political or ideological lines. “Pollinators don’t care about our differences. They just need food, shelter, and water. Our goal is to give people the confidence to become habitat stewards in their own backyards, schoolyards, or even community gardens. One patch of native plants may seem small, but when thousands of people plant those patches, we create a network of life that sustains biodiversity right where we live.”

The garden has planned a series of complementary programs to coincide with the trail’s launch. Beginning in August, a monthly “Night Walk” series will allow visitors to experience the night-blooming station under the supervision of staff naturalists. Additionally, a free downloadable guidebook—featuring plant lists, garden layout tips, and a seasonal planting calendar—will be available on the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website starting July 12.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, a nonprofit organization dedicated to plant conservation, education, and community engagement, has operated for more than 40 years. The Pollinator Path represents the garden’s latest effort to translate scientific research into accessible, actionable guidance for the public. All trail features were designed with input from local entomologists, horticulturists, and accessibility advocates.

The Pollinator Path will be open during regular garden hours: daily from 8 a.m. to sunset. Admission is included with general garden entry; free access is offered to Harborleaf members and children under 5. Weekend volunteer-led walks are included with admission and do not require advance registration, though groups of 10 or more are encouraged to notify the program office in advance.

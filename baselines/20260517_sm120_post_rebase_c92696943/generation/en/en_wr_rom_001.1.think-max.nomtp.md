“I never heard the end of it,” she said. Her voice was steady, but her hands were gripping the sides of her book. “I called you. The next day. And the next. You never answered.”

“I listened to it at the airport,” he said, barely a whisper. “I had a layover. I was going to call you back. But I didn’t know how to explain running away.”

“You never tried.”

“No.” The word was a wound. “I kept the machine. I couldn’t let go of the tape. It was all I had. Proof that someone wanted me to stay.”

“It wasn’t proof of anything.” She stood abruptly, the book sliding off her lap onto the floor. She ignored it and walked to the washer, punching the lid. It was locked.

The machine beeped. The cycle finished. The lid clicked.

She yanked it open. A billow of steam rushed out, thick and warm, carrying the clean scent of cotton and detergent. It clouded the air between them, softening the sharp lines of his face.

She grabbed a towel. It was heavy and hot. She slammed it on the folding table.

He moved before she could ask him not to. He took the other corner. She stiffened.

“Let me help.”

She did not say yes. She did not say no. She pulled her corner taut, matching his. They folded in silence. The routine was absurdly domestic—pulling corners together, smoothing the warm fabric, stacking them into a perfect pile.

They stood on opposite sides of the table. The battery lantern sat between them, a small sun illuminating the soft pattern of the terry cloth.

“Why did you leave?” she asked, not looking up.

“I was scared,” he said. “Of the future. Of being not good enough. Of dragging you into something I couldn’t see the end of.”

“You could have told me.”

“I was too young to know how. And by the time I did, it was too late.”

The last towel was folded. She put her hands flat on the pile, feeling the heat radiate into her palms.

The lights buzzed. They flickered. For a moment, the laundromat was plunged back into deep dark, only the lantern holding the space. The shadows leaped across his face, making him look younger, softer.

Then the power surged back. The fluorescents flared to life, harsh and sterile, washing out the warmth of the lantern light. The illusion of intimacy shattered.

Jack reached down and picked up the battery lantern. It was still glowing.

“Maya.”

She gathered the laundry basket. The folded towels sat warm and perfect inside it.

She walked to the door. Her hand was on the push bar. She stopped.

She set the basket down. She walked back. Just two steps.

She looked at the lantern in his hands. She reached out, and her fingers closed around the switch. She clicked it off. The tiny light died.

“You left this on.”

“I’m not very good at turning things off.”

She looked at the dark lantern. She looked at him. Her hand hovered in the air between them, a hairsbreadth from his. Then it dropped.

She turned, gathered the basket, and walked out into the rain.

The door swung shut behind her.

Jack stood in the bright, empty laundromat. The only sounds were the hum of the lights and the drum of the rain against the glass. He set the lantern down on the table.

It sat there, alone, a small cold vessel in the harsh light where the warm dark used to be.

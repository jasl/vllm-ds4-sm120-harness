**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens ‘Pollinator Path’ to Help Visitors Turn Gardens into Wildlife Havens**

**HARBORLEAF, CT – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of a new public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through practical, home-garden planting choices. The “Pollinator Path” will officially open to the public on **July 12, 2026**, in the east meadow loop, a fully accessible 0.8-mile trail that winds through native plant demonstration beds and interactive learning stations.

The new trail responds directly to a growing number of visitor inquiries asking for specific, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly habitats. Over the past two years, Harborleaf staff recorded a sharp uptick in questions about which local plants provide nectar for Monarch butterflies, which flowers attract hummingbirds, and how to manage a yard to support moths and native bees without using chemical pesticides. The Pollinator Path turns those questions into a self-guided, hands-on learning experience.

“We heard from our visitors again and again: ‘I want to help pollinators, but I don’t know where to start,’” said **Maya Ortiz**, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. It shows that even the smallest balcony planter or backyard garden can become a vital link in a much larger network of habitat. When people make thoughtful planting decisions in their own neighborhoods, they can literally create connected corridors for pollinators to travel across the city and beyond.”

The trail begins at the east meadow entrance and follows a gently sloping, paved loop suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and visitors with limited mobility. Along the route, seven distinct feature areas offer educational opportunities:

- **Native Plant Demonstration Beds** – Four raised beds display locally adapted wildflowers, shrubs, and grasses that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host plants for different pollinator species. Each bed is labeled with common and botanical names, bloom season, and specific pollinator targets (e.g., “Milkweed for Monarch caterpillars,” “Goldenrod for late-season bees”).
- **Night-Blooming Flower Station** – A dedicated small garden of evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jasmine, lit by soft, low-glowing path markers. Interpretive panels explain how moths—including the iconic Hummingbird Clearwing—use scent and pale color to forage after dark.
- **Tactile Signage** – All interpretive signs include raised text, braille, and embossed illustrations of plant shapes and pollinator body parts. Visitors can feel the difference between a tubular flower (favorite of hummingbirds) and a flat landing pad (preferred by butterflies).
- **Youth Observation Cards** – A free, take-along activity card for children encourages them to count different pollinator types they spot, match flowers to visitors, and record notes on weather and time of day. Completed cards can be returned to the garden’s visitor center for a small sticker reward.
- **Shaded Resting Points** – Three bench alcoves with overhead shade sails allow visitors to sit and observe pollinator activity at close range. Each bench is positioned near a high-traffic flower bed, with a small mounted magnifying glass for detailed viewing.
- **Volunteer-Led Weekend Walks** – Starting July 13, trained garden volunteers will lead free, 30-minute guided walks along the Pollinator Path every Saturday and Sunday at 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. through the end of September. No registration is required.

According to garden records, the east meadow loop previously served as a simple grass path with limited interpretive value. The conversion to the Pollinator Path involved the installation of more than 1,200 individual plants sourced from local nurseries, all chosen for their low water needs and high ecological value in the region’s climate. No synthetic fertilizers or pesticides will be used in the demonstration beds, and garden staff will maintain the trail using organic mulch and hand weeding.

Maya Ortiz emphasized that the trail’s lessons are designed to be directly transferable to home landscapes, regardless of property size. “A window box with native phlox can attract a hummingbird. An unmown patch of clover can feed dozens of bees every afternoon. The idea isn’t that everyone needs a full meadow—just that every single plant choice matters,” she said.

The garden plans to update the trail’s interpretive content seasonally to reflect bloom times and pollinator activity cycles. A companion digital guide—accessible via smartphone QR codes posted at each station—will provide extended video content and species identification tips.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden will host a short opening ceremony at the east meadow entrance on **July 12 at 9:00 a.m.** Garden staff will be on hand to answer questions, and the first 50 visitors to arrive will receive a complimentary native wildflower seed packet.

**About Harborleaf Botanical Garden**
Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a nonprofit public garden dedicated to plant conservation, education, and community engagement. Its 65-acre campus features curated plant collections, habitat restoration projects, and year-round programming for all ages. The garden is open daily from sunrise to sunset. Admission is free for members; general admission applies for non-members.

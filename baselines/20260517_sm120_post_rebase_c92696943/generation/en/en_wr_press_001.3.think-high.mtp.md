**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ Education Trail to Inspire Community Conservation**

HARBORLEAF — July 12, 2026 — Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile accessible public education trail designed to provide visitors with practical, hands-on guidance for supporting local pollinators. The trail, located along the garden’s east meadow loop, will welcome the public starting July 12, 2026, and is part of the institution’s ongoing commitment to ecological education and community engagement.

The new trail features a series of native plant demonstration beds, a dedicated night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, youth observation cards to encourage scientific exploration, shaded resting points, and volunteer-led weekend walks. Each element has been carefully planned to offer actionable lessons on how individual gardening choices can create connected habitats for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds across neighborhoods.

“Our visitors have consistently asked for more practical guidance on planting for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path was designed to show that even small planting decisions in a backyard, on a balcony, or along a street median can link up to create a vital corridor for pollinators. When we think about habitat connectivity, every flower counts.”

The trail’s native plant demonstration beds showcase species specifically selected for their value to regional pollinators. Each bed is labeled with both common and scientific names, bloom times, and the specific pollinators they attract. The night-blooming flower station, located near the trail’s midpoint, highlights plants such as evening primrose and moonflower that serve nocturnal moths and bats—an often-overlooked group of essential pollinators.

Tactile signage along the route includes raised images and braille descriptions of plants and insects, allowing visitors with visual impairments to experience the trail through touch and text. Younger visitors can pick up youth observation cards at the trailhead, prompting them to record sightings of pollinators, identify plant shapes, and note weather conditions. The cards are designed to align with elementary and middle school science curricula.

Shaded resting points—equipped with benches and interpretive panels—provide opportunities for reflection and learning. The panels explain the ecological roles of different pollinator groups, the importance of reducing pesticide use, and simple steps to create pollinator-friendly spaces at home.

Every Saturday and Sunday from July 12 through October, trained volunteers will lead guided walks along the Pollinator Path. These sessions are free with garden admission and will cover trail highlights, seasonal changes in bloom and insect activity, and tips for adapting home gardens to support a diversity of species.

Ortiz added, “We want people to leave this trail feeling empowered, not overwhelmed. The idea is that one pot of native milkweed on an apartment balcony can make a difference for monarch butterflies, and a row of coneflowers in a front yard can feed dozens of bees throughout the summer. When neighbors do the same, a patchwork of small habitats becomes a connected landscape. That’s the message we hope visitors carry home.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden developed the Pollinator Path in response to a growing number of visitor inquiries about pollinator decline and how individuals can help. Staff conducted informal surveys at the garden’s visitor center and during previous educational programs, which consistently identified a need for clear, local, and actionable information.

“It’s one thing to hear that pollinators are in trouble, and another to know exactly which plant to put in the ground and why,” said Ortiz. “Our goal is to bridge that gap with a direct, hands-on experience that anyone can replicate.”

The east meadow loop, where the Pollinator Path is situated, was selected for its existing diversity of flowering plants and its relatively flat terrain, making it fully accessible to visitors using wheelchairs, strollers, or walkers. The entire trail is paved or surfaced with compacted crushed stone, and the interpretive stations are placed at heights suitable for all ages and abilities.

The garden plans to update the youth observation cards seasonally and to introduce a digital companion guide—available via QR codes posted along the trail—in the coming months. Volunteer-led walks will continue through the end of October, with potential extensions based on public interest.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a nonprofit educational institution dedicated to fostering a deeper understanding of plants, ecosystems, and sustainable gardening practices. The garden is open daily from 9 a.m. to 6 p.m., with extended hours during peak bloom seasons.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including a trail map, a schedule of volunteer-led walks, and tips for creating pollinator-friendly spaces at home, visit www.harborleafgardens.org/pollinatorpath or contact the garden’s education department.

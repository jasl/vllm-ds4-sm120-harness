**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ Educational Trail to Inspire Home Gardening for Wildlife**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 1, 2026** – The Harborleaf Botanical Garden will officially open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on Sunday, July 12, 2026, transforming the east meadow loop into a living classroom designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through practical, small-scale planting choices.

The 0.8-mile accessible trail, located in the Garden’s east meadow, will feature native plant demonstration beds, a specialized night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, and youth observation cards that encourage children to document pollinators they encounter. The route also includes shaded resting points at regular intervals and will host volunteer-led weekend walks beginning July 19, 2026.

The new trail directly responds to a growing trend among visitors: a desire for concrete, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly habitats at home. Garden staff noted that many guests have asked for more than just general information, seeking step-by-step advice on which plants to choose and how to arrange them for maximum ecological benefit.

“We kept hearing the same thing from our visitors: ‘I want to help the bees and butterflies, but I don’t know where to start,’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer to that question. It shows that even small planting decisions, like choosing a single native milkweed or adding a patch of late-blooming asters, can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods and support entire populations of pollinators.”

The trail’s design emphasizes accessibility and education for all ages. Tactile signage along the route provides interpretive information in braille and raised text, allowing visitors with visual impairments to identify plants and learn about pollinator life cycles through touch. The youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, guide children through a simple scavenger hunt of pollinator-friendly features, such as flower shapes that attract hummingbirds versus moths or the appearance of caterpillar host plants.

The night-blooming flower station, planted with species such as evening primrose and moonflower, will be illuminated with low-impact lighting during evening hours, offering a rare opportunity for visitors to observe the flowers and moths that depend on nocturnal pollination. The station is paired with interpretive panels explaining the critical role of moths, often overlooked in favor of daytime pollinators.

Native plant demonstration beds throughout the trail showcase species selected for their proven value to local pollinators, including California lilac, desert globemallow, and chaparral currant. Each bed is labeled with both common and scientific names, bloom seasons, and the specific pollinators each plant serves. The shaded resting points, constructed from sustainably sourced materials, provide seating with views of the meadow and interpretive text about the ecological role of each plant community.

The volunteer-led weekend walks, starting July 19, will be offered Saturdays and Sundays at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. through the end of September. Trained docents will lead small groups along the trail, answering questions and demonstrating identification techniques. No registration is required, and all materials, including the youth observation cards, are provided free of charge.

The Pollinator Path is part of Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s broader mission to promote ecological literacy and community-based conservation. The Garden has long offered workshops on composting and water-wise gardening, and the new trail extends that education to the specific needs of pollinators in urban and suburban settings.

“When you plant for one butterfly, you are planting for dozens of other creatures too—the bees that pollinate your tomatoes, the moths that feed bats and birds, the hummingbirds that visit your balcony,” Ortiz added. “We want people to leave this trail feeling empowered, not overwhelmed. You don’t need a large yard or a lot of money to make a difference. A single pot of flowering sage on a balcony can be a stepping stone for a migrating monarch.”

The trail opens to the public on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. Harborleaf Botanical Garden is open daily from 8:00 a.m. to sunset. Admission to the Garden applies; members and children under 12 enter free. The Pollinator Path is wheelchair- and stroller-accessible.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, volunteer-led walks, or educational programming, visit the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website or contact the communications office.

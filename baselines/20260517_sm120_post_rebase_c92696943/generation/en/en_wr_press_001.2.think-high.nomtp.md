**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils Pollinator Path, a New Educational Trail Opening July 12**

HARBORLEAF, CA – June 10, 2026 – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on July 12, 2026, providing visitors with practical, science-based guidance on creating habitats for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile accessible loop, located in the garden’s east meadow, features native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage, youth observation cards, shaded resting points, and a volunteer-led weekend walk program.

The trail was developed in direct response to growing visitor interest in how individual planting decisions can support local pollinators. Garden staff observed that guests frequently asked for more detailed, hands-on advice about which plants to choose and how to arrange them to benefit insects and birds. The Pollinator Path aims to turn that curiosity into actionable knowledge.

“People often think that helping pollinators requires a large property or a major landscaping overhaul,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “But small planting decisions in a backyard, on a balcony, or along a street can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. This trail shows visitors how to start those changes, one plant at a time.”

The trail begins at the east meadow entrance and follows a fully accessible paved loop, suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. Along the route, nine native plant demonstration beds showcase species that attract specific pollinators. For example, one bed highlights milkweed for monarch butterflies, another features blazing star for bees, and a third groups night-blooming plants such as evening primrose and moonflower for moths. The night-blooming station includes interpretive panels explaining how moths navigate by scent and moonlight, and a low-level lighting system that will be activated during evening programs later in the season.

Tactile signage is installed at each station, featuring embossed leaf shapes, seed pod textures, and Braille descriptions. The signs are designed to be readable by sighted and visually impaired visitors alike, and they include QR codes linking to audio recordings in English and Spanish. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, guide children through simple activities such as counting the number of different bee species seen in 10 minutes or sketching a flower visited by a hummingbird. The cards can be turned in at the garden’s visitor center for a small reward sticker.

Five shaded resting points are spaced evenly along the loop, each equipped with benches, a water fountain, and a small interpretive stand describing a different pollinator’s life cycle. One rest stop focuses on the solitary nesting habits of native bees, another on the migration patterns of hummingbirds.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will begin on July 19 and continue every Saturday and Sunday through October. Each walk lasts 45 minutes and is led by a trained docent who can answer questions and offer personalized planting recommendations. No reservation is required, and walks are free with garden admission.

The Pollinator Path was designed and planted by Harborleaf’s horticulture team with input from local entomologists and ecologists. The project uses only regionally native plants, all sourced from the garden’s own nursery. Maintenance is carried out without pesticides or herbicides, consistent with the garden’s integrated pest management practices.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has long been a resource for sustainable gardening education. The Pollinator Path expands on existing programs such as the monthly “Gardening for Wildlife” workshop series and the annual Pollinator Count citizen science event. With the new trail, the garden aims to reach a broader audience, including families with young children, educators, and homeowners looking for low-cost, high-impact ways to support biodiversity.

“We see this trail as a living classroom,” Ortiz added. “When a family walks the Pollinator Path and then goes home to plant a small patch of coneflowers, that’s a direct contribution to a healthier ecosystem. Our hope is that every visitor leaves feeling empowered, not overwhelmed.”

The Pollinator Path opens to the public on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. Regular garden hours apply: 9:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m. daily. Admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under 5. Members of Harborleaf Botanical Garden receive free entry. For more information about the trail, weekend walks, or youth observation cards, visit the garden’s website or contact the visitor center.

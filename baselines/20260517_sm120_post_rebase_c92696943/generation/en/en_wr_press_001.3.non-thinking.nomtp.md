**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” to Teach Visitors How to Build Wildlife-Friendly Habitats**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its newest public education trail, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated 0.8-mile accessible loop in the east meadow designed to provide visitors with practical, hands-on guidance for supporting local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds.

The trail opens to the public on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m., and represents the garden’s most recent effort to address a growing demand from visitors for specific, actionable advice on how to turn private yards, community plots, and balcony containers into productive pollinator corridors. The path is fully accessible, with a compacted gravel surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers, and features shaded resting points at regular intervals.

According to garden staff, the project was motivated by frequent questions from guests seeking more than general gardening tips. “Our visitors have been asking for years: ‘What should I actually plant? How do I make a difference for the insects and birds I see in my backyard?’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. We want to show people that small planting decisions—choosing a native milkweed over a nonnative ornamental, for instance—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods and even entire cities. It’s about turning individual gardens into a patchwork of support for pollinators.”

The Pollinator Path is organized into several distinct learning stations. The native plant demonstration beds showcase species that thrive in the region’s climate, including California poppy, goldenrod, and buckwheat, each labeled with clear botanical names and bloom season information. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights plants such as evening primrose and moonflower that attract nocturnal pollinators like moths and bats, complete with soft ambient lighting to allow evening visits. Throughout the trail, tactile signage incorporates raised text, Braille, and simple illustrations to serve visitors with visual impairments or reading difficulties, ensuring that the educational content is accessible to all.

For younger visitors, the garden has produced a set of youth observation cards. These laminated, reusable cards list common pollinator species, their preferred plants, and simple prompts—such as “Count how many bees visit this flower in two minutes”—to encourage independent exploration. The cards are available at the trailhead and can be returned after use. Volunteer-led weekend walks will be offered every Saturday and Sunday at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. beginning July 13, led by trained docents who will discuss the ecological relationships between plants and pollinators and answer specific questions from participants.

The trail’s design emphasizes comfort and ease of use. Benches constructed from locally sourced recycled materials are placed every 200 to 300 feet, providing rest stops with views of the meadow. Overhead shade structures made from reclaimed wood offer protection from sun during warmer months. The path itself is a gentle loop with minimal elevation change, making it suitable for visitors of all ages and physical abilities.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has long served as a community resource for sustainable horticulture and environmental education. The development of the Pollinator Path involved consultation with local ecologists, habitat restoration specialists, and garden staff with expertise in native plant cultivation. No external funding organizations or celebrity endorsers were involved in the project. The garden’s own horticulture team prepared the planting beds over the past two growing seasons, using plants grown from seed on-site to ensure genetic diversity and local adaptation.

“This trail is not about grand gestures or expensive commitments,” Ortiz added. “It’s about showing that a single planter box with the right flowers can be a rest stop for a migrating butterfly. A patch of clover in a lawn can feed a bumblebee. By walking this path, we hope visitors will leave with the confidence and knowledge to make those small changes—and to share them with neighbors.”

The Pollinator Path is open during regular garden hours: daily from 8:00 a.m. to sunset. Admission to the garden includes access to the new trail. No reservations are required for individual visits, though groups of 10 or more are encouraged to call ahead to coordinate volunteer-led walks. The garden is located at 1200 Botanical Lane, Harborleaf, CA.

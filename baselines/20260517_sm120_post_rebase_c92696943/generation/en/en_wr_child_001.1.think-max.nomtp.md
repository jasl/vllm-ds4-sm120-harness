The wind scooped her up, and it was glorious. The garden became a patchwork quilt of greens and browns. "I'm flying!" she sang.

But the wind had a tricky sense of humor. It swirled. The garden spun. Pip looked for the ground, but it was rushing up to meet her far too fast. She didn't know how to slow down. She didn't know how to say hello to the earth.

*SPLAT.*

She skidded into a muddy puddle. She wasn't hurt, just dizzy and covered in brown specks. Seymour inched over. "You flew beautifully. The start was perfect."

Pip spat out a bit of leaf. "That was the end. It was terrible."

"That was a crash," Seymour said gently. "A landing is a different thing entirely. A landing is a choice. It takes bravery to choose the ground."

"Bravery is UP," Pip insisted, shaking mud from her wing. "Bravery is GOING."

"Stopping takes patience," Seymour said. "And patience can be a very quiet kind of bravery."

He pointed a slimy antenna at his map. "Look. See how this puddle is smaller than yesterday? The water is a traveler too. The sun warms it gently, and it becomes invisible, joining the wind. Everything is connected to the air."

Pip stared. "The puddle is flying away?"

"Slowly," Seymour smiled. "And patiently. A good flyer learns from a puddle. You must learn to read the wind. Watch the puddle's surface. If it is rippled and messy, the wind is gusty and grumpy. If it is smooth and still, the wind is holding its breath. That is the moment for a brave landing."

Pip's chest quivered. She saw the puddle Seymour called "The Brave Landing." It was a perfect oval, drying at the edges like a golden frame.

"I want to try again," she whispered.

She flew to a low branch. She wanted to dive right now! But the wind was gusty. A leaf skittered across the puddle, leaving ripples.

"Wait," Seymour said.

Waiting was the hardest thing Pip had ever done. Her muscles ached to move. Her heart pounded. It felt like doing nothing. It felt like being a coward.

"I can't wait," she trembled. "I have to go!"

"Why?" Seymour asked gently. "Is running away from the stillness brave? Or is sitting in it, listening, the braver thing?"

Pip closed her eyes. She felt the wind on her back. She felt her own heart beating. The wind gusted, then sighed. Then… it stopped.

She opened her eyes. The puddle was a perfect mirror of the blue sky. The leaf was still.

"Now," Seymour whispered.

Pip didn't jump wildly. She pushed off gently. She flew straight. She remembered the puddle's water becoming an invisible traveler. She didn't fight the air. She trusted it.

The puddle grew larger and larger. She remembered to flare her wings. She reached out her tiny toes. It was the opposite of a crash. She touched the water just as the first new breeze touched the edge. The puddle barely shivered.

It was the softest landing in the history of the garden.

She stood in the shallow water, catching her breath. "I did it!"

"You did," smiled Seymour, reaching the edge.

"The waiting was the scariest part," she said. "My legs wanted to jump, but I made them wait. Was that bravery?"

"The very best kind," said Seymour. "Patience is the wind beneath a brave landing."

The sun was high, and the puddle was shrinking. Pip looked at Seymour's map. "Will you draw my puddle on your map?"

"Every brave landing deserves to be remembered," said Seymour.

Pip looked up at the sky, then down at the puddle. She knew now that the sky didn't end in the clouds. It ended on the ground. And meeting the ground with patience was the bravest part of the journey.

She wasn't just a flyer anymore. She was a lander, too. And that was the best kind of bird to be.

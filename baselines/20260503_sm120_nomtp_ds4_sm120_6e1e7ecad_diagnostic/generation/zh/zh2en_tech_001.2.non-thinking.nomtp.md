**Making Bamboo Paper**
Bamboo paper is made in the south, especially in Fujian province. When bamboo shoots have grown, check the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is just about to sprout leaves is the best material. On the same mountain, dig a pond and fill it with water to soak the bamboo. To keep the pond from drying up, use bamboo pipes to channel in a constant stream of water. Soak for over a hundred days. Then beat and wash to remove the coarse shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green," 杀青). The inner fiber now looks like ramie. Mix high-quality lime into a paste, coat the bamboo with it, and put it into a wooden vat (楻桶) for cooking. Cook for eight days and eight nights.

For cooking bamboo, use a cauldron about four *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. Build up a rim of clay and lime around the cauldron, making it tall and wide like the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong; it can hold over ten *dan* (~500 kg) of water. Cover with a wooden vat (楻桶), about 1.5 *zhang* (5 m) in circumference and over four *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. After sealing, cook for exactly eight days. Let it rest for one day. Then open the vat and take out the bamboo fiber (竹麻). Wash it in a clean soaking pond. The bottom and sides of this pond must be lined with tightly joined planks to prevent mud contamination (skip this for coarse paper). After washing, treat the fiber with ash lye (柴灰浆). Put it back into a cauldron, spread it flat, and cover with a half-inch layer of rice straw ash. When the water in the cauldron boils, transfer the fiber to another vat and pour the ash lye over it again. If the liquid cools, reheat it and pour again. Repeat for about ten days, until the fiber naturally rots and softens. Then take it out and pound it in a mortar (mountain areas use water-powered trip-hammers, 水碓). Pound until it becomes like mud. Pour the pulp into a vat.

**The Papermaking Vat and Mold**
The papermaking vat (抄纸槽) has a square top. Its width depends on the mold (帘), and the mold's size depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water about three inches above the pulp. Add papermaking drug liquid (纸药水汁, made from leaves like peach or bamboo; local names vary). This makes the water drain away cleanly, leaving white paper. The mold (抄纸帘) is made from very finely scraped bamboo strips woven together. It is stretched over a frame with crossbars. Hold the mold with both hands and dip it into the vat, scooping up the pulp. The thickness of the sheet depends on the operator's skill: a light dip makes thin paper, a heavy dip makes thick paper. As the pulp floats on the mold, water drains through the sides back into the vat. Then turn the mold over to deposit the wet sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. When the pile is thick enough, place a board on top, tighten ropes and insert levers (like pressing wine) to squeeze out all the water. Then, using fine copper tweezers, lift each sheet one by one and dry it over heat.

**Drying the Paper**
To dry paper, build a narrow chamber of earth bricks. The floor is paved with bricks, leaving one brick gap every few bricks. Light a fire at one end; the heat passes through the gaps and warms the whole brick chamber. Paste the wet sheets onto the hot bricks, let them dry, then peel them off into stacks.

**Later Variants**
In recent times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" (大四连) became valued for writing. Waste paper (with ink and vermilion cleaned off) can be soaked, rotted, and re-pulped, skipping the initial cooking and soaking steps, to make new paper with little loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is often ignored. In the north, even small scraps are collected and remade into "returned-soul paper" (还魂纸). Bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, all follow similar methods. For fire paper (火纸, spirit money) and rough paper, the steps are the same—cutting bamboo, cooking the fiber, applying ash lye—but after lifting from the mold, they are not heated; they are just pressed and sun-dried.

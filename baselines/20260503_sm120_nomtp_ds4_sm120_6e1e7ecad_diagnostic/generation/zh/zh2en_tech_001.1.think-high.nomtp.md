**Making Bamboo Paper**

This craft originates in the south, but Fujian province is especially famous for it. After bamboo shoots appear, one assesses the depth of the mountain hollows. Bamboo that is about to grow branches and leaves is the best material. Around the **Mangzhong** (Grain in Ear, ~early June) solar term, go into the mountains to cut the bamboo. Chop it into sections 5 to 7 *chi* (approx. 1.6–2.3 meters) long. Right there on the mountain, dig a pit, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo. To prevent the pit from drying out, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. Soak for a hundred days or more. Then, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green" - **sha qing**). The inner bamboo fiber will then resemble ramie fiber in appearance. Make a solution using good-quality lime, coat the bamboo with it, place it in a **huang** (a large wooden vat/tub) and boil it. The fire should be kept going for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling, use a pan roughly 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter. Seal the rim of the pan with a mixture of clay and lime, building it up high and wide, just like the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, so it can hold over 10 *dan* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. Place the *huang* tub on top; it should be about 1.5 *zhang* (approx. 5 meters) in circumference and over 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter. After sealing it, boil for the full eight days. Let it rest, with no fire, for one day. Then, open the *huang* and take out the bamboo pulp (referred to as bamboo hemp). Wash it clean in a clear-water soaking pit. The bottom and all four sides of this washing pit must be made of closely fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, mix the pulp with wood-ash slurry, then put it back into a cauldron. Level the surface and spread a layer of rice straw ash about an inch thick over it. When the water in the cauldron boils, remove the pulp and place it in another tub, continuing to pour the ash-water mixture over it. If the liquid cools, reboil it before pouring again. Repeat this for ten-odd days. The material will naturally rot and disintegrate. Then, take it out and put it into a mortar for pounding (mountain regions have water-powered trip-hammers for this). Pound it until it has the consistency of mud. Finally, pour this mixture into the vat.

**The paper-making vat** is rectangular at the top. Its dimensions depend on the mould (the screen frame), and the mould’s dimensions depend on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about 3 *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. Add a prepared "paper medicine" liquid (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, though the local name varies). This liquid causes the water to drain away, leaving clean, white fibers.

**The papermaking mould** is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is mounted on a rectangular frame for support. The worker holds the mould by both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts it, causing the bamboo pulp to spread evenly on the screen. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by the worker’s technique: a gentle lift makes a thin sheet, a vigorous lift makes a thick one. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains through the sides back into the vat. Then, the mould is turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack reaches the desired height, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed using a rope and lever system, similar to pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water. After pressing, each sheet is lifted from the stack using fine, light copper tweezers and dried.

**Drying the paper** is done as follows: first, build a long, narrow corridor (a drying tunnel) with earthen bricks. On the floor of this corridor, leave a gap every few bricks. Build a fire at one end of the tunnel; the heat and flames travel through the gaps between the bricks, heating the entire brick surface. The wet sheets of paper are pasted one by one onto these hot bricks to dry. Once dry, they are peeled off and collected into quires.

Recently, a wide-format paper called **Da Si Lian** (Big Four-fold) has become highly valued for calligraphy and writing. Waste paper, after having its red ink and other stains washed away, can be soaked, rotted, and put back into the vat for reuse. This process saves all the initial steps of boiling and soaking, and the resulting paper is just as good, with little material lost. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, even a small scrap of paper is picked up from the ground and repulped to make "Returned Soul Paper". Regardless of the fiber source (bamboo or bark) or quality, the principle is the same.

For **fire paper** and coarse paper, the processes of cutting the bamboo, boiling the pulp, applying ash slurry, and leaching are identical. However, after being lifted from the mould, these papers are not baked. They are simply pressed to remove excess water and then dried in the sun.

During the height of the Tang Dynasty, when spiritual/ghost worship was prevalent, paper money was used to replace silk cloth for burning as offerings (in the north, they used cut strips called "board money"). Therefore, paper made for this purpose is called **Fire Paper**. In the modern custom of the Hubei/Hunan region, a single funeral can burn up to a thousand *jin* (catties) of this paper. About 70% of fire paper is used for these spirit offerings, and 30% for daily use. The coarsest and thickest variety is called **Wrapping Paper**, and it is made from a mixture of bamboo pulp and stalks from last year's late rice.

In contrast, the fine letter paper made in places like Yanshan uses only fine bamboo material, made into a thick sheet, to command a high price. The highest grade is called **Official Letter Paper**, used by wealthy and aristocratic families for their name cards. It is thick, firm, and free from fiber lumps. To make red-colored auspicious letter paper, the paper is first treated with an alum solution, and then a solution of safflower dye is applied.

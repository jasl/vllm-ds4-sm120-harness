Making bamboo paper is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots have grown, look deep into the mountain valleys. After soaking for over 100 days, take it out and beat and wash it to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this is called *sha qing*, "killing the green"). The bamboo core material will now look like ramie fiber. Make a paste from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the bamboo fiber with it, and put it into a *huang* bucket (a large, covered wooden vat) to cook. The fire should burn for eight days and eight nights.

For cooking the bamboo, use a cauldron about four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. Build up the rim of the cauldron with a mixture of mud and lime, making it tall and wide, similar in shape to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong; it should hold over ten *dan* (approx. 600 liters) of water. Place the *huang* bucket over it as a cover; the bucket's circumference is 1.5 *zhang* (approx. 5 m) and its diameter is about four *chi*. Cover it and cook. After eight full days, let the fire go out for one day. Then remove the cover and take out the bamboo pulp. Wash it clean in a clear-water soaking pond. The bottom and all four sides of this pond should be sealed with wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). The paper-making vat is square at the top. Its dimensions depend on the mold, and the mold's dimensions depend on the desired paper size. The paper-making mold screen is woven from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips. When opened and spread out, it is supported by a frame of vertical and horizontal bars. When dry, lift them off and assemble them into quires.

In recent times, wide-format paper is called "Big Four-Link" and was valued for writing and official documents.

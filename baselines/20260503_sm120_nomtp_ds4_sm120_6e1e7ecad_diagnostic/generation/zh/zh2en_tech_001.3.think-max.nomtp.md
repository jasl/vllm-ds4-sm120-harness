**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking comes from the south, and Fujian province is especially famous for it. After the bamboo shoots grow, one examines the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is *about to* grow branches and leaves is the best material. Around the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), they go up the mountain to cut it down. They cut the bamboo into pieces five to seven *chi* (roughly 1.7–2.3 meters) long. Right there on the mountain, they dig a pond, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo. In case the pond water dries up, they use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, they work to beat and wash the bamboo, removing the coarse outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The remaining bamboo fiber looks like ramie. They mix quicklime into a paste, coat the bamboo with it, and put it into a large vat to boil. The fire is kept going for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling, they use a cauldron four *chi* (about 1.3 meters) in diameter. The rim of the cauldron is sealed with a mixture of clay and lime, making a rim as high and wide as the salt pans used in Guangdong, holding over ten *dan* (roughly 1000 liters) of water. A large wooden vat (the *huang tong*) is placed on top; it is fifteen *chi* (5 meters) in circumference and over four *chi* (1.3 meters) in diameter. After sealing and cooking for a full eight days, the fire is stopped for one day. Then the vat is opened, and the boiled bamboo pulp (called "bamboo hemp") is taken out and washed clean in a clean pond. The bottom and sides of this washing pond must be made of tightly fitted planks to prevent mud from dirtying the pulp (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is treated with a wood-ash lye and put back into a cauldron. The pulp is leveled, and a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (3.2 cm) thick is spread evenly on top. When the water in the cauldron boils, the liquid is ladled out and poured back over the pulp in another vat. If the liquid cools, it is reheated before pouring. This is repeated for over ten days, until the pulp naturally rots. Then it is taken out and beaten in a mortar (in mountainous areas, they use water-powered trip-hammers). It is beaten until it is as fine as mud, then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat is a square box. Its width is determined by the mold, and the mold's size is determined by the paper size. After the bamboo pulp is ready, clear water is added to the vat until it stands about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. A "paper medicine" liquid is added (the plant used looks like peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name). This makes the pulp sheets drain and separate cleanly when dry. The paper mold is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven into a mat. It is mounted on a rectangular frame that can be opened and rolled. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts some bamboo pulp, letting it settle on the mold's surface. The thickness of the sheet depends on the worker’s skill: a gentle lift makes thin paper; a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the bamboo pulp floats on the mold, water drains back into the vat from all sides. Then the mold is turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is high enough, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever and rope, like pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water. After this, each sheet is lifted one by one with fine copper tongs and dried on a heated wall. For drying, a narrow chamber is built from brick, with a fire chamber on the ground. Several bricks are left out of the floor to create vents. A fire is lit in the fire chamber, and the heat passes through the brick vents, heating the entire inner wall. Wet sheets are pasted onto the hot wall to dry, then lifted off as finished sheets.

In recent times, a wide format called "Big Four-Linked" (Da Si Lian) was valued for writing and calligraphy. Waste paper, after having its ink and colors washed off, is soaked and broken down, then put back into the vat. This saves all the steps of soaking and boiling, yet still makes paper with little loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. In the north, even a tiny scrap of paper on the ground is picked up and reused to make "soul-returning paper." Bamboo, bark – coarse or fine – can all be used this way. For spirit paper ("fire paper") and rough paper, the process is the same: cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, treating with ash lye, and washing. However, after the sheet is formed and taken off the mold, it is not dried on a hot wall. Instead, water is pressed out, and it is sun-dried.

In the High Tang period, spiritual practices were common. Paper money replaced silk as an offering (in the north, they used cut strips called "board money"). So this type was called "fire paper." In the modern custom of the Chu region (Hubei/Hunan), a single burning can use up to a thousand *jin* (600 kg). About 70% of this paper is for ghost offerings; 30% is for daily use. The coarsest and thickest type is called wrapping paper, made from bamboo pulp mixed with old rice straw from late fields. Paper like the letter paper made in Yanshan County is made entirely from fine bamboo material, formed thickly, and sold at a high price. The highest grade is called "official letter paper." Wealthy families use it for their name cards. It is thick and solid, with no fiber clumps. For red-dyed paper used for joyful occasions, the paper is first soaked in alum water, then coated with safflower juice.

Making bark paper uses the same basic equipment and many of the same steps as bamboo papermaking. The key difference is the raw material: the inner bark of trees, such as the mulberry tree or the wingceltis (known as *qingtan* for its tough fibers). The bark is stripped, soaked, boiled with lime, washed, beaten, and formed into sheets using the same vat-and-mold method.

The best quality bark paper, used for high-grade calligraphy and printing, is silky and durable. A thinner type, called "patched paper" (*fengzhi*), involves combining two thin sheets while still wet to form a single, stronger sheet. The finest of all is *chengxin tang* paper, praised for its smoothness and luster. These fine papers are often sized with a thin glue made from plant juice to make them less absorbent for ink.

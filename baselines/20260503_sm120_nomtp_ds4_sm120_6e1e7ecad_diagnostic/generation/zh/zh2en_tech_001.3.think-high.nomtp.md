After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the coarse outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). A solution made from high-quality lime is then mixed into the fibers, which are placed into a vat (called a *huang* bucket) and boiled over a fire for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron with a diameter of four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) is used. The rim of the cauldron is built up with a mixture of mud and lime, making it tall and wide, similar in size to the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong, capable of holding over ten *dan* of water (a *dan* is ~100 liters). The *huang* bucket, which is placed on top, has a circumference of fifteen *chi* (approx. 5 meters) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters). When the water in the cauldron boils, the liquid is ladled out and poured over the fibers in another container. The rotten fibers are then taken out and placed in a mortar for pounding (in mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers are used). Pounding continues until the material becomes as fine as mud. The papermaking vat is rectangular, half open at the top. Its width and length match the mold (*lian* – the bamboo screen frame), and the mold's size matches the desired paper sheet. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to a depth of about three inches above the pulp. The papermaking mold is made of very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. When unfolded for use, it is supported by a frame underneath. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by hand technique: a gentle lift produces thin paper; a vigorous lift produces thick paper. While the bamboo pulp is floating on the screen, water drains back into the vat from all edges. Then the mold is turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a pile on a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the pile reaches the desired height, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever-and-rope system, like pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water until the stack is dry. Then, the sheets are lifted one by one with light, fine copper tweezers and dried on a heated wall.

For drying the paper, a double-walled chamber is built from brick, with a fire passage in the floor. Every few bricks, one brick is left out to create vents. A fire is lit at one end of the passage; the heat flows through the vents, warming the outer wall of the chamber. Damp sheets are pasted onto the hot wall to dry, then removed and gathered into quires (folded bundles).

In recent times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Lian" (a large sheet size) has been highly valued for calligraphy and writing.

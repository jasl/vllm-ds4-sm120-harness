**Making Bamboo Paper**
(from *Tiangong Kaiwu*)

Bamboo paper-making is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially famous for it. After bamboo shoots grow, look for bamboo in deep mountain valleys. The best material is bamboo that is just about to sprout leaves. Around the time of *Mangzhong* (Grain in Ear, early June), go up the mountain and cut it down. Chop the stems into sections five to seven *chi* (about 1.7–2.3 meters) long. On the mountain itself, dig a pit and fill it with water to soak the bamboo. If the pit might dry out, use bamboo pipes to keep fresh water flowing in. Soak for over a hundred days, then pound and wash off the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called *shaqing*, "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber then looks like ramie. Mix high-quality lime into a paste, coat the bamboo with it, and put it into a *huangtong* (a tall wooden boiling vat). Boil for eight days and eight nights.

The boiling setup: use a cauldron about four *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. Seal its rim with a mixture of clay and lime, making the raised edge as wide and high as the salt-boiling pans used in Guangxi. The cauldron can hold over ten *dan* (about 500–600 liters) of water. Place the *huangtong* over it; the vat is 15 *chi* (5 m) in circumference and over four *chi* (1.3 m) wide. Cover and boil for a full eight days. Let it rest for one day without fire. Then open the vat, take out the bamboo fiber (*zhuma*), and wash it in a clean soaking pit. The pit’s floor and sides must be lined with tightly fitted wooden boards to prevent mud from getting in (not necessary for coarse paper). After washing, mix the fiber with wood-ash lye and put it back into a cauldron. Spread it flat and cover with a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (3.3 cm) thick. When the water in the cauldron boils, transfer the fiber to another vat and pour the ash lye over it again. If the liquid cools, reboil and pour again. Repeat this for over ten days, until the fiber naturally rots and softens. Then take it out, put it into a mortar, and pound it (in mountainous areas, water-powered trip-hammers (*shuidui*) are used). Pound until the fiber becomes smooth and mud-like, then pour it into a vat.

**The paper-making vat**: it is square at the top. Its size depends on the mold (*lian*, a sieve-like frame), and the mold’s size depends on the paper’s desired dimensions. When the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. Add a "paper drug" (*zhiyao*) liquid (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves; local names vary). This makes the pulp drain quickly and form white paper. The mold is made of very fine, smooth bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is stretched over a supporting frame with horizontal and vertical bars. Holding the mold in both hands, dip it into the vat and scoop up bamboo pulp, spreading it evenly on the screen. The paper’s thickness depends on the operator’s skill—a light shake gives thin paper, a heavy one gives thick paper. As the pulp floats on the screen, water drains through the gaps back into the vat. Then turn the mold over and lay the wet sheet onto a board. Stack up thousands of sheets. When enough sheets are stacked, press them under a board, tighten ropes with wedges (like pressing wine) to squeeze out all water. Then lift each sheet with fine brass tweezers and dry it over heat.

**Drying**: build a long narrow chamber with brick walls. Cover the floor with bricks, leaving every few bricks open as vents. Light a fire at one end of the chamber; heat and smoke rise through the gaps between the bricks, heating the outer brick wall evenly. Paste each wet sheet onto the hot brick wall to dry, then peel it off as a finished sheet.

Modern wide-format paper (called *daliàn*) is prized for writing. Waste paper—stained with red ink or dirt—can be soaked, rotted, and re-pulped, skipping the earlier boiling and soaking steps, to make new paper with little loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not common. In the north, even small scraps are collected and remade into "soul-returning paper" (*huanhunzhi*). The same process applies to both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse.

**Special types**: *Huozhi* (fire paper) and *caozhi* (coarse paper) follow the same steps of cutting bamboo, boiling fiber, and using ash lye, but after removing from the mold, they are not heat-dried—just pressed and sun-dried. About 70% of fire paper is used for offerings; 30% for daily purposes. The coarsest, thickest paper (wrapping paper) is made by mixing bamboo pulp with old late-rice straw. For red auspicious notepaper, the sheets are first dipped in alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

**Manufacturing Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo paper-making is practiced in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one inspects the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to sprout leaves is considered the best material. Around the Grain-in-Ear solar term (early June), the bamboo is cut down on the mountain, chopped into pieces 5 to 7 *chi* long (approx. 1.7–2.3 m). A pit is dug right there on the mountain, filled with water, and the bamboo is soaked in it. To prevent the pit from drying out, bamboo pipes are used to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called *sha qing* – "killing the green"). The inner fiber, now resembling ramie, is mixed into a paste with high-quality lime dissolved in water and placed in a *huang* vat (a large covered pot) to boil. The boiling lasts for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron with a diameter of 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) is used. The rim of the cauldron is sealed with clay and lime, forming a high, wide edge similar to a salt-boiling pan used in Guangdong, and it can hold over ten *dan* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. The *huang* vat, which covers the cauldron, has a circumference of 15 *chi* (approx. 5 m) and a diameter of 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 m). Once covered, the contents are boiled for a full eight days. After resting for one day, the vat cover is removed, and the bamboo pulp (called *zhu ma* – "bamboo hemp") is taken out and washed in a clean soaking pit. This pit—its bottom and all four sides—must be lined with tightly fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is mixed with wood-ash slurry and placed back into a cauldron, leveled, and covered with a layer of rice-straw ash about 1 *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick. When the water in the cauldron boils, the pulp is transferred to another vat, where the ash-water is poured over it. If the liquid cools, it is reboiled and poured again. After about ten days of this process, the pulp naturally rots and becomes soft. It is then taken out and beaten in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers for this) until it resembles mud, and then poured into a vat.

The paper-making vat has a square upper frame. Its width matches the size of the *lian* (bamboo screen), and the screen matches the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, clean water is added to the vat until it stands about 3 *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. Then paper-making drug liquid (made from a leaf resembling peach or bamboo leaves; local names vary) is mixed in, which causes the water to drain away and the fibers to form clean white sheets. The *lian* (paper-forming screen) is woven from extremely fine, polished bamboo strips. It is mounted on a rectangular frame that can be rolled open and closed. The worker holds the screen by both ends and dips it into the vat, scooping up bamboo fibers onto the screen. The thickness of the sheet depends on the hand motion: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains through its mesh back into the vat. The screen is then turned over to deposit the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When enough sheets have been accumulated, a board is placed on top, and a rope is tightened with a lever (like pressing wine) to squeeze out all the remaining water. After pressing, the sheets are individually lifted with fine copper tweezers and dried over heat.

For drying paper, a low chamber with brick walls is built. The floor inside is paved with bricks, leaving gaps between some bricks. A fire is lit in a tunnel at one end; hot smoke and air pass through the brick gaps into the chamber, heating the brick walls. Wet sheets are pasted onto the hot walls, dried, and then removed as finished sheets.

In recent times, wide sheets called "Big Four-Linked" (*da si lian*) have been valued for writing and calligraphy. Waste paper—after washing off ink and vermilion stains—can be soaked, rotted, and re-processed in the vat. This method saves all the previous steps of boiling and soaking and still produces paper with little loss of material. In southern regions where bamboo is cheap, this is not commonly practiced. In the north, however, even scraps and bits are collected for remaking into "Returned-Soul Paper" (*huan hun zhi*). Both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, can be recycled this way.

As for *huo zhi* (spirit paper) and coarse paper, the steps of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and applying ash-water are the same as above. The difference is that after the sheet is formed and pressed, it is not dried by heat but simply sun-dried.

During the High Tang period (8th century), when religious and ancestral rites flourished, paper money replaced burned silk cloth as offerings (in the north, cut strips called *ban qian* were used). Paper made for this purpose is called *huo zhi* ("fire paper"). In the Jiang-Huai region (modern Hubei/Hunan), a single ceremony sometimes burns up to 1,000 *jin* (approx. 600 kg). About 70% of this paper is used for burning as offerings; the rest is for daily use. The coarsest and thickest type, called *bao guo zhi* (wrapping paper), is made from bamboo pulp mixed with old rice straw from late-season fields. As for the note-paper (*jian zhi*) made in Yanshan and other counties, it is made entirely from fine bamboo material, formed thick by scooping, and is sold at high prices. The finest grade is called *guan jian* (official note-paper), used by wealthy families for name cards and letters. It is firm, thick, and free of fiber clumps. For red-colored auspicious note-paper, the sheets are first treated with alum water and then dyed with safflower juice.

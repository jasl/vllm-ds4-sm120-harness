**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” to Inspire Neighborhood-Wide Habitat Connections**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 1, 2026** – The Harborleaf Botanical Garden will officially open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on **Sunday, July 12, 2026**, transforming a 0.8-mile loop in the east meadow into an immersive, accessible learning experience focused on the needs of bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. Designed in direct response to visitor requests for practical, actionable planting guidance, the trail features native-plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage, and shaded resting points.

The Pollinator Path occupies the east meadow loop, a paved route suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and visitors of all mobility levels. It is intended to serve as a living classroom that demonstrates how small, intentional plant choices in a home garden, balcony, or community plot can create linked corridors of habitat across urban and suburban neighborhoods.

Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden, emphasized the trail’s core educational mission.

“We hear from visitors regularly that they want to help pollinators but don’t know where to start—what to plant, when to plant, or which flowers support specific species through different seasons,” Ortiz said. “The Pollinator Path is designed to show, in a very concrete way, that small planting decisions—a pot of native milkweed on an apartment balcony, a patch of goldenrod in a front yard—can add up to create connected habitats that stretch across entire neighborhoods.”

The trail includes multiple demonstration beds planted exclusively with California-native species known to support local pollinators. Stop signs along the route identify host plants for caterpillars, nectar sources for adult butterflies and bees, and larval food plants for moths. A dedicated night-blooming flower station will be illuminated during evening hours to showcase plants such as evening primrose and moonflower, which attract nocturnal pollinators including certain moth species and bats.

Interpretive content is presented through tactile signage, with raised text, braille, and embossed illustrations of key plant and insect species. Youth observation cards—available free at the trailhead—guide younger visitors through a scavenger-hunt-style activity, prompting them to note which pollinators they see at each station. Six shaded resting points, each equipped with a small bench and a shade sail, offer respite along the loop and double as quiet observation spots for photography or sketching.

A volunteer-led weekend walk will launch on the opening day and continue every Saturday and Sunday through the end of the growing season. These guided tours, led by trained community educators, will provide in-depth explanations of plant-pollinator relationships, seasonal bloom cycles, and simple garden modifications such as leaving bare soil for ground-nesting bees or avoiding pesticide use.

The Pollinator Path was developed following a visitor survey conducted by the garden in 2024, in which a majority of respondents expressed interest in learning more about “practical, low-cost ways to support pollinators at home.” The garden’s horticulture team curated the plant list based on regional ecological data from local native-plant societies and academic research on urban pollinator movement.

The trail also features a small “seed swap station” near the trailhead, where visitors can leave and take seeds of native pollinator-friendly plants in exchange. The station is stocked with species that are easy to grow in containers or small garden plots, reinforcing the trail’s message that habitat creation is accessible to anyone.

The east meadow loop is part of the garden’s existing network of walking paths, but the Pollinator Path represents the first themed educational trail dedicated entirely to pollinator ecology. The garden expects the trail to become a primary draw for school field trips, community garden groups, and individual home gardeners alike.

“If every person who walks this trail chooses just one plant that supports a pollinator, the collective impact across this region could be transformative,” Ortiz added.

The Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a public, nonprofit educational institution that has operated since 1978. It manages more than 40 acres of display gardens, natural habitats, and conservation collections. Admission to the Pollinator Path is included with regular garden entry. The garden is open daily from 9 a.m. to sunset.

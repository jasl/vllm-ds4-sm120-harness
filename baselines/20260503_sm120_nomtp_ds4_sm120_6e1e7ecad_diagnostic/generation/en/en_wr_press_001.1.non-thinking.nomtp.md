**Harborleaf Botanical Garden To Open “Pollinator Path” Educational Trail on July 12**

DATELINE: HARBORLEAF, March 15, 2026 — Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the launch of its new “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile public education trail dedicated to native plants and the insects, birds, and mammals they support. The trail will officially open to visitors on July 12, 2026, along the east meadow loop, and is designed to provide practical, hands-on guidance for anyone looking to create pollinator-friendly spaces in their own yards or communities.

The trail addresses a growing demand from Harborleaf’s visitors for clear, actionable information about planting for local pollinators. According to garden staff, guests have increasingly asked for exhibits that go beyond identification and explain how small-scale planting choices can boost the survival of bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The Pollinator Path is the garden’s response: an immersive, accessible outdoor classroom that blends native plant demonstration beds, sensory stations, and interpretive signage aimed at all ages.

“What we hear most often is that people want to help, but they don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is designed to show that even a single pot of native milkweed, a patch of goldenrod, or a night-blooming moonflower can matter. When neighbors each make one small planting decision, those patches connect. You get corridors of habitat stretching across whole neighborhoods.”

The trail is fully accessible, with a hard-packed surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers. Its east meadow loop location offers gentle terrain and shaded resting points every 200 feet, allowing visitors to move at their own pace while exploring the exhibits.

Key features of the Pollinator Path include:

- **Native Plant Demonstration Beds:** Six distinct garden beds showcase pollinator-friendly species native to the region, including butterfly milkweed, purple coneflower, bee balm, and spicebush. Each bed is labeled with the common and scientific names of plants, as well as the specific pollinators each attracts. Interpretive signs explain bloom times, host plant relationships (such as which plants caterpillars require), and soil moisture needs.

- **Night-Blooming Flower Station:** A dedicated section highlights plants that open after dusk, such as evening primrose, night-blooming jasmine, and the aptly named moonflower vine. The station features low-level amber lighting that does not disrupt nocturnal pollinators, plus signage explaining how moths, bats, and some beetles rely on flowers that release fragrance and nectar only at night.

- **Tactile Signage:** For visitors with visual impairments or those who prefer a hands-on learning experience, raised-text signs and a Braille trail guide are installed at key stops. The tactile signage includes embossed images of leaf shapes, flower structures, and insect silhouettes, enabling visitors to “read” the landscape through touch.

- **Youth Observation Cards:** Free, reusable observation cards are available at the trailhead. Designed for children ages 5 to 12, the cards guide young visitors through simple tasks: counting the number of butterflies seen on a single flower, sketching a bumblebee’s flight path, or identifying which colors attract the most hummingbirds. Completed cards can be exchanged for a small Pollinator Path sticker at the garden’s welcome center.

- **Shaded Resting Points:** Six benches under shade structures are placed along the loop. Each bench is positioned near a demonstration bed or interpretive sign, offering a place to sit, observe, and take notes. The benches are made from recycled materials and are designed to minimize heat absorption.

- **Volunteer-Led Weekend Walks:** Beginning July 12, volunteer docents will lead free guided walks along the Pollinator Path every Saturday and Sunday at 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. Walks last approximately 45 minutes and cover topics such as identifying common pollinators, selecting plants for different light conditions, and designing small-scale habitat patches. No registration is required, and walk-ups are welcome. Docents will be equipped with hand lenses and portable microphone systems for added clarity.

The trail will remain open during regular garden hours: Tuesday through Sunday, 9 a.m. to 6 p.m., through the end of October. Admission to the Pollinator Path is included with general garden entry, and no additional ticket is required. Harborleaf Botanical Garden is located at 2000 Willow Bend Road, Harborleaf, with parking available on-site.

Ortiz emphasized that the project is intended to be nonpolitical and educational, focusing on ecological literacy rather than advocacy. “Our goal is to give people the science and the tools to make informed decisions about their own yards, balconies, or community green spaces,” she said. “The more people understand how bees forage, how moths navigate by moonlight, or how a simple caterpillar host plant can support dozens of butterfly species, the more empowered they will be to act.”

The Pollinator Path will be dedicated in a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony on July 12 at 9:30 a.m., followed by the first volunteer-led walk. The event is open to the public.

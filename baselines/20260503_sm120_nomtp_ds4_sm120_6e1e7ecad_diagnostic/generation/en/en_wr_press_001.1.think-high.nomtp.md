**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New “Pollinator Path” Education Trail, Opening July 12, 2026**

HARBORLEAF, CA — Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the launch of its newest public education initiative: the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated trail designed to teach visitors how to create supportive habitats for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The trail, located on the east meadow loop of the Garden’s property, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

The 0.8-mile accessible trail aims to address a growing demand from visitors seeking practical, actionable guidance on planting for local pollinators. Garden staff developed the Pollinator Path after repeated requests from guests for more concrete information on how to transform home gardens, balconies, and community spaces into sanctuaries for beneficial insects and birds.

The trail features a series of native plant demonstration beds that showcase species specifically selected for their nectar and pollen value. Each bed is accompanied by tactile signage, allowing visitors of all abilities to learn through touch and read detailed descriptions of plant-pollinator relationships. A night-blooming flower station highlights species that support moths and nocturnal pollinators, with interpretive panels explaining the unique ecological roles these creatures play after dark.

Young visitors will receive youth observation cards at the trailhead, encouraging them to record pollinator sightings and plant interactions along the route. The cards are designed to foster curiosity and scientific thinking in children ages 5 to 12. Shaded resting points are spaced throughout the trail, offering benches and informational boards where families can pause and discuss what they have observed.

“For years, our guests have told us they want to help pollinators but don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path transforms that curiosity into confidence. We show that small planting decisions—choosing a native milkweed over an ornamental, or adding a single night-blooming flower near a porch light—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. It’s about showing people that every garden matters.”

Ortiz emphasized that the trail is designed to be nonpolitical and purely educational, focusing on ecological principles rather than advocacy. The Garden’s mission is to provide evidence-based information that visitors can adapt to their own landscapes, regardless of the size or style of their outdoor spaces.

In addition to self-guided exploration, the Pollinator Path will offer a volunteer-led weekend walk each Saturday and Sunday morning from 9:00 a.m. to 10:30 a.m., beginning July 13. These walks, led by trained Garden volunteers, will provide deeper insights into pollinator behavior, plant selection, and seasonal gardening practices. No advance registration is required; participants simply meet at the east meadow loop trailhead.

The trail’s design prioritizes accessibility. The entire 0.8-mile loop is paved and meets current standards for wheelchair and stroller accessibility. Tactile signage includes braille and raised illustrations, ensuring that visitors with visual impairments can engage fully with the content. Audio guides will also be available for loan at the Garden’s visitor center starting on opening day.

The Pollinator Path is part of Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s ongoing commitment to environmental education and public engagement. The Garden has long served as a regional resource for native plant conservation, hosting workshops, school field trips, and research collaborations with local universities. This new trail expands that mission by making pollinator ecology accessible to a broader audience.

According to Garden staff, the east meadow loop was chosen for its existing diversity of wildflowers and its proximity to the Garden’s main pathways. The site already attracted a variety of bees and butterflies, making it a natural laboratory for the demonstration beds. The new installations—including the night-blooming station and tactile signage—were designed to complement the existing landscape without disrupting sensitive habitats.

Ortiz noted that the Pollinator Path is intended to serve as a living classroom. “We want people to walk away with one simple idea: you don’t need a large yard or a master gardener’s certification to make a difference. A pot of native sage on a balcony, a patch of clover in a lawn, a single sunflower beside a fence—these small choices add up,” she said. “When neighbors make those choices together, they create corridors of life that help pollinators thrive.”

The Garden will host a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at 10:00 a.m. on July 12 at the east meadow loop trailhead. The public is invited to attend. Regular Garden admission will apply, and no additional ticket is required to access the Pollinator Path.

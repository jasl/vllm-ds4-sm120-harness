**HARBORLEAF, CA – [Date] –** With a growing number of visitors seeking concrete ways to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds, Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its new “Pollinator Path” educational trail on July 12, 2026. The 0.8-mile, fully accessible loop through the east meadow is designed to transform casual curiosity into practical, actionable knowledge for home gardeners of all ages.

Developed in direct response to visitor feedback, the Pollinator Path moves beyond general appreciation of gardens to provide specific, hands-on guidance on how to create habitat patches that support regional pollinator species. Rather than a passive stroll, the trail functions as an outdoor classroom, equipping guests with the tools and confidence to turn even a small balcony or yard into a waystation for essential insects and birds.

The central feature of the path is a series of native plant demonstration beds, each showcasing species that have evolved alongside the region’s most important pollinators. Interpretive signs identify plants by common and scientific names, bloom periods, and the specific pollinators they serve—such as milkweed for monarch caterpillars, goldenrod for late-season bees, and trumpet vine for hummingbirds. A particularly unique stop is the night-blooming flower station, where visitors can learn about the critical role of moths in nighttime pollination. This station displays evening primrose, four-o’clocks, and moonflowers, accompanied by lighting designed to reveal their subtle beauty without disrupting nocturnal activity.

To ensure the trail is welcoming to all visitors, Harborleaf has installed tactile signage with raised text and botanical illustrations, making the information accessible to guests with visual impairments. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead and online, invite children to record the species they spot, compare flower shapes, and note the time of day they see the most activity. These cards align with the garden’s broader mission to foster environmental literacy from an early age.

The path also incorporates shaded resting points with benches, allowing walkers to pause, observe, and reflect without feeling rushed. Each rest station includes a small informational panel describing how different plant heights and bloom times can create a continuous food source from early spring through late autumn.

Complementing the permanent features will be a volunteer-led weekend walk, offered every Saturday and Sunday at 10 a.m. from July 12 through October 31. Guides will lead small groups along the Pollinator Path, pointing out subtle ecological interactions—such as the specific angle at which a bee lands on a penstemon flower or the precise timing of a hummingbird’s visit to a tubular bloom. These walks are free with general garden admission and are designed to accommodate both novice and experienced gardeners.

Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs for Harborleaf Botanical Garden, emphasized that the Pollinator Path was born from a recurring question from the public: “I want to help pollinators, but what do I actually plant?”

“We kept hearing from visitors who felt overwhelmed by the choices,” said Ortiz. “They wanted more than a list of nice plants; they wanted a clear, practical road map. The Pollinator Path answers that by showing, step by step, that small planting decisions—choosing a native aster over an exotic hybrid, leaving a patch of bare ground for ground-nesting bees, or clustering three of the same species instead of one—can create connected habitats across entire neighborhoods. You don’t need a farm or a large estate to make a real difference. A window box, a balcony pot, or a backyard corner, planted with intention, becomes a stepping stone in a larger network.”

The path is named “Pollinator Path” to underscore that it is not merely a garden exhibit but a journey through the daily lives of the animals that sustain nearly three-quarters of the world’s flowering plants. Ortiz noted that the garden’s staff observed a marked increase in visitor interest in pollinator health following recent regional shifts in weather patterns and available green space. However, she stressed that the trail’s focus remains purely educational and nonpolitical, centered on what individuals can do within their own landscapes.

“Our goal is to empower, not to alarm,” Ortiz added. “We want visitors to leave feeling hopeful and equipped, with a clear next step they can take when they get home.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden will officially open the Pollinator Path at a brief ceremony at 9 a.m. on July 12, 2026, at the east meadow trailhead. The garden’s regular hours are 8 a.m. to sundown, seven days a week. Admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under 12. Harborleaf members and annual pass holders are admitted at no charge.

**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New ‘Pollinator Path’ Trail Focused on Habitat Education**

**HARBORLEAF, MA – June 10, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through native planting. The trail, situated along the east meadow loop, will officially open on July 12, 2026, and is fully accessible to visitors of all mobility levels.

The Pollinator Path emerges in direct response to a growing trend among Harborleaf visitors: a desire for practical, actionable guidance on how to create pollinator-friendly habitats at home. Garden staff have noted a sharp increase in questions about which plants best support local pollinators, how to design gardens that provide year-round resources, and what small-scale changes can make a measurable difference for declining insect and bird populations.

“Our visitors come to us inspired by the beauty of our collections, but they often leave wanting to do more,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “They ask, ‘What can I actually plant in my backyard? What will help the bees and butterflies I see here?’ The Pollinator Path is our answer. It shows that small planting decisions, when multiplied across a neighborhood, can create connected corridors of habitat that sustain entire pollinator communities.”

The trail has been designed as an immersive, self-guided educational experience. Its features include:

- **Native Plant Demonstration Beds:** Eleven distinct planting zones showcase native perennials, shrubs, and grasses that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host plants for key pollinator species. Each bed is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom periods, and the specific pollinators it attracts.
- **Night-Blooming Flower Station:** A dedicated area highlights plants such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jasmine, illustrating the critical role of moths and nocturnal insects in nighttime pollination.
- **Tactile Signage:** All interpretive panels incorporate raised text, botanical illustrations, and textured elements (such as embossed leaf shapes and seed pod replicas) to ensure the trail is fully accessible to visitors with visual impairments.
- **Youth Observation Cards:** Children and families can pick up free, laminated observation cards at the trailhead. The cards prompt young visitors to record pollinator sightings, identify flower shapes, and note the time of day different insects appear.
- **Shaded Resting Points:** Four bench-lined rest areas, shaded by native oak and serviceberry trees, offer quiet spots for reflection and observation. Each rest point includes a small interpretive panel with a “Did You Know?” pollinator fact.
- **Volunteer-Led Weekend Walks:** Starting July 12, trained garden docents will lead free, 45-minute guided walks along the Pollinator Path every Saturday and Sunday at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. through October. Walks cover plant identification, pollinator life cycles, and simple at-home gardening techniques.

The trail’s design emphasizes low-impact, sustainable landscaping. All plants were sourced from regional native nurseries, and the path surface is made of compacted, permeable gravel to reduce runoff. The Garden’s horticulture team worked closely with the extension service of a nearby university to ensure the demonstration beds reflect ecologically sound planting combinations for the local climate zone.

Ortiz noted that the Pollinator Path is not intended as a lecture, but as a starting point for individual action. “You don’t need a large garden or a costly redesign to make a difference,” she said. “A single pot of native milkweed on an apartment balcony can support monarch caterpillars. A patch of wild bergamot in a front yard can feed bumblebees for weeks. The Pollinator Path shows these choices, celebrates them, and gives people the confidence to try.”

The trail opens during a period of increased public awareness about pollinator decline. While Harborleaf Botanical Garden does not engage in advocacy or political campaigns, its educational mission is grounded in the science of ecology. The Garden’s leadership emphasized that the Pollinator Path is a nonpartisan, science-based resource available to all community members.

“Pollinators don’t care about political boundaries,” Ortiz added. “They move across fences, roads, and yards. When we make small, thoughtful planting decisions, we are connecting our landscapes to a larger, living network. That is what this trail is about: connection.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a private, nonprofit institution dedicated to plant conservation, education, and horticultural display. The Garden’s 42-acre campus features themed collections, a greenhouse complex, and a year-round calendar of programs for adults, families, and school groups. The Pollinator Path was funded entirely through the Garden’s general operating budget and existing volunteer programs.

**Opening Day Details**
The Pollinator Path will be accessible during regular Garden hours (9:00 a.m. to 6:00 p.m.) beginning July 12, 2026. Admission to the trail is included with general Garden entry. Youth observation cards and a printable trail map will be available at the welcome center. The weekend volunteer-led walks begin at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. on opening day.

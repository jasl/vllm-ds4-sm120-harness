**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New “Pollinator Path” to Educate Public on Habitat-Friendly Planting**

**PORTLAND, OR – June 2, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden announced today the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” an accessible public education trail designed to provide visitors with practical, hands-on guidance for supporting bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through native plant choices. The trail will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026, at 10:00 a.m.

The Pollinator Path is located along the east meadow loop, a 0.8-mile accessible trail that winds through the garden’s eastern grounds. The route is fully paved and meets Americans with Disabilities Act standards, ensuring visitors of all mobility levels can explore the installation. The trail was developed in direct response to a growing number of visitor inquiries seeking concrete advice on how to transform personal gardens, balconies, and community spaces into productive pollinator habitats.

“Our visitors have repeatedly asked for more than just inspiration—they want clear, actionable steps they can take at home,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. It demonstrates that small planting decisions—choosing a native milkweed over an ornamental, or adding a night-blooming flower near a porch light—can ripple outward to create connected habitats across entire neighborhoods.”

The trail features a series of educational installations designed for both self-guided exploration and group learning. Among the key features are native plant demonstration beds, each labeled with species name, bloom period, and the specific pollinators it attracts. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights plants that support nocturnal moths and bats, with interpretive panels explaining how light pollution affects their foraging patterns.

Tactile signage is installed at every stop, incorporating raised text and botanical illustrations for visitors with visual impairments. Youth observation cards are available at the trailhead, encouraging children and families to record what they see—species of butterflies, types of bees, or the time of day a hummingbird visits. Shaded resting points, constructed from sustainably sourced wood and native stone, are spaced at regular intervals along the path, providing places for quiet reflection or sketching.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will begin on July 18, 2026, and continue every Saturday and Sunday through October. Garden docents will lead small groups along the trail, explaining the ecological relationships on display and answering questions about plant selection, soil preparation, and seasonal maintenance. No prior gardening experience is necessary to participate, and the walks are free with garden admission.

The motivation behind the Pollinator Path stems from a broader shift in public interest observed by Harborleaf staff over the past three years. Surveys conducted at the garden’s main entrance and during its annual Spring Bloom Festival indicated that more than 70 percent of visitors wanted guidance on how to incorporate pollinator-friendly plants into their own landscapes. Many reported feeling overwhelmed by conflicting information found online and expressed a desire for trusted, science-based recommendations in a real-world setting.

“We saw an opportunity to turn our visitors’ curiosity into confidence,” Ortiz said. “Instead of just telling people to ‘plant for pollinators,’ we’re showing them exactly how—with living examples, clear labels, and staff who can answer their specific questions. The Path is meant to be a resource that visitors can revisit throughout the growing season as plants change and new pollinators appear.”

The east meadow loop was chosen for the project because of its existing biodiversity and gentle terrain. The area already supported a variety of native grasses and wildflowers before construction began in early 2026. Garden staff worked with ecologists from Portland State University’s Department of Biology to select demonstration species that are regionally appropriate and low-maintenance, including Oregon grape, showy milkweed, Douglas aster, and evening primrose.

The night-blooming flower station was placed away from the garden’s main lighting to minimize disruption to nocturnal pollinators. Interpretive panels there explain how flowers such as evening primrose and yucca open only at dusk, releasing scent compounds that attract moths. A small mesh-sided observation box allows visitors to see nocturnal visitors without disturbing them.

The Pollinator Path is part of Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s larger mission to promote ecological literacy and community engagement with native habitats. The garden, which spans 45 acres in the Willamette Valley, has operated as a nonprofit educational institution since 1975. Its programs serve more than 50,000 visitors annually, including school groups, master gardeners, and local conservation volunteers.

Admission to the Pollinator Path is included with regular garden entry. Youth observation cards are available at no additional cost at the trailhead kiosk. Volunteer-led walks require no advance registration, though groups of 10 or more are asked to contact the garden’s education office for scheduling.

The July 12 opening will include brief remarks by garden leadership, a ribbon-cutting at the trailhead, and opportunities for visitors to walk the path with staff available to answer questions. Light refreshments will be provided.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, hours of operation, or volunteer opportunities, visit www.harborleafbotanical.org or contact the garden’s media office.

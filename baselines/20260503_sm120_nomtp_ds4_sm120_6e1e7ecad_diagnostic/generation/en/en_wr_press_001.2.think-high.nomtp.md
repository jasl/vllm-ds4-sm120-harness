**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ to Inspire Community-Wide Habitat Connections**

**HARBORLEAF, NY – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the launch of its new “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through thoughtful planting choices. The trail, located on the east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026, offering a 0.8-mile accessible route featuring native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage, and youth observation cards.

Developed in direct response to visitor requests for practical, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly habitats, the Pollinator Path aims to bridge the gap between botanical garden displays and everyday home gardening. The trail’s design emphasizes accessibility and hands-on learning, making it suitable for families, school groups, and individuals of all mobility levels.

“Our visitors have consistently asked for more than just beautiful displays—they want to know what they can actually do in their own yards, balconies, or community spaces to help pollinators thrive,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “Small planting decisions can create connected habitats across neighborhoods. One window box with native milkweed can support a monarch caterpillar, and a cluster of late-blooming asters can fuel a migrating hummingbird. The Pollinator Path shows how those individual actions link together.”

The trail begins at the east meadow loop entrance, winding through a series of curated demonstration beds. Each bed highlights a specific type of native plant—such as goldenrod, bee balm, and wild columbine—alongside interpretive signage that explains the plant’s relationship to particular pollinators. A dedicated night-blooming flower station, featuring moonflowers and evening primrose, will open after sunset on select evenings, allowing visitors to observe moths and nocturnal pollinators in action.

To ensure inclusivity, all signage on the Pollinator Path is tactile and includes Braille and large-print text, as well as raised illustrations of key plants and insects. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, encourage children to document the pollinators they spot, with guided prompts for sketching and note-taking. Shaded resting points, equipped with benches and water stations, are spaced every 200 feet along the route to accommodate visitors who may need breaks.

The path is designed to be self-guided, with interpretive markers at each station. On weekends, Harborleaf will offer volunteer-led walks, led by trained garden docents, that provide deeper insights into pollinator behavior, plant life cycles, and seasonal care tips. The walks are free with garden admission and will run every Saturday and Sunday morning from 10:00 a.m. to 11:30 a.m., beginning July 13.

The east meadow loop itself has been under restoration for two years, with a focus on replacing non-native grasses with a mix of wildflowers and shrubs that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host plants. The Pollinator Path occupies the northern half of the loop, leaving the southern section open for passive recreation and wildlife observation. According to Harborleaf staff, the entire trail is wheelchair- and stroller-friendly, with a compacted gravel surface and gentle slopes.

The impetus for the trail came from a survey conducted by Harborleaf in late 2025, in which nearly 60 percent of respondents indicated they wanted more information on how to create pollinator-friendly spaces at home. Many cited confusion over which plants to choose, when to plant, and how to avoid harming beneficial insects. The Pollinator Path aims to address these questions directly, with signage that explains topics such as pesticide-free gardening, the importance of bloom succession, and the role of dead wood and leaf litter as overwintering habitat.

“This is not about grand gestures,” Ortiz added. “It’s about recognizing that a suburban lawn, a school garden bed, or a community park can all be part of a larger network. Pollinators don’t see property lines. When we plant for them, we build corridors of survival.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is also distributing free take-home guides at the trailhead, which list recommended native plants for the region, planting timelines, and maintenance tips. The guides are available in English and Spanish.

The Pollinator Path opens on July 12, 2026, at 9:00 a.m. with a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at the east meadow loop entrance. Garden staff will be on hand to answer questions, and the first 50 visitors will receive a complimentary packet of native wildflower seeds. The trail will remain open daily from dawn to dusk, year-round, with no additional fee beyond regular garden admission.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is located at 742 Meadow Lane, Harborleaf, NY. For trail maps, accessibility information, and the full schedule of volunteer-led walks, visitors are encouraged to visit the garden’s website or contact the education department directly.

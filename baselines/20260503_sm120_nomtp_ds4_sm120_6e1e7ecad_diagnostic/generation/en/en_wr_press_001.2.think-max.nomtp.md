**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New “Pollinator Path” Education Trail, Opening July 12**

HARBORLEAF CITY, STATE – June 16, 2026 – Harborleaf Botanical Garden has announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through practical, residential-scale planting. The accessible trail, located in the garden’s east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

Developed in direct response to visitor inquiries, the Pollinator Path offers concrete, hands-on guidance for gardeners and nature enthusiasts who want to create habitat-friendly landscapes at home. The trail features native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, youth observation cards, shaded resting points, and a volunteer-led weekend walk program.

“Our visitors have consistently asked for more practical advice on what to plant and how to arrange their gardens to actually attract and support pollinators,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer to that request. It shows that small planting decisions—choosing a native milkweed over an exotic ornamental, or clustering nectar-rich flowers in a sunny spot—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods and strengthen our local ecosystem.”

The trail begins at the east meadow entrance, where a clear map and interpretive panels welcome visitors. The first section, a series of native plant demonstration beds, highlights species that provide food and shelter for specific pollinators. Oregon grape and ceanothus are featured for early-season bees, while showy goldenrod and Douglas aster illustrate late-season forage. Each bed is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom periods, and the specific pollinator species that depend on them.

Approximately halfway through the loop, the night-blooming flower station showcases pale-colored, strongly fragrant blossoms—such as evening primrose, Yucca, and night-blooming jessamine—that open after dusk to attract moths and bats. This area is intentionally placed away from general lighting to allow natural nocturnal activity, with low-level path lighting provided for visitor safety. Tactile signage, including raised text and botanical illustrations, ensures that visually impaired visitors can independently explore the station’s features.

Interactive learning is a central element of the trail. At the trailhead, families can pick up free youth observation cards that guide children through activities such as counting the number of different flower colors, identifying a bee’s pollen basket, or sketching a moth’s wing pattern. Completed cards can be returned at the garden’s visitor center for a small Pollinator Path sticker. The cards were developed in consultation with local elementary educators to align with third- through fifth-grade science standards.

Throughout the trail, six shaded resting points provide benches and interpretive panels that explain broader ecological concepts, such as the difference between generalist and specialist pollinators, the importance of host plants for butterfly larvae, and how to avoid common pesticide pitfalls. Each resting point is oriented for a clear view of a demonstration bed or natural meadow edge, encouraging visitors to sit, observe, and reflect.

To extend the learning beyond the trail’s opening day, Harborleaf has established a weekend volunteer-led walk program. Every Saturday and Sunday from 10 a.m. to 11 a.m., trained docents will lead small groups along the Pollinator Path, answering questions and demonstrating simple planting techniques. The walks are free with garden admission and require no advance registration. Garden staff anticipate that the program will evolve based on participant feedback, potentially adding seasonal themes such as “spring emergence” or “fall seed dispersal.”

The trail itself is fully accessible, with a compacted gravel surface that accommodates wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility devices. Grade changes are gentle, and all signage is posted at both standing and seated heights. The loop is designed to be completed in 30 to 45 minutes at a leisurely pace, though visitors are encouraged to linger at demonstration beds or observation stations.

Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is an educational resource, not a policy statement. “We are not telling people what to do with their entire yard,” she said. “We are showing them what works, based on decades of horticultural research and observation at this garden. If someone walks away from the Pollinator Path and decides to plant one pot of native phacelia on their balcony, that is a success. Over time, those small decisions add up to real habitat connectivity across the region.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, founded in 1932, is a nonprofit living museum dedicated to plant conservation, environmental education, and public enjoyment of the natural world. The garden receives no general fund support from the city or state and relies on admission fees, memberships, and donations to operate. The Pollinator Path was funded through the garden’s annual education budget and a dedicated capital improvement fund.

The official opening ceremony on July 12 will include brief remarks from Ortiz and garden leadership, followed by a ribbon-cutting at the trailhead. The first 50 visitors to complete the trail and return a youth observation card will receive a complimentary packet of native wildflower seeds. The trail will be open daily from 9 a.m. to dusk, with the volunteer walks beginning the following weekend.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including a full list of plant species featured on the trail and a downloadable youth observation card template, visit harborleafbotanical.org/pollinatorpath.

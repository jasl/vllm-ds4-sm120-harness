**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens Pollinator Path, a New Educational Trail for Wildlife-Friendly Gardening**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on July 12, 2026, offering visitors a dedicated outdoor classroom focused on the role of native plants in supporting bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile accessible loop in the east meadow is designed to provide practical, hands-on guidance for home gardeners seeking to create habitat for local pollinators.

The trail opens in response to growing numbers of visitors who have asked the garden for more specific, actionable advice on how to landscape for pollinators. Garden staff observed that while general interest in pollinator conservation is high, many people want step-by-step demonstrations of which plants to choose and how to arrange them for maximum benefit. The Pollinator Path aims to bridge that gap by showcasing real-world installations that visitors can replicate at home.

“We hear from so many people who want to help, but they aren’t sure where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that small, intentional planting decisions—like choosing a single native milkweed variety or adding a night-blooming flower near a porch light—can create connected patches of habitat that stretch across neighborhoods. You don’t need a vast property to make a difference.”

The trail features several distinct experiential stations. Native plant demonstration beds are planted with regionally appropriate species known to attract a range of pollinators, including showy goldenrod, purple coneflower, butterfly weed, and bee balm. Each bed is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom periods, and the specific pollinators each plant supports. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights species such as evening primrose and moonflower, which open at dusk to attract moths and night-flying insects.

To ensure accessibility for visitors with visual impairments, the trail incorporates tactile signage with raised text and embossed plant illustrations. Braille labels are installed at each station, and interpretive panels include high-contrast graphics and simple language suitable for all ages. The trail surface is paved and graded for wheelchair users and strollers, with shaded resting points placed at regular intervals along the loop.

Youth engagement is a core component of the trail experience. Harborleaf has produced reusable youth observation cards that children can pick up at the trailhead. The cards prompt young visitors to note specific details—such as the color of a flower, the number of bees on a bloom, or the shape of a moth’s wings—and include a simple checklist for identifying common pollinators. Garden educators hope the activity encourages families to spend more time observing and discussing the natural world.

On weekends, the garden will offer volunteer-led walks along the Pollinator Path. These free guided tours, led by trained docents, will cover topics such as seasonal bloom cycles, pollinator life cycles, and low-maintenance garden design strategies. The walks are scheduled for Saturday and Sunday mornings through the end of September, with additional evening walks during the peak night-blooming season.

The Pollinator Path is part of Harborleaf’s broader commitment to public environmental education. The garden provides year-round programming for school groups, community organizations, and individual visitors. According to garden leadership, the new trail aligns with a national trend of botanical gardens emphasizing applied ecology over purely ornamental displays.

“The response from our community has been tremendous,” Ortiz added. “We’ve seen that once people understand how a single garden bed connects to the next garden down the street, they become more invested in making their own yards welcoming to pollinators. That cumulative effect is powerful.”

The trail opening falls during peak summer bloom periods for many native plants, offering visitors immediate examples of pollinator activity. Harborleaf encourages visitors to bring water, sun protection, and comfortable walking shoes. The east meadow loop is open during regular garden hours, from 8 a.m. to sunset daily. Admission is included with general garden entry; no additional registration is required for self-guided visits. Weekend walks are open to the public on a first-come, first-served basis.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including a downloadable trail map and a list of plant species featured on the demonstration beds, visit the garden’s website or contact the media office.

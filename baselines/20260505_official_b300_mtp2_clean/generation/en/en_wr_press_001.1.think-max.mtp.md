**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ to Educate Public on Habitat Creation**

**HARBORLEAF, PA – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the official opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through small, intentional planting choices. The 0.8-mile accessible trail, located on the garden’s east meadow loop, opens to the public on July 12, 2026.

The trail addresses a growing demand from Harborleaf visitors for practical, actionable guidance on how to create pollinator-friendly environments in their own yards. Garden staff developed the trail after receiving repeated requests for more detailed information about which native plants attract specific pollinators and how to arrange them for maximum impact.

“Our visitors have been telling us they want to do more than just admire the flowers—they want to know what to plant in their own gardens to make a real difference,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that even small planting decisions, when made thoughtfully, can create connected habitats that extend across neighborhoods and help sustain pollinator populations.”

The trail features a series of native plant demonstration beds, each highlighting species that serve specific pollinators. Visitors will see beds designed for monarch butterflies, bumblebees, swallowtails, clearwing moths, and ruby-throated hummingbirds. Each bed is labeled with a tactile sign that includes both standard text and raised botanical illustrations, making the experience accessible to visitors with visual impairments.

A signature stop along the path is the night-blooming flower station, which showcases plants such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jasmine. Interpretive panels at the station explain how moths and other nocturnal pollinators are attracted by scent and pale coloration after sunset. The garden plans to offer evening flashlight tours during peak bloom periods later in the season.

Youth observation cards are available at the trailhead, allowing children to check off pollinators and plants they spot along the route. The cards include simple prompts such as “Find a flower that opens at night” and “Count how many bees visit one plant in two minutes.” Completed cards can be turned in at the garden’s visitor center for a small commemorative sticker.

Shaded resting points have been constructed at regular intervals along the path, using locally sourced lumber and native shade trees. Each rest area includes a bench and a small informational panel on a different aspect of pollinator ecology, such as nesting habits, overwintering strategies, or the difference between generalist and specialist pollinators.

The trail is fully accessible, with a compacted stone dust surface wide enough for wheelchairs and strollers. The grade never exceeds five percent, and all signage is positioned at height-appropriate levels for both seated and standing visitors.

Beginning the first weekend after opening, the garden will host volunteer-led weekend walks along the Pollinator Path. These guided tours, led by trained garden volunteers, will take place each Saturday and Sunday morning at 10 a.m. through the end of September. No registration is required, and the walks are included with general garden admission.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s plant nursery will also carry starter kits of the native species featured along the trail, with take-home care guides developed specifically for the project. “We want people to leave here not just inspired, but equipped,” Ortiz added. “When one gardener plants a patch of milkweed, it helps monarchs. When a whole street plants milkweed, you can actually measure the increase in butterfly populations.”

The Pollinator Path was designed in consultation with local entomologists, native plant specialists, and accessibility advocates. The garden plans to evaluate the trail’s educational impact through visitor surveys and observational studies conducted by the learning programs team over the next two years.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is open daily from 8 a.m. to sunset. The Pollinator Path is accessible during regular garden hours. Admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under five and garden members.

**About Harborleaf Botanical Garden**
Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a nonprofit public garden dedicated to plant conservation, education, and community engagement. Located on 65 acres in central Pennsylvania, the garden maintains more than 2,000 species of native and adapted plants, with an emphasis on regional ecology and sustainable horticulture. Harborleaf welcomes more than 150,000 visitors annually and offers year-round programming for all ages.

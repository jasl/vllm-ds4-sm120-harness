**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New “Pollinator Path” Public Education Trail, Opening July 12**

**PORT HARBOR, Mass. — June 10, 2026** — Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its newest educational attraction, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through accessible, practical gardening techniques. The trail opens to the public on July 12, 2026, and will be located along the east meadow loop, a 0.8-mile fully accessible route.

The Pollinator Path represents a direct response to visitor requests for more hands-on guidance in creating pollinator-friendly spaces at home. The trail combines demonstration gardens, interpretive features, and volunteer-led programming to bridge the gap between botanical knowledge and everyday action.

“Our visitors consistently ask the same question: ‘What can I actually plant to help the bees and butterflies in my own yard?’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. We’ve designed every element to show that small planting decisions—choosing one native flower over a non-native, or leaving a patch of bare soil for ground-nesting bees—can create connected habitats that ripple across neighborhoods.”

The trail begins at the east meadow entrance and follows a level, packed-earth loop suitable for strollers, wheelchairs, and mobility devices. Along the route, visitors will encounter five distinct feature zones. The first, a series of native plant demonstration beds, showcases species that provide essential nectar, pollen, and larval host support for the region’s most common pollinators. Each bed is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom periods, and recommended spacing for home gardens.

A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights plants that open their blossoms after dusk, such as evening primrose and moonflower, which are critical food sources for nocturnal moths. This station includes low-level solar lighting so visitors can observe the flowers without disrupting their natural cycles.

Throughout the trail, tactile signage—featuring embossed images, raised text in both English and Spanish, and braille descriptions—allows visually impaired visitors to engage with the content by touch. Youth observation cards, available at a kiosk near the trailhead, provide children with simple prompts such as “Count how many different colors you see” and “Draw a moth’s wing pattern.” The cards are designed to encourage repeat visits and observational skills without requiring a smartphone or app.

For those who prefer rest and reflection, shaded resting points are spaced at roughly one-quarter-mile intervals along the loop. These small alcoves feature benches and interpretive panels explaining how even a single tree canopy can provide thermal refuge for flying insects during heat waves.

The Garden will also offer a volunteer-led weekend walk each Saturday and Sunday from 10 a.m. to 11 a.m. beginning July 13. Trained docents will guide small groups along the trail, answer questions, and demonstrate techniques such as leaving dead stems for cavity-nesting bees and selecting plants that bloom in succession from early spring through late fall. No advance registration is required, and the walks are free with Garden admission.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden staff spent two years consulting with entomologists, landscape architects, and accessibility specialists to develop the trail’s content and physical design. The east meadow loop was chosen for its existing sun exposure, gentle topography, and proximity to the Garden’s main visitor center. The trail replaces an underused mowed field, with native grasses and wildflowers having been established over the past 18 months to create a living foundation for the demonstration areas.

Maya Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is not intended as a political statement, but as an educational resource grounded in conservation biology and urban ecology.

“This isn’t about mandates or controversy—it’s about connection,” Ortiz said. “When a family sees a bumblebee working a bloom that they can plant in a pot on their apartment balcony, that’s a tangible, hopeful act. We want every visitor to leave with at least one idea they can try at home.”

The opening on July 12 will include brief remarks from Garden leadership, a ribbon cutting at 9:30 a.m., and self-guided opportunities throughout the day. The trail will be open during regular Garden hours thereafter, from 8 a.m. to dusk, seven days a week. Admission to the Pollinator Path is included with standard Garden entry. Youth observation cards and tactile guides are available at no additional cost.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, established in 1972, is a private, nonprofit institution dedicated to plant conservation, environmental education, and public horticulture. The Garden maintains over 100 acres of display gardens, natural areas, and research collections. For more information, visit harborleafbotanical.org or call the visitor services line.

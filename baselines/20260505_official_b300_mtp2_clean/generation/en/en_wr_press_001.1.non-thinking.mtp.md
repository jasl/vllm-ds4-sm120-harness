**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New “Pollinator Path” Educational Trail on July 12**

**PORTLAND, OR – June 15, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden announced today the opening of its newest public education initiative, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated 0.8-mile accessible trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through native plant choices. The trail, located on the east meadow loop of the Garden’s grounds, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

The Pollinator Path was developed in direct response to a growing number of visitor inquiries seeking practical, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly habitat at home. Garden staff noted that many guests expressed interest in moving beyond general awareness to specific, replicable planting strategies.

“Our visitors consistently ask, ‘What should I plant, and where should I put it, to actually make a difference for pollinators?’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path answers that question by showing, step-by-step, how small planting decisions in a single yard or balcony container can create connected, life-sustaining habitats across entire neighborhoods. We want every visitor to leave feeling equipped to transform their own outdoor space, regardless of its size.”

The accessible trail meanders through the east meadow loop, a gently graded, paved route suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. Along the path, visitors will encounter a series of educational features designed for all ages and learning styles.

Native plant demonstration beds form the core of the experience. Each bed highlights a different microhabitat—sunny meadow, shaded edge, and dry slope—showing which native species thrive in each condition. Plants were selected specifically for their value to local pollinators, including Oregon iris, showy milkweed, goldenrod, and bee balm. Companion signage explains bloom timing, host plant relationships, and nectar quality.

A dedicated night-blooming flower station, planted with evening primrose, yucca, and four o’clocks, is positioned near the trail’s midpoint. This feature is designed to illuminate the role of moths and nocturnal pollinators, a group often overlooked in public education. Interpretive panels explain how pale-colored, fragrant flowers attract night-flying species and why reducing outdoor lighting benefits these animals.

Tactile signage, with raised text and botanical illustrations, is installed at each station, allowing visitors with visual impairments to engage with the content through touch. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, encourage children to document the species they spot. Each card includes simple prompts—“Count the colors on a butterfly’s wing” or “Describe the shape of a flower’s petals”—designed to build observation skills without requiring literacy.

Shaded resting points, constructed from sustainably sourced timber and native stone, are spaced at regular intervals. These benches and small pavilions provide respite for older visitors and families, while also serving as informal gathering spots for discussion.

To deepen public engagement, the Garden will offer a volunteer-led weekend walk every Saturday and Sunday from 10 a.m. to noon, beginning July 12 and continuing through October. Trained docents, many of whom are master gardeners and community naturalists, will guide small groups, answer questions, and demonstrate techniques such as deadheading and mulching for pollinator benefit. No registration is required, and walks are included with general Garden admission.

The east meadow loop was chosen for its existing pollinator activity and its accessibility. Previously an underutilized section of the Garden, the area has been redesigned to minimize disturbance to existing wildlife while adding interpretive elements. Garden staff worked with local ecologists to ensure that plant selections align with the Willamette Valley’s native ecosystem and that no invasive species were introduced.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has a long-standing commitment to environmental education but has historically focused its public programming on general horticulture and plant science. The Pollinator Path represents a shift toward more targeted, action-oriented learning—one that encourages visitors to apply what they learn immediately.

“The idea is not just to show people a beautiful garden, but to give them a toolkit,” said Ortiz. “If someone visits the Pollinator Path and then goes home to swap a non-native shrub for a native flowering plant, that is a tangible win for local biodiversity. When thousands of people make that same small shift, the cumulative effect is significant.”

The Garden plans to evaluate the trail’s impact through visitor surveys and observation logs submitted by youth using the activity cards. Data on pollinator visitation rates along the path—recorded by staff and volunteers—will inform future plantings and programming decisions.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden remains an independent, nonpartisan institution. The Pollinator Path’s content is focused exclusively on ecological education and does not address policy, legislation, or political advocacy.

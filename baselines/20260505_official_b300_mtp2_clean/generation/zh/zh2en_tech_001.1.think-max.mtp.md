**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After the bamboo shoots appear, one inspects the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow leaves is the best material. Around the Grain in Ear solar period (early June), one goes up the mountain to cut it. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber left behind looks like ramie fibers. A solution is made by mixing high-quality lime with water, and the bamboo is coated with this paste. It is then placed in an *huang* bucket (a large wooden vat) for boiling, with the fire kept going for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron about four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter is used. The cauldron's rim is sealed with a mixture of mud and lime, forming a rim as tall and wide as the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong, holding over ten *dan* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. The *huang* bucket is placed on top; it is about one *zhang* five *chi* (approx. 5 meters) in circumference and over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter. The papermaking vat is square and has a half-open top. The vat's size is determined by the mold, and the mold's size is determined by the paper being made. After the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water until it stands about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. The papermaking mold is made of very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. It can be rolled up and opened, supported by a wooden frame underneath. When the stack is thick enough, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever and rope, like pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water. After that, each sheet is lifted one by one with fine copper tweezers and dried over a fire. For drying, a brick chamber with a narrow corridor is built. The floor of the corridor is paved with bricks, leaving every few bricks a gap. A fire is lit at one end, and the heat passes through the gaps under the bricks, heating them all. The wet sheets are pasted onto the hot bricks to dry, then lifted off and gathered into quires.

In recent times, a wide format called "Big Four-Link" has been used for important writings. Waste paper, with its red and black ink stains washed off, can be soaked, rotted, and put back into the vat.

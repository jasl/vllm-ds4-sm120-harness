**Making Bamboo Paper**
(from *Tiangong Kaiwu* – “Exploitation of the Works of Nature”)

Bamboo paper is made in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots appear, inspect the hillsides. Bamboo that is about to sprout branches and leaves is the best material. Around the *Mangzhong* (Grain in Ear) solar term (early June), go up the mountain, cut the bamboo, and cut it into pieces five to seven *chi* (about 1.7–2.3 m) long. On the mountain itself, dig a pit and fill it with water to soak the bamboo. In case the pit water dries up, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called *shaqing* – “killing the green”). The inner bamboo fiber will then resemble ramie. Mix slaked lime into a paste, coat the bamboo with it, and put it in a *huang* (a large cylindrical vat) to boil. The fire should be kept going for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling, use a cauldron about four *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. Seal the rim with clay and lime, making the rim as tall and wide as the salt-boiling pans in Guangdong, so it can hold over ten *dan* (about 500–600 liters) of water. Place the *huang* vat on top—its circumference is 15 *chi* (5 m), diameter about four *chi* (1.3 m). Once sealed, boil for a full eight days. Let it rest for one day, then open the vat and remove the bamboo pulp (*zhuma*). Wash it in a clean soaking pit. The pit’s bottom and sides should be lined with wooden boards fitted tightly together to prevent mud contamination (not necessary for coarse paper). After washing, mix the pulp with plant-ash water, put it back into a cauldron, level it, and spread a layer of rice-straw ash about one *cun* (3.3 cm) thick on top. When the water in the vat boils, transfer the pulp to another vat and pour the ash water over it again. If it cools, reheat and pour again. Repeat this for ten or more days, until the bamboo naturally rots and decomposes. Then take it out and pound it in a mortar (in mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers are used). Pound until it becomes like mud. Pour it into a vat.

**The Papermaking Vat and Mold**
The vat is square on top. Its width depends on the mold (*lian*, a bamboo screen), and the mold’s size depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water to about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. Add a “paper medicine” (a plant-based liquid, often from leaves resembling peach or bamboo; local names vary). This makes the water drain cleanly and the paper white.

The paper mold is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is mounted on a frame that can be opened and closed. Hold the mold with both hands, dip it into the vat, and lift the bamboo pulp onto the screen. The thickness of the sheet depends on the operator’s skill—a light lift makes thin paper, a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains from all sides back into the vat. Then flip the mold over to deposit the wet sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. When enough sheets have been stacked, place a board on top and press them, using a rope and lever (like a wine press), to squeeze out all the water.

Then, using fine copper tweezers, lift each sheet one by one and dry it over a heated wall. To build the drying wall: first, make a double wall (a narrow passage) from brick, with the floor covered in bricks, leaving one brick gap every few bricks. Light a fire at one end of the passage; the heat passes through the gaps and heats the entire wall. The damp paper sheets are pasted onto the hot wall one by one, dried, and then removed as finished sheets.

**Modern Varieties**
Recently, wide sheets called “Big Four-Link” have become valued for writing. Waste paper—after washing off red ink and stains—can be soaked, rotted, and reused in the vat, skipping the earlier boiling and soaking steps. This still makes paper, with little waste. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. In the north, even scraps are collected and remade into “soul-returning paper.” Bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, can all be recycled this way.

**Fire Paper and Coarse Paper**
For *huozhi* (fire paper) and rough paper, the process of cutting bamboo, boiling, adding ash water, and rinsing is the same. The only difference is that after removing the sheet from the mold, it is not dried on a hot wall. Instead, water is pressed out, and the sheets are sun-dried.

In the high Tang dynasty (8th–9th century), paper money was burned as offerings to spirits, replacing cloth (in the north, cut strips called “board money”). Thus, this kind is called *huozhi* (fire paper). In parts of Hubei and Hunan, up to a thousand *jin* (600 kg) may be burned in one ceremony. About 70% of this paper is used for funerary burning, 30% for daily use. The coarsest, thickest kind is called *baoguozhi* (wrapping paper); it is made from bamboo pulp mixed with old rice straw.

**Stationery Paper**
The *jianshi* (letter paper) made in Yanshan and other counties is entirely made from fine bamboo material, formed thick on the mold, and sold at high prices. The best quality is called *guanjian* (official letter paper). Wealthy families use it for name cards. It is thick and free of coarse fibers. To make red auspicious paper (*jijian*), the sheets are first treated with alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking occurs in the south, but Fujian province specializes in it. After bamboo shoots grow, one inspects the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow leaves is the best material. Soak for over 100 days. Then, beat and wash the bamboo to remove the coarse outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber now resembles ramie. Make a paste from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the bamboo with it, and place it in a *huang* bucket (a large cooking vat) to boil. The fire should be maintained for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling bamboo, use a cauldron with a diameter of four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters). Build a rim around the cauldron with mud and lime, making it tall and wide like the salt-boiling pans in Guangdong, holding over ten *dan* (approx. 1,000 liters) of water. Place the *huang* bucket on top; its circumference is 15 *chi* (approx. 5 meters) and its diameter is over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters). After sealing it, the boiling is done in eight days. Let it rest for one day. Then, open the bucket, take out the bamboo pulp, and wash it in a clean soaking pond. The pond’s bottom and all four sides must be lined with tightly joined wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, soak the pulp in wood-ash lye. Then, put it back into a cauldron, level the surface, and evenly spread a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3.3 cm) thick on top. When the water in the bucket boils, take the pulp out and place it in another bucket, still pouring the ash lye over it. If the lye cools, re-boil it and pour again. Repeat this for over ten days. The pulp will naturally rot and become foul-smelling. Take it out and put it into a mortar for pounding (mountain regions have water-powered trip-hammers for this). Pound it until it has the consistency of mud. Then, pour it into a vat.

The papermaking vat is square and half-open at the top. Its dimensions depend on the mold, and the mold's dimensions depend on the desired paper size. When the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. Add the "paper drug" liquid (a plant extract, often from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves – local names vary). This causes the water to drain away, leaving clean white fibers. The papermaking mold is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. It can be rolled or unrolled, and when in use, it is supported by a frame. Holding the mold with both hands, dip it into the vat and scoop up the bamboo pulp, spreading it evenly on the screen. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by the craftsman’s technique: a gentle lift makes a thin sheet, a heavy lift makes a thick one. While the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains back into the vat from all sides. Then, turn the mold over to deposit the wet sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. When a stack is complete, place a board on top, tighten it with ropes and a lever (like pressing wine), to squeeze out the remaining water. After drying, use fine, light copper tweezers to lift each sheet individually for drying. For drying, first build a narrow chamber from mud bricks. Cover the chamber floor with bricks, leaving one brick missing every few bricks. Light a fire at one end of the chamber. The hot air and flames travel through the gaps in the bricks, heating them. The outer bricks become very hot. Paste the wet sheets onto the hot brick wall one by one. Once dry, lift them off and gather them into quires.

In recent times, a wide-format paper called "Big Four-Linked" has become prized for writing. Waste paper, after washing off its ink and colors, can be soaked until rotten, then put back into the vat for reuse. This process saves all the earlier cooking and soaking steps. It still makes good paper, with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this recycling is not common. But in the north, even a small scrap of paper is picked up from the ground and recycled, making what is called "returned-soul paper." Both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, are recycled this way. As for fire paper and rough paper, the steps of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and treating it with ash and water are the same as above. During the Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE), there was much activity regarding ghosts and spirits. Paper money replaced silk as burnt offerings (in the north, they used cut strips called "board money"). To make red auspicious paper, the sheets are first treated with an alum water solution, then dyed with safflower juice.

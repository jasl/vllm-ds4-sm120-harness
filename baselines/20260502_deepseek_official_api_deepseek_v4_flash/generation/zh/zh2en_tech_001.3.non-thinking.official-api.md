**Making Bamboo Paper**

All bamboo papermaking occurs in the south, and Fujian province is particularly known for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one inspects the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow branches and leaves is the best material. Around the time of Grain in Ear (芒種, early June), people go into the mountains to cut the bamboo. They cut it into lengths of five to seven *chi* (about 1.7–2.3 meters), dig a pit on the mountain, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo in it. If the pit water might dry up, bamboo pipes are used to channel a constant stream of water into it. After soaking for more than a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this is called "killing the green" 殺青). What remains is the bamboo fiber, which looks like ramie. This fiber is coated with a paste made from high-quality lime dissolved in water, placed in a *huang* vat (a large cylindrical vessel), and boiled. The fire is kept going for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron with a diameter of four *chi* (about 1.3 m) is used. The rim of the cauldron is sealed with a mixture of mud and lime, made as wide and tall as the salt-boiling pans in Guangdong, capable of holding over ten *dan* of water (a *dan* is about 100 liters). A *huang* vat, with a circumference of 15 *chi* (about 5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi*, is placed on top as a lid. After sealing, the boiling continues for eight full days. The fire is let to rest for one day, then the vat is opened and the bamboo pulp (now called "bamboo hemp" 竹麻) is removed and washed clean in a clear-water pit. The bottom and sides of this pit must be lined with tightly joined wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is treated with wood-ash slurry, then put back into the cauldron. The pulp is leveled and covered with a one-inch (about 3 cm) layer of dry rice straw ash. Once the water in the cauldron boils, the liquid is removed and poured into a separate vessel, then poured back over the pulp. If the liquid gets cold, it is reheated and poured again. This process repeats for ten or more days, until the pulp naturally rots and becomes soft. The pulp is then removed and beaten in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers 水碓), continuing until it has the consistency of mud. It is then poured into a vat.

**The Papermaking Vat and Screen:**
The vat is square and half as wide at the top as at the bottom. Its width matches the screen, and the screen matches the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, clear water is added to the vat until it stands about three inches (about 10 cm) above the pulp. A "paper medicine" liquid (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name) is stirred in. This causes the fibers to bond into white paper as the water drains. The papermaking screen (簾) is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven together. It is stretched over a rectangular frame. The papermaker holds the screen with both hands and dips it into the vat, lifting a layer of bamboo pulp onto the screen. The thickness of the paper depends on the technique: a gentle lift gives thin paper, a heavy one gives thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The screen is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When enough sheets have been accumulated, a board is placed on top, ropes are tied around it, and a lever is inserted (like in pressing wine) to squeeze out the remaining water. The sheets are then individually lifted with fine copper tweezers and dried.

**Drying the Paper:**
To dry paper, an oven is built from brick, forming a narrow passage. The floor of the passage is also bricked, but every few bricks, one brick is left out to create a gap. A fire is lit at the entrance of the passage; the hot air and smoke travel through the gaps, heating the bricks above. The wet sheets are pasted one by one onto the hot brick walls, dried, and then peeled off into finished stacks.

Modern wide-format paper, called "Large Four-Linked" (大四連), is valued for writing. Waste paper, with ink or vermilion cleaned off, can be soaked, rotted, and returned to the vat, skipping the earlier boiling and soaking steps. It still becomes paper with little loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not commonly done. In the north, even small scraps are collected and remade into "Returned Soul Paper" (還魂紙). This process works for both bamboo-based and bark-based papers, fine or coarse.

**Special Types:**
- **Fire Paper** (火紙) and **Rough Paper** (糙紙): Made by the same process of cutting bamboo, boiling, and treating with ash slurry, but after being lifted from the screen, they are not baked. Instead, they are pressed to remove water and dried in the sun.
  During the Tang dynasty, paper money was burned as offerings in religious ceremonies (replacing silk), hence the name "Fire Paper." In Hubei and Hunan, a single burning can consume up to 500 kg. About 70% of this paper is used for offerings, 30% for daily use.
- **Wrapping Paper** (包裹紙): The coarsest and thickest, made from bamboo pulp mixed with old rice straw.
- **Letter Paper** (柬紙): Made in places like Yanshan (in Jiangxi), it uses only fine bamboo material, formed thickly to command a high price. The best quality is called **Official Letter Paper** (官柬), used by wealthy families for name cards. It is thick and free of fiber clumps. For festive red paper, it is first dyed with alum water, then coated with safflower juice.

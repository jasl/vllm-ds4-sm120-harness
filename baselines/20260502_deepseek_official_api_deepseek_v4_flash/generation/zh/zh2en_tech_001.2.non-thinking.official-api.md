**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking is carried out in the south, but Fujian province is particularly renowned for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one observes the depth of the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow branches and leaves is considered the best material. Around the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), one goes up the mountain to cut the bamboo. It is cut into pieces five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.7–2.3 meters) long. Right there on the mountain, a pit is dug and filled with water to soak the bamboo. To prevent the pit water from drying up, bamboo pipes are used to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, work begins: the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo core then resembles ramie fibers. A lime solution made from high-quality lime is prepared, and the bamboo is coated with it. The coated material is placed into a large vat and boiled over a fire for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron with a diameter of four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) is used. The rim of the cauldron is built up with clay and lime paste to a height and width similar to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, making it capable of holding over ten *dan* (approx. 600+ liters) of water. A large wooden vat (called a *huangtong*) with a circumference of 1.5 *zhang* (approx. 5 meters) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) is placed on top as a lid. After sealing and boiling for a full eight days, the fire is stopped for one day. Then, the vat is uncovered, and the bamboo mass (now called "bamboo hemp") is taken out. It is taken to a clean washing pool filled with fresh water. The bottom and sides of this pool are carefully lined with wooden boards to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing clean, the material is treated with a wood-ash lye solution and then put back into a cauldron. It is leveled off, and a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3.2 cm) thick is spread evenly over it. When the water in the cauldron boils, the hot lye water is ladled out and poured back over the material in another vat. If the lye water cools, it is reheated and poured again. This process is repeated for over ten days, until the bamboo naturally rots and becomes mushy. It is then taken out and placed in a mortar for pounding (mountain regions have water-powered trip-hammers). It is pounded until it becomes a fine, mud-like paste, which is then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat is rectangular. Its width and length determine the size of the paper mold, which in turn determines the size of the paper sheet. Once the bamboo paste is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to a depth of about three *cun* (10 cm) above the paste. A papermaking drug (a plant juice, often from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves; local names vary) is added to the vat. This causes the water to drain cleanly, leaving a white, formed sheet. The paper mold is made of very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is mounted on a supporting frame that can be rolled open or closed. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and sways it to collect the bamboo pulp evenly on the screen. The thickness of the sheet depends on the worker's technique: a gentle sway makes a thin sheet, a vigorous sway makes a thick one. As the bamboo pulp floats on the screen, the water drains back into the vat from all four sides. The screen is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet of paper onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When enough sheets have been stacked, a board is placed on top, and a lever is used to press out the water (like pressing wine or oil), squeezing it completely dry. Then, a light, fine copper tweezer is used to lift each sheet individually for drying.

Drying is done in a special chamber. A narrow corridor is built with adobe bricks on the sides and floor. Every few bricks on the floor, one brick is left out to create a fire channel. A fire is lit at one end, and the hot air and smoke travel through the channels, heating the brick walls. The wet sheets of paper are pasted one by one onto the hot brick walls to dry. Once dry, they are peeled off and gathered into bundles.

In recent times, a wide format called "Big Four-link" has been valued for writing and official documents. Waste paper, with its red ink and dirt washed off, can be soaked, rotted, and re-processed in the vat. This saves the entire initial process of boiling and soaking and still yields usable paper, with little loss of material. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered worthwhile. But in the north, even small scraps of paper are saved and re-made into "returned-soul paper." Whether made from bamboo or tree bark, fine or coarse, the principle is the same. For "fire paper" (spirit money) and rough paper, the steps of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and treating it with ash lye are the same. The only difference is that after the sheet is formed and pressed, it is not baked in the drying chamber; it is simply sun-dried.

During the Tang Dynasty, spirit worship was widespread, and paper money replaced silk as offerings (in the north, cut strips were used, called "board money"). Hence, this type of paper is called "fire paper." In the area of ancient Chu and Jing (modern Hubei/Hunan), it is customary to burn up to a thousand *jin* (approx. 600 kg) of it in one ceremony. About 70% of this paper is used for such spirit offerings, and 30% for daily use. The coarsest and thickest kind is called "wrapping paper," which is made by mixing bamboo pulp with the straw from old rice paddies. As for the fine letter paper made in Qianshan County and other places, it is made entirely from fine bamboo material, formed into a thick sheet, and sold at high prices. The finest grade is called "official letter paper." Wealthy and noble families use it for their calling cards. It is thick and free from fiber clumps. To make red paper for auspicious occasions, it is first treated with an alum solution, and then coated with the juice of safflower (Carthamus tinctorius).

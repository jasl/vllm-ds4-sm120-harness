**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, and Fujian province is especially known for it. When bamboo shoots have grown, check the mountain valleys. After soaking for over 100 days, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber will then look like ramie fibers. Make a paste from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the bamboo fibers with it, and place them in a wooden vat (called a *huang* bucket) for boiling. The boiling should last for 8 days and 8 nights.

For boiling the bamboo, use a cauldron about 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter. Seal the rim of the cauldron with clay and lime, forming a high and wide rim like the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, so it can hold over 10 *dan* (approx. 500–1000 liters) of water. Cover this with the *huang* bucket, which has a circumference of 15 *chi* (approx. 5 meters) and a diameter of over 4 *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters). Once sealed, boil for a full 8 days. Let it rest for one day, then open the *huang* bucket and take out the boiled bamboo pulp (called "bamboo hemp"). Wash it clean in a clear-water soaking pit. The bottom and all four sides of this pit must be lined with tightly fitted wooden planks to prevent mud from contaminating the pulp (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, treat the pulp with wood-ash lye. Put it back into a cauldron, level the surface, and spread an even layer of rice straw ash about 1 *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick on top. When the water in the cauldron boils, remove the pulp and put it into another vat, continuing to pour hot ash lye over it. If the lye cools, re-boil it and pour again. Repeat this for over ten days; the pulp will naturally rot and become soft. Then take it out and put it into a mortar for pounding. (In mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers are used.) Pound it until it has the consistency of mud. Then pour it into a vat.

For the paper-scooping vat: it is rectangular at the top. Its size depends on the paper-mold (the sieve frame), and the mold's size depends on the paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about 3 *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp surface. Add "paper medicine" water (made from a plant that looks like peach or bamboo leaves—the local name varies). This causes the water to drain cleanly, leaving white paper. The paper-mold is made from very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a mat. This mat is stretched on a frame with horizontal and vertical supports. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts some bamboo pulp onto the mat. The paper's thickness is controlled by the worker's technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the mat, water drains through the sides back into the vat. Then the worker flips the mold over, depositing the wet sheet of paper onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is tall enough, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever and rope, like pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water. Afterward, the sheets are lifted one by one with fine light copper tweezers and dried. For drying, first build a brick wall with a hollow passage inside. Cover the ground under the passage with bricks, leaving one brick missing every few bricks. Build a fire at one end of the passage; the hot air and flames enter the passage and heat the bricks from the inside until the outer surface is very hot. Wet sheets are pasted onto this hot brick wall to dry. Once dry, they are removed and gathered into a book.

In recent times, a wide format called "Big Four-Connections" has been valued for writing and printing. Waste paper—after washing off the red and black ink stains—can be soaked, rotted, and re-processed in the vat, saving all the previous steps of boiling and soaking. It becomes paper again with little loss of material. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important.

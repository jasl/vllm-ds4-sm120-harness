**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo paper-making originates in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots grow, the best material comes from bamboos that are about to sprout leaves, growing in deep mountain valleys. Around the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), workers go into the mountains to cut the bamboo. It is chopped into sections five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.7–2.3 m) long. On the same mountain, a pit is dug and filled with water to soak the bamboo. To keep the water from drying up, bamboo pipes channel a constant stream into the pit. Soaking lasts over a hundred days, after which the bamboo is beaten and washed to remove the coarse outer shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber looks like ramie. High-quality lime is dissolved into a paste, mixed with the fiber, and placed in a *huang*-shaped bucket (large cylindrical vat) for boiling, which continues for eight days and nights.

The boiling vat has a diameter of four *chi* (~1.3 m). Its rim is sealed with clay and lime, similar in height and width to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, and can hold over ten *dan* (approx. 1,000 L) of water. The *huang* bucket, with a circumference of 15 *chi* (~5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi*, is placed on top as a lid. After eight full days of boiling, the fire is stopped for one day. Then the lid is removed, and the bamboo pulp (called "bamboo hemp") is taken out and washed clean in a clear-water soaking pit. The pit's bottom and sides are lined with tightly fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this lining is unnecessary for coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is coated with wood-ash slurry and placed back into the vat. It is leveled, and a one-cun (~3.3 cm) layer of rice-straw ash is spread on top. The vat is brought to a boil; then the liquid is drained into another vat and poured back over the pulp repeatedly. If the liquid cools, it is re-boiled before pouring. After about ten days of this, the fiber naturally rots and softens. It is then taken out and beaten in a mortar (in mountainous areas, water-powered trip-hammers are used) until it becomes mud-like in consistency. This paste is then poured into a vat for sheet-forming.

**Sheet-Forming Vat & Process**

The sheet-forming vat is a rectangular trough. Its dimensions depend on the mold (called a *lian* or "screen"), and the mold's dimensions depend on the desired paper size. The bamboo pulp is placed in the vat, covered by about three cun (~10 cm) of clear water. A "paper drug" liquid (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves; local names vary) is added, which helps the fibers bond and the paper to become white when dried.

The mold is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven together. When opened, it is supported by a frame with crossbars. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts it, letting the pulp settle onto the screen. Thickness depends on the technique: a gentle lift produces thin paper; a heavy lift makes thick paper. While the pulp floats on the screen, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The mold is then flipped to transfer the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way.

When the stack reaches the desired height, a board is placed on top, and a rope with a stick is used to press out water (similar to pressing wine). After the water is fully squeezed out, individual sheets are lifted with fine copper tweezers and dried on a heated wall.

**Drying Wall**

The drying wall is built with two parallel brick walls forming a narrow passage. The floor of the passage is covered with bricks, except for one missing brick per section. A fire is lit at one end of the passage; flames and heat travel through the brick gaps, heating the outer wall surfaces. Wet sheets are pasted onto the hot wall to dry, then peeled off and collected into bundles.

**Variations & Uses**

In recent times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" has been valued for writing and books. Waste paper—after washing off red or black ink and soaking until disintegrated—can be repulped in the vat, skipping the earlier boiling and soaking steps, and still turned back into paper with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this recycling is not common. In the north, even small scraps are collected and remade into "returned-soul paper." Bamboo and bark papers, fine or coarse, follow similar recycling.

Torch paper and rough paper are made with the same steps—cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and treating with ash and lime water—but after sheet-forming, they are not dried on the hot wall. Instead, they are pressed and dried in the sun.

During the Tang dynasty, as spirit worship became popular, paper money replaced burned silk in rituals (in the north, cut strips called "board money" were used). This paper is called "fire paper." In the Chu region, some burn up to a thousand *jin* (~600 kg) in a single ceremony. About 70% of this paper goes to offerings; the rest is for daily use.

The coarsest, thickest paper, called wrapping paper, is made from bamboo pulp mixed with old rice straw from late-season fields.

For high-quality letter paper (like that produced in Qianshan county), only fine bamboo material is used, formed thickly on the mold, and sold at high prices. The best grade is called "official letter paper." Wealthy families use it for name cards. It is thick and free of fiber clumps. To make red "auspicious letter paper," the paper is first treated with alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

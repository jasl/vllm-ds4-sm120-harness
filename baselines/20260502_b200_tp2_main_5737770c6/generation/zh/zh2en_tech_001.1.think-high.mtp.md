### Translation of "Tian Gong Kai Wu" – Bamboo Papermaking

All bamboo papermaking originates in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. When bamboo shoots have grown, the best material comes from bamboo in mountain valleys that is about to produce leaves. Around the Grain in Ear season (early June), cut the bamboo down, chop it into pieces five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.6–2.3 m) long, and dig a pit on the mountain to soak them in water. To keep the pit from drying out, use bamboo pipes to channel a continuous stream into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, pound and wash the bamboo to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner part now resembles ramie fibers. Mix slaked lime into a paste, coat the bamboo with it, and place it in a "vertical bucket" (a large wooden vat) to boil for eight days and nights.

For boiling, use a cauldron four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. Its rim is sealed with clay and lime, similar to the large salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, and can hold over ten *shi* (approx. 1,000 liters) of water. The bucket cover is 1.5 *zhang* (approx. 5 m) in circumference and over four *chi* in diameter. After boiling for eight full days, let it rest for one day. Then open the bucket, take out the bamboo fiber ("bamboo hemp"), and wash it in a clean soaking pit. The pit's bottom and sides must be lined with tightly fitted planks to prevent mud contamination (not necessary for coarse paper). After washing, coat the fibers with wood-ash lye and put them back into the cauldron. Level the surface and spread a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick on top. When the water in the bucket boils, transfer the fibers to another bucket and let the ash-lye water drip over them. If the liquid cools, reheat it and repeat. After ten or more days, the fibers will naturally rot and become soft. Then remove them for pounding in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers). Pound until the fibers form a muddy paste, then pour it into a vat.

### The Papermaking Vat and Screen

The papermaking vat is square at the top. Its width matches the screen, and the screen's size matches the desired paper. Once the bamboo fiber paste is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the paste. Add a papermaking liquor ("paper drug") made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves (local names vary). This makes the water drain cleanly, leaving pure white paper. The screen is woven from finely polished bamboo strips. It is stretched over a rectangular frame when in use. Hold the screen with both hands, dip it into the vat, and lift it to spread the bamboo fibers evenly. The thickness of the paper depends on the operator's technique: a gentle lift yields thin paper, a heavy one yields thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains back into the vat. Then flip the screen onto a board to deposit the paper sheet. Stack thousands of sheets this way. Once the stack is complete, press it with a board and tighten ropes with a lever, like pressing wine, to squeeze out all moisture. Finally, lift each sheet with fine copper tweezers and dry it.

### Drying the Paper

Build a drying chamber with brick walls and a brick floor, leaving gaps between some bricks. Light a fire at one end; heat rises through the gaps and warms the bricks. Paste each wet sheet onto the hot bricks to dry, then peel it off and gather it into quires.

### Modern Variations

Wide-format paper, called "Large Four-Link," was once popular for writing. Waste paper—cleaned of ink and dyes, soaked, and repulped—can be reused, saving the initial soaking and boiling steps. This is common in the north, where even small scraps are collected to make "soul-returning paper," but not in the south, where bamboo is cheap. Both bamboo and bark papers, fine or coarse, follow similar processes. For fire paper (used in offerings) and rough paper, the steps are identical, but after removing from the screen, they are pressed and sun-dried instead of baked.

### Historical Note

During the Tang dynasty, paper money was burned for rituals, replacing silk offerings. In the north, cut strips called "board money" were used. Paper for this purpose is called "fire paper," and in Jing-Chu region, a single burning can involve thousands of sheets. About 70% of fire paper goes to funerary use, 30% to everyday use. The coarsest, thickest variety is wrapping paper, made from bamboo fiber mixed with old rice straw. Fine letter paper from Yanshan, however, uses pure, high-quality bamboo pulp to command high prices. The best is "official letter" paper; wealthy families use it for name cards. It is thick and free of fiber lumps. To make red auspicious paper, the sheets are first treated with alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

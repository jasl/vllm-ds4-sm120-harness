Bamboo papermaking is done in the south, but Fujian province specializes in it. After bamboo shoots grow, observe the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to sprout leaves is the best material. Make a slurry from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the bamboo with it, and place it in a *huang* bucket (a large boiling vat) to cook. The cooking fire should burn for eight days and eight nights.

For cooking bamboo, use a cauldron four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) in diameter. Seal the rim of the cauldron with a mixture of mud and lime, making it tall and wide—similar in shape to the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong. It should hold over ten *dan* (roughly 500+ liters) of water. Place the *huang* bucket on top; it has a circumference of 15 *chi* (approx. 5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m). Seal it and cook. After exactly eight days, let the fire rest for one day. Then, open the bucket and take out the cooked bamboo pulp (called *zhu ma*, "bamboo hemp"). Wash it in a clean soaking pond. The bottom and all four sides of this pond must be lined with tightly-fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, apply a slurry of wood ash and then put the pulp back into a cauldron. Level the surface and spread a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick on top. When the water in the bucket boils, transfer the pulp to another bucket and pour the ash-water mixture over it. If the liquid cools, re-boil it and pour again. Repeat this for about ten days; the pulp will naturally rot and disintegrate. Then, take it out and place it in a mortar for pounding (mountain regions have water-powered trip-hammers for this). Pound it until it has the consistency of mud. Finally, pour the pounded pulp into a vat.

The papermaking vat is rectangular and half-covered. Its width depends on the mold (*lian*, a bamboo screen), and the mold's size depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. Add papermaking potion (made from a leaf resembling peach or bamboo leaves—its name varies locally). This causes the fibers to bond into white sheets when the water drains. The paper mold is woven from extremely fine, polished bamboo strips. It is stretched flat over a rectangular framework. Hold the mold with both hands and dip it into the vat, lifting up the bamboo fibers onto the screen. The thickness of the sheet depends on the technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper; a heavy lift makes thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains from all sides back into the vat. Then, flip the mold over to transfer the wet sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets this way. When the stack is high enough, place a board on top and press it with a lever and rope, like pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water. After pressing, use fine brass tweezers to lift each sheet individually and dry it on a heated wall. To build a drying wall: use clay bricks to construct a double-walled tunnel. Cover the tunnel floor with bricks, leaving one brick missing every few bricks. Light a fire at the entrance; the heat passes through the gaps in the bricks, heating the entire outer wall. Paste the wet sheets onto the hot wall. When dry, peel them off into quires.

In modern times, wide sheets called "Big Four-Link" are valued for writing and documents. Waste paper—washed clean of ink and dyes, then soaked and rotted—can be re-pulped in the vat. This saves all the earlier cooking and soaking steps, and the result is still good paper with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. In the north, even small scraps are collected and re-made into "returned-soul paper". Bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse, are all made this way.

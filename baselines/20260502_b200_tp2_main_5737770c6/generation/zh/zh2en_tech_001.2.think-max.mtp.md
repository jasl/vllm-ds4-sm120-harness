### Bamboo Papermaking

Bamboo papermaking is practiced in southern China, but Fujian province is especially renowned for it. After the bamboo shoots have grown, one examines the depth of the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to grow branches and leaves is considered the best raw material. Around the time of the Mangzhong solar term (early June, Grain in Ear), workers go into the mountains to cut it down. The bamboo is cut into lengths of five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.7–2.3 meters). Right there on the mountain, a pit is dug and filled with water to soak the bamboo. To prevent the pit water from drying out, bamboo pipes (aqueducts) are used to channel a continuous flow of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is pounded and washed to remove the rough outer shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green"). What remains is the bamboo fiber, which resembles ramie (a plant fiber) in texture.

Quicklime (calcium oxide) is dissolved in water to make a lime slurry. This slurry is mixed with the bamboo fibers, which are then placed into an iron pot (called a *huangtong* tank/pot setup) for boiling. The boiling takes a full eight days and eight nights.

The pot used for boiling the bamboo has a diameter of four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m). Its rim is built up with clay and lime, making it high and wide, similar in shape to the large salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong. The pot can hold over ten *dan* (approx. 600+ liters) of water. A large wooden vat (the *huangtong* tank, circumference 15 *chi* / approx. 5 m, diameter 4+ *chi* / approx. 1.3 m) sits on top of the pot, sealed in place. Once sealed, the contents are boiled for the full eight days. The fire is then stopped for one day. After that, the vat is opened and the boiled bamboo pulp (now called "bamboo hemp") is taken out and washed clean in a clear-water soaking pit. The bottom and all four sides of this soaking pit must be lined with tightly fitted wooden planks to prevent mud from dirtying the pulp (this lining step is not necessary for making coarse paper).

After washing, the bamboo hemp is treated with an ash slurry (water mixed with plant ashes). It is then put back into a pot, leveled flat, and covered with a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3.2 cm) thick. The water in the pot is brought to a boil. The hot liquid (the lye) is then ladled over the bamboo hemp, which has been moved to another vat. If the liquid cools, it is reheated and poured again. This process is repeated for ten or more days, until the bamboo fibers naturally rot and become soft. The rotted pulp is then taken out and placed in a mortar for pounding (in mountainous regions, water-powered trip-hammers, *shuidui*, are used for this). It is pounded until it has the consistency of mud or paste. This finished pulp is then dumped into the papermaking vat (the *chao* or dipping vat).

The papermaking vat is built with a square or rectangular top opening. The size of the vat depends on the size of the mold (the screen frame), and the mold size depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to a depth of about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. A "paper medicine" liquid (a plant-based sizing agent, made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name) is added to the vat. This agent causes the water to drain cleanly, leaving the paper sheet white.

The papermaking mold (*lian*) is made from very finely scraped and smoothed bamboo strips woven into a screen. This screen is mounted on a frame that can be unfolded and opened; underneath the screen is a supporting grid structure. The papermaker holds the mold by both hands and dips it into the vat, scooping up the bamboo pulp so that it spreads evenly on the screen. The thickness of the sheet depends on the worker's technique: a gentle dip makes a thin sheet, a forceful dip makes a thick one. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains from the edges back into the vat. Then the mold is turned over, depositing the wet sheet of paper onto a stack on a board. Thousands of sheets are built up one by one in this way. When the stack is high enough, a board is placed on top, and a lever system (like that used for squeezing wine) is applied with ropes and a stick to press the water out completely. After pressing, each sheet is individually lifted with light, fine copper tweezers and then dried over heat.

For drying the paper, a double wall (an oven-like drying alley) is built from brick. The floor of the alley is also tiled, with bricks laid leaving a gap every few bricks. A fire is lit in a firebox at one end of the alley. The hot gases and flames flow through gaps in the bricks, heating the entire brick wall surface from the inside. The wet sheets of paper are pasted one by one onto the hot outer brick wall to dry. Once dry, they are peeled off and gathered into quires (folded sets).

In recent times, a wide-format paper called "Big Four-Lian" has become highly valued for writing and documents. Waste paper, even if stained with red ink or ink marks, can be washed, soaked until rotted, and returned to the vat. This completely bypasses the earlier boiling and soaking steps, yet it still makes paper with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, even a small scrap of paper found on the ground is picked up and recycled this way; the resulting product is called "returned-soul paper." The principle is the same for both bamboo and bark papers, and for fine and coarse papers.

As for "fire paper" (spirit paper used for burning) and rough paper, the processes of cutting the bamboo, boiling the pulp, treating with ash slurry, and draining with water are all the same. The only difference is that after being lifted from the mold, these papers are not dried on the heated wall. Instead, they are simply pressed to remove water and sun-dried.

During the High Tang period (7th-9th centuries), religious ceremonies for ghosts and spirits became common, and paper money replaced burned silk cloth for offerings (in the north, strips of this paper are used, called "board money"). About 70% of this fire paper is used for burning in rituals, and 30% for daily use. The coarsest and thickest type is called "wrapping paper," which is made from a mixture of bamboo pulp and the straw from last year's late rice crop.

As for the writing paper (*jian* paper) made in places like Qianshan county, it is made entirely from fine bamboo raw material, formed into a thick sheet on the mold, and commands a high price. The highest quality is called "official stationery" (*guan jian*). Wealthy and noble families use it for their name cards (visiting cards).

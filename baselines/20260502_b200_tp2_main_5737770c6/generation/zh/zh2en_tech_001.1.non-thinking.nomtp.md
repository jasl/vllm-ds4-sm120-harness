**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo paper-making is done in the south, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After bamboo shoots grow, people inspect mountain hollows of varying depths. Bamboo that is about to sprout branches and leaves is the best material. Around the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), they go up the mountain to cut the bamboo, chopping it into lengths of 5 to 7 *chi* (roughly 1.7–2.3 m). After soaking for over 100 days, they pound and wash the bamboo to remove the coarse shell and green skin (this step is called "killing the green"). What remains is the bamboo fiber, which looks like ramie. They make a paste from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the fibers with it, put them into a *huang* bucket (a large cylindrical vat), and boil them over a fire for exactly eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, they use a pot about 4 *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. The rim of the pot is built up with clay and lime into a high, wide collar, similar to the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong, and can hold over ten *dan* (roughly 1,000 liters) of water. The *huang* bucket, placed on top as a cover, is 15 *chi* (5 m) in circumference and over 4 *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. After covering and boiling for a full eight days, they let the fire cool for one day, then open the bucket and take out the bamboo pulp. They wash it in a clean pond lined on the bottom and all four sides with tightly fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this lining is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, they mix the pulp with wood-ash lye, put it back into a pot, level it, and spread a one-*cun* (3.3 cm) layer of rice straw ash on top. When the liquid in the bucket boils, they transfer the pulp to another bucket and continue pouring the ash lye over it. If the liquid cools, they reheat it and pour again. This process repeats for over ten days, until the fibers naturally rot and break down. They then take the pulp out and pound it in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers) until it becomes smooth as mud, and finally pour it into a vat.

The paper-making vat is rectangular at the top. Its width and length depend on the mold, and the mold depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, clear water is added to the vat until the pulp floats about 3 *cun* (10 cm) below the surface. They add a "paper drug" solution (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves—regional names vary), which makes the fibers bond evenly and the paper white when dried. The paper mold is made from extremely fine, polished bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is stretched flat over a rectangular frame. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts it so the pulp spreads evenly over the screen. Thickness depends on technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a heavy lift thick paper. As the pulp settles on the screen, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The worker then flips the mold onto a board, depositing a sheet of paper. Thousands of sheets are stacked layer by layer. When the stack is complete, a board is placed on top, and a lever is tightened with ropes (like pressing wine) to squeeze out all remaining water. For drying, a narrow chamber is built from brick walls. The floor inside the chamber is paved with bricks, leaving one brick gap every few bricks. A fire is lit at one end of the chamber, and heat seeps through the gaps, heating all the outer bricks. Wet sheets are pasted onto the hot bricks one by one, dried, and then lifted off as finished sheets.

In modern times, wide-format paper called "Big Four-Lien" is prized for writing and documents. Waste paper—washed clean of red ink and dirt, soaked until disintegrated, and re-processed in the vat—can be remade into paper, saving all the previous boiling and soaking steps, with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered worthwhile. In the north, even scraps and corners are picked up and remade into "returned-soul paper." Both bamboo paper and bark paper, fine or coarse, follow these same principles.

For *huo* paper (spirit paper) and rough paper, the process—cutting bamboo, boiling pulp, and applying ash lye—is the same. The only difference is that after lifting from the mold, the sheets are not dried over heat; they are pressed to remove water and then dried in the sun.

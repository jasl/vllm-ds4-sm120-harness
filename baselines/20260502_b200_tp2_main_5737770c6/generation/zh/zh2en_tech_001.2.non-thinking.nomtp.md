Soak for over 100 days, then pound and wash to remove the coarse outer shell and green skin (this is called "killing the green"). The remaining bamboo core looks like ramie fiber. Make a solution of good lime and coat the bamboo with it, then put it into a large vat for boiling. The fire should be kept going for eight days and eight nights.

The boiling pot is about 4 *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. The vat cover is 1.5 *zhang* (5 m) in circumference and 4 *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. After sealing, boil for exactly eight days. Let it rest for one day, then open the vat and take out the bamboo pulp. Rinse it in a clean, water-filled pond, whose bottom and sides are lined with wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (not necessary for coarse paper).

After washing, mix the pulp with ash (made from firewood) and put it back into a pot. Spread it flat, cover with a layer of rice straw ash about 1 *cun* (3 cm) thick. When the water in the pot boils, take the pulp out into another container, and keep pouring the hot ash-water over it again. If the water cools, reheat it and pour again. Do this for over ten days; the pulp will naturally rot and soften. Then take it out and pound it in a mortar (mountain regions have water-powered trip-hammers) until it becomes like mud, then pour it into a vat.

**The paper-scooping vat** is square on top, with width depending on the screen, which in turn depends on the desired paper size. The bamboo pulp is mixed with clean water in the vat, about 3 *cun* (10 cm) deep, and a plant-based sizing liquid is added (made from leaves resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with local names varying). This makes the fibers separate and turn white when dried.

The screen ("paper curtain") is made from finely scraped bamboo strips woven together. It is stretched over a rectangular frame. The worker holds the screen with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts some bamboo pulp onto the screen. The thickness depends on the technique: a light lift gives thin paper, a heavy lift gives thick paper. Water drains through the screen edges back into the vat. Then the screen is flipped to deposit the paper onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked.

When enough sheets are stacked, a board is placed on top, and a lever is tightened (like pressing wine) to squeeze out water. After drying, each sheet is lifted with fine copper tweezers and dried over heat.

**Drying paper:** Build a brick-lined corridor, leaving gaps between bricks on the floor. Light a fire at one end; the heat passes through the gaps, heating the bricks. Wet sheets are stuck onto the hot bricks to dry, then removed and bundled into quires.

In recent times, wide-format paper called "big four-link" has been valued for writing. Waste paper, with ink and vermilion cleaned off, can be soaked, rotted, and re-processed in the vat as if new, saving the initial boiling and soaking steps. This produces paper with little loss.

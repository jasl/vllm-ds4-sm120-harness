**Making Bamboo Paper**
*(from *Tiangong Kaiwu*, public domain)*

Bamboo paper is made in the south, especially in Fujian. When bamboo shoots have grown, choose bamboo that is about to sprout branches and leaves—this is the best material. Around the *Mangzhong* (Grain in Ear) solar term, cut the bamboo on the mountain, chop it into pieces 5–7 *chi* (~1.7–2.3 m) long, and dig a pit nearby. Fill the pit with water and soak the bamboo. If the pit might dry out, use bamboo pipes to keep a steady flow of water.

Soak it for over 100 days, then pound and wash it to remove the outer bark and green skin (this step is called *shaqing*, “killing the green”). The inner fiber, called *zhu rang* (bamboo pulp), looks like ramie. Make a paste from good lime dissolved in water, coat the fibers with it, put them in a wooden vat (*heng tong*), and boil for exactly 8 days and 8 nights.

**Boiling setup:** Use a cauldron about 4 *chi* (~1.3 m) in diameter. Seal its rim with a mixture of clay and lime, making it wide and high like the salt-boiling pans in Guangxi. It holds over 10 *dan* (~600 liters) of water. Cover it with a wooden vat that is 1.5 *zhang* (~5 m) in circumference and over 4 *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter. After 8 days of boiling, let it rest for 1 day. Open the vat, take out the bamboo pulp, and wash it in a clean soaking pit. The bottom and sides of the pit must be lined with tightly joined wooden planks (to prevent mud from spoiling the pulp; this is not needed for coarse paper).

After washing, mix the pulp with wood-ash slurry, then put it back into the cauldron. Spread it flat and cover with a *cun* (~3 cm) layer of rice-straw ash. When the water in the vat boils, remove the pulp and transfer to another vat, pouring the ash-water over it again. If the water cools, reheat it and pour again. Repeat this for over 10 days, until the pulp rots and stinks. Then take it out and pound it in a mortar (mountain regions use water-powered trip-hammers, *shui dui*). Pound until it becomes like mud, then pour it into a vat.

**The paper-making vat:** The vat is square at the top, with dimensions matching the size of the mold (*lian*, a bamboo screen), which in turn depends on the paper size. Put the pounded pulp into the vat, add clear water so that about 3 *cun* (~10 cm) of water covers the surface. Then add “paper medicine” (a plant-juice solution, made from leaves like peach or bamboo leaves—local names vary). This makes the water drain away and leaves white paper.

**The mold:** It is made of extremely fine, polished bamboo strips woven into a screen, stretched on a frame with crossbars. Hold the mold with both hands, dip it into the vat, and lift up the pulp, letting it spread evenly on the screen. Thickness depends on the skill of the maker—a gentle shake makes thin paper; a heavy shake makes thick paper. As the pulp floats on the screen, water drains through the edges back into the vat. Then turn the mold over to transfer the wet sheet onto a board. Stack thousands of sheets. When the stack is thick enough, place a board on top, tighten ropes and insert a lever to press out the water (like pressing wine). After the water is fully pressed out, lift each sheet with fine copper tweezers and dry it.

**Drying:** Build a brick chamber with a hollow wall. Leave gaps between the bricks at the bottom, with a fire hole at one end. Light a fire inside; heat rises through the gaps, warming the outer bricks. Paste the wet sheets onto the hot bricks to dry, then peel them off to form a stack.

In recent times, wide sheets called *da si lian* (“big four-fold”) are valued for writing and books. Waste paper can be washed of ink and stains, soaked, rotted, and re-pulped in the vat, skipping the boiling and soaking steps, yet still making usable paper with little loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not done. But in the north, every scrap of paper is collected and remade—this is called *huan hun zhi* (“soul-returning paper”). The same applies to both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse.

For **fire paper** (used as spirit money) and coarse paper, the steps are the same: cut bamboo, boil the pulp, ash-soak, and water-wash. But after removing from the mold, they are not dried on a hot brick wall. Instead, press out water and sun-dry.

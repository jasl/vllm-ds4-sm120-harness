**Making Bamboo Paper**

The production of bamboo paper is a practice of the southern regions, and Fujian province is especially renowned for it. When the bamboo shoots have grown, one observes the depth of the mountain valleys. Bamboo that is about to sprout branches and leaves is considered the best material. Around the time of the Grain in Ear solar term (early June), people go into the mountains to cut it down. The bamboo is cut into lengths of five to seven *chi* (roughly 1.7 to 2.3 meters). On the same mountain, a pit is dug and filled with water for soaking the bamboo. To prevent the pit water from drying up, bamboo pipes are used to channel a continuous stream of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is beaten and washed to remove the coarse outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green"). The inner bamboo fiber, now resembling ramie, is then coated with a paste made from high-quality lime dissolved in water, placed in a large wooden vat, and boiled over a fire for a full eight days and nights.

The boiling vessel is a pot about four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters) in diameter. Its rim is built up with clay and lime to form a high edge, similar to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, holding over ten *shi* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. A large wooden vat, fifteen *chi* (approx. 5 meters) in circumference and over four *chi* in diameter, covers the pot. After eight days of boiling, the fire is stopped for one day. The vat is then opened, and the cooked bamboo pulp (now called "bamboo hemp" or *zhuma*) is removed and taken to a clean washing pit. The bottom and sides of this pit are lined with tightly fitted wooden boards to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is mixed with a slurry of wood ash and placed back into a pot. It is leveled, and a layer of dry rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3.3 cm) thick is spread on top. When the water in the pot boils, the liquid is ladled out and poured back over the pulp in a different container. If the liquid cools, it is reheated and poured again. This process is repeated for over ten days, until the bamboo fibers naturally rot and soften. The softened pulp is then taken out and beaten in a mortar (water-powered trip-hammers, or *shuidui*, are common in mountainous areas) until it becomes a paste as fine as mud. This paste is then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat is rectangular. Its dimensions are determined by the size of the mold, which in turn determines the size of the paper. In the vat, the bamboo paste is covered by about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) of clear water. A papermaking liquor (made from a plant resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name) is added. This causes the water to drain, leaving pure white fibers. The papermaking mold is a frame strung with a screen made of extremely fine, polished bamboo wires. When in use, the screen is opened and supported by a framework. The papermaker holds the mold with both hands and dips it into the vat, lifting a layer of fibers onto the screen. Paper thickness depends on the handler's technique: a gentle lift makes thin paper, a vigorous lift makes thick paper. As the fiber layer floats on the screen, water drains back into the vat from all sides. The mold is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet of paper onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. Once the stack is complete, a board is placed on top, and a lever-and-rope system (like pressing wine) is used to squeeze out the remaining water. After pressing, each sheet is lifted with light, fine copper tweezers and dried.

For drying, a brick chamber with a narrow inner channel is built. The floor of the channel is paved with bricks; every few bricks, one is left out. A fire is lit at one end of the chamber, and the hot air and flames pass through the gaps between the bricks, heating the outer wall surface. The wet paper sheets are then pasted one by one onto the hot wall to dry, and are then gathered into quires.

In recent times, the wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" has been valued for correspondence. Waste paper, after having its ink and vermilion markings washed away, can be soaked, rotted, and returned to the vat. This process saves all the previous steps of cooking and soaking, and the resulting paper is quite usable with little loss of material. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not commonly done. In the north, even small scraps of paper on the ground are collected and remade into so-called "returned-soul paper" (*huanhun zhi*). This principle applies to both bamboo and bark papers, fine and coarse.

As for “fire paper” and coarse paper, the process of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and treating it with ash and water is the same. However, after the sheet is lifted from the mold, it is not dried on a heated wall but simply pressed to remove water and then dried in the sun.

In the glorious Tang Dynasty (618–907 CE), spiritual and religious practices were widespread, and paper money was burned as a substitute for silk cloth (in the north, cut strips of paper were used, called "board money"). Paper made for this purpose was called "fire paper" or spirit money. A common custom in the Chu region is to burn up to a thousand *jin* (approx. 600 kg) in a single ritual. About 70% of this paper is for burning for the spirits; the other 30% is for daily use. The coarsest and thickest kind is called "wrapping paper," made from a mixture of bamboo pulp and straw from late-season rice. The fine letter paper made in Qianshan and other counties, however, is made entirely from fine bamboo material, formed into a thick sheet to command a high price. The highest grade is called "official letter paper." Wealthy families use it for their name cards. It is thick and strong, without fiber clumps. When dyed red for festive name cards, the paper is first treated with a solution of alum, and then with a dye made from safflower juice.

**Making Bamboo Paper**

The making of bamboo paper originates in the south, and Fujian province is especially renowned for it. After bamboo shoots have grown, one inspects the depth of the mountain valleys. They cut the bamboo into pieces five to seven *chi* (approx. 1.7–2.3 meters) long. Right there on the mountain, they dig a pond, fill it with water, and soak the bamboo. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is beaten and washed to remove the coarse shell and green bark (this is called "killing the green" *shā qīng*). The inner bamboo fiber then looks like ramie. High-quality lime is made into a liquid paste, applied to the fibers, and they are placed into a *huang* vat for boiling. The fire is maintained for a standard period of eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, a cauldron with a diameter of four *chi* (approx. 1.3 m) is used. The rim of the cauldron is sealed with a mixture of mud and lime, making it tall and wide, similar in shape to the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong. It can hold over ten *dan* (approx. 600+ liters) of water. An inverted *huang* vat, with a circumference of 15 *chi* (approx. 5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi*, is placed on top as a lid. Once sealed for boiling, eight days are sufficient. The fire is stopped for one day, then the lid is removed, and the bamboo pulp (*zhú má*) is taken out. It is washed clean in a clear-water washing pond. The bottom and four sides of this pond are completely lined with planks fitted tightly together to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is treated with a wood-ash [lye] slurry. It is then put back into a cauldron, leveled off, and covered with about an inch of dry rice straw ash. When the water in the vat boils, this lye water is ladled out and poured over the pulp in another vat. If the liquid cools, it is heated to a boil again before pouring. This process is repeated for over ten days, and the pulp naturally rots and becomes soft. It is then taken out and placed in a mortar for pounding (mountain regions all have water-powered trip-hammers *shuǐ duì*). It is pounded until it resembles mud in consistency, then poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat (*chāo zhǐ cáo*) is square on top. Its dimensions depend on the mold frame, and the mold frame's dimensions depend on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. A "paper medicine" liquid [a plant-based mucilage] is added (it looks like peach or bamboo leaves, and its local name varies). This causes the water to drain cleanly, leaving white paper. The papermaking mold (*lián*) is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven together. When opened and stretched, it has a supporting frame underneath. The worker holds the mold by both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts the bamboo pulp, letting it settle onto the mold's surface. The thickness of the sheet is controlled by the worker's technique: a gentle lift makes a thin sheet, a vigorous lift makes a thick sheet. As the bamboo fibers float on the mold, water drains from all four sides back into the vat. The mold is then turned over, depositing the wet sheet onto a board. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When the stack is thick enough, a board is placed on top, and it is pressed with a lever and rope, similar to pressing wine, to squeeze out all the water. After drying, the sheets are lifted one by one with fine, lightweight copper tweezers and dried on a heated wall. For drying the paper, a brick chamber with a narrow passage is built. The floor of the passage is laid with bricks, leaving every few bricks missing a space for a vent. A fire is lit from the opening at one end; the heat passes through the gaps between the bricks, heating the outer wall. The wet sheets are pasted one by one onto the hot wall to dry, then lifted off to form quires.

In recent times, a wide format called "Big Four-Link" has been used for important official documents and books. Waste paper, after washing off red ink and dirt, can be soaked, rotted, put back into the vat, and remade into paper. This process skips the earlier boiling and soaking steps, yet still produces paper with little material loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not often practiced. In the north, even inch-long scraps and corner pieces are picked up and remade into "Returned Soul Paper" (*huán hún zhǐ*). The principle is the same for both bamboo and bark papers, whether fine or coarse. For "fire paper" (*huǒ zhǐ*, spirit money) and rough paper, the steps of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and treating it with ash-lixivium and water are the same as before. The only difference is that after the sheet is removed from the mold, it is not dried on a heated wall. Instead, after pressing out the water, it is simply sun-dried.

During the height of the Tang Dynasty, spirit worship was common, and paper money was used to replace burned silk fabric. (In the north, cut strips are used, called "board money" *bǎn qián*.) So, this type of paper is called "fire paper" (*huǒ zhǐ*). In the modern custom of the Jing-Chu region, some burn as much as a thousand *jin* (approx. 600 kg) in a single ceremony. 70% of this paper is used for spirit offerings, 30% for daily use. The coarsest and thickest kind is called "wrapping paper" (*bāo guǒ zhǐ*), which is made from bamboo pulp mixed with leftover rice straw from the previous year's late crop. The fine letter paper (*jiǎn zhǐ*) made in places like Yanshan county is made entirely from fine bamboo material, formed thickly on the mold to fetch a high price. The best quality is called "official letter paper" (*guān jiǎn*). Wealthy families use it for their calling cards. This paper is firm and thick, with no fibrous streaks. To make red "auspicious note" paper, the sheets are first sized with alum water and then dyed with safflower juice.

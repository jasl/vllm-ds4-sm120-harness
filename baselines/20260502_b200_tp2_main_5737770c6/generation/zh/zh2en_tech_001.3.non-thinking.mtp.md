Soak the bamboo for over a hundred days, then beat and wash it to remove the coarse outer shell and green bark (this step is called "killing the green" or *sha qing*). The remaining bamboo fiber will look like ramie fibers. Make a paste from high-quality lime dissolved in water, coat the fibers with it, and put them into a wooden vat ( *huang tong* ) for boiling. The boiling fire should be maintained for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling the bamboo, use a cauldron with a diameter of four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters). Seal the rim of the cauldron with a mixture of mud and lime, making a wide, high edge similar in shape to the salt-boiling pans used in Guangdong. This cauldron can hold over ten *shi* (approx. 1000 liters) of water. Cover it with the large wooden vat, which has a circumference of 15 *chi* (approx. 5 meters) and a diameter of over four *chi* (approx. 1.3 meters). Once sealed and boiling, eight days is sufficient. Let the fire stop for one day, then open the vat and remove the bamboo pulp ( *zhu ma* ). Wash it clean in a clean soaking pit. The bottom and all four sides of this pit must be lined with tightly fitted wood planks to prevent mud contamination (this is unnecessary for making coarse paper). After washing, coat the pulp with plant-ash lye (water leached from wood or grass ashes). Put it back into a cauldron, level the surface, and spread a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (approx. 3 cm) thick on top. When the liquid in the cauldron boils, transfer the pulp to another vat and continue to pour the hot ash lye over it. If the liquid gets cold, reheat it to boiling before pouring again. Do this for over ten days, and the fibers will naturally rot and soften. Then, remove the pulp and beat it in a mortar (mountain regions all use water-powered trip-hammers or *shui dui* ), beating it until it has the consistency of mud. Finally, pour this beaten pulp into a vat (the papermaking vat or *chao zhi cao*).

The papermaking vat is a rectangular trough. Its width and length depend on the size of the mold ( *lian* ), which in turn depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, fill the vat with clear water until it stands about three *cun* (approx. 10 cm) above the pulp. Add a "paper medicine" liquid (a plant-based mucilage, often from the leaves of plants like *Actinidia chinensis* or *Hibiscus manihot*; local names vary). This additive helps the water drain away cleanly, leaving white paper.

The paper mold is made of very finely scraped and polished bamboo strips woven into a mat. It is stretched flat over a supporting frame with crossbars. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts and shakes it, catching a layer of bamboo pulp on the mold's surface. The thickness of the paper is controlled by the worker's skill: a gentle shake makes thin paper, a heavy shake makes thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the mold, water drains from the four sides back into the vat. Then, the mold is turned over (*fu lian*), depositing the wet sheet of paper onto a stack on a board. This is repeated to build a stack of thousands of sheets.

When the stack is high enough, place a board on top and press it, using a rope and lever (like a wine press), to squeeze out all the water. Then, use fine copper tweezers to lift each sheet individually and dry it on a heated wall. To build the drying wall (*bei zhi*), first construct a hollow, double-walled corridor (a "flue") from brick, leaving gaps in the floor bricks. A fire is lit in a firebox at one end; the hot air and smoke pass through the gaps, heating the entire brick wall. Wet sheets are pasted one by one onto the hot wall to dry. Once dry, they are peeled off and gathered into quires.

In recent times, a wide-format paper called "Big Four-Link" has been valued for writing and official documents. Waste paper (with ink and vermillion writing) can be washed, soaked until it rots, and then put back into the vat to be remade into paper. This saves all the previous steps of boiling and soaking the raw bamboo. The loss is not great. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important.

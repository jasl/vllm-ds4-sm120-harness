**Making Bamboo Paper**

Bamboo paper is made in southern China, but Fujian province is especially known for it. After the bamboo shoots grow, the best material comes from bamboo that is about to sprout leaves (i.e., just before branching). Around the time of the Grain in Ear solar term (*Mangzhong*, early June), the bamboo is cut in the mountains. It is chopped into pieces five to seven *chi* (roughly 1.7–2.3 m) long. A pond is dug right there on the mountain, filled with water, and the bamboo is soaked in it. To keep the pond from drying up, bamboo pipes are used to channel a constant flow of water into it. After soaking for over a hundred days, the bamboo is beaten and washed to remove the coarse outer shell and green skin (this step is called *shaqing*, "killing the green"). The remaining bamboo fiber looks like ramie. It is then coated with a paste made from high-quality lime dissolved in water, placed in a vat, and boiled over a fire for eight days and eight nights.

For boiling, a cauldron about four *chi* (1.3 m) in diameter is used. Its rim is sealed with a mixture of mud and lime, making it wide and deep like the salt-boiling pans of Guangdong, capable of holding over ten *shi* (roughly 1000 liters) of water. A large cylindrical vat (called *huangtong*) is placed on top; it has a circumference of 15 *chi* (5 m) and a diameter of over four *chi* (1.3 m). After sealing, the boiling continues for eight full days. Then the fire is stopped for one day. The lid is removed, and the cooked bamboo pulp (called *zhuma*) is taken out and washed clean in a clear-water pond. The pond's bottom and sides are lined with tightly fitted wooden planks to prevent mud contamination (this is not necessary for making coarse paper). After washing, the pulp is mixed with wood-ash lye and put back into a cauldron. It is leveled off, and a layer of rice straw ash about one *cun* (3.3 cm) thick is spread on top. When the water in the vat boils, the liquid is ladled out into another vat, and the ash lye is poured back over the pulp. If the liquid cools, it is reheated and poured again. This is repeated for over ten days, until the pulp naturally rots and softens. It is then taken out and beaten in a mortar (in mountainous areas, water-powered trip-hammers are used) until it becomes a paste as fine as mud. Finally, it is poured into a vat.

The papermaking vat is rectangular on top. Its width and length depend on the mold, and the mold depends on the desired paper size. Once the bamboo pulp is ready, the vat is filled with clear water to about three *cun* (10 cm) above the pulp. A papermaking agent (made from a plant resembling peach or bamboo leaves, with no fixed local name) is added; this makes the water drain cleanly, leaving pure white paper.

The paper mold (*lian*) is made of very finely scraped bamboo strips woven into a screen. It is stretched over a rectangular frame that can be opened and closed. The worker holds the mold with both hands, dips it into the vat, and lifts some pulp onto the screen. Thickness is controlled by hand: a gentle lift gives thin paper, a heavy lift gives thick paper. As the bamboo fibers float on the screen, water drains through the sides back into the vat. The mold is then turned over onto a board, depositing a sheet of paper. Thousands of sheets are stacked this way. When enough sheets have accumulated, a board is placed on top, and a lever is used to press out the water (like pressing wine). After pressing, each sheet is lifted with fine copper tweezers and dried on a heated wall.

For drying, a double wall is built from clay bricks. The floor of the passage between the walls is covered with bricks except for gaps left every few bricks. A fire is lit at one end; the hot air and smoke pass through the gaps, heating the outer wall. Damp sheets are pasted onto the hot wall one by one. Once dry, they are removed and gathered into stacks.

In recent times, wide-format paper called *da si lian* has been valued for writing and books. Waste paper—even if stained with red ink or ink—can be washed, soaked until rotten, and remade into pulp, skipping the previous soaking and boiling steps. This still yields usable paper with little loss. In the south, where bamboo is cheap, this is not considered important. But in the north, even small scraps are collected and remade into "returned-soul paper" (*huanhun zhi*). Both bamboo and bark papers, fine or coarse, follow the same principle.

For spirit paper (*huo zhi*) and rough paper (*cao zhi*), the steps of cutting bamboo, boiling the pulp, and treating it with ash lye are the same. However, after the sheets are lifted from the mold, they are not dried on a heated wall. Instead, they are pressed to remove water and then sun-dried.

During the Tang dynasty (618–907 AD), spirit worship was widespread. Paper money replaced burned silk cloth (in the north, cut strips called *ban qian* were used). This kind of paper is called *huo zhi* (fire paper). In the modern custom of Hubei and Hunan, a single burning may consume up to a thousand *jin* (about 600 kg) of such paper. About 70% of it is used for funeral offerings, 30% for daily use. The coarsest, thickest type is wrapping paper, made by mixing bamboo pulp with old, late-season rice straw. The letter paper (*jian zhi*) made in Yanshan County and elsewhere is entirely from fine bamboo material, made thick by heavy scooping, and sold at high prices. The best grade is called *guan jian* (official letter paper). Wealthy families use it for calling cards; it is thick and free of fiber lumps. For red festive cards, the paper is first treated with alum water, then dyed with safflower juice.

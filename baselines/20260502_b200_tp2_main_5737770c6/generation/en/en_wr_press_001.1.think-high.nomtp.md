**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New Pollinator Path Education Trail**

**HARBORLEAF, MD – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today opened the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile public education trail designed to help visitors understand and support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. Located on the garden’s east meadow loop, the fully accessible trail features interactive learning stations, native plant demonstration beds, and tactile signage intended to translate scientific concepts into practical, at-home gardening actions.

The trail opens at a time when public interest in pollinator conservation has grown significantly. Garden staff report that visitors have increasingly requested hands-on, actionable guidance for creating pollinator-friendly spaces in their own yards, balconies, and community gardens. The Pollinator Path directly addresses this demand by offering a sequence of outdoor learning experiences that demonstrate how small, informed planting decisions can collectively strengthen regional ecosystems.

“Visitors have told us they want to help pollinators but don’t always know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path gives them concrete, manageable steps. We show that a single pot of native milkweed on an apartment balcony or a cluster of wild bergamot in a suburban garden can make a real difference. When neighbors make those same small choices, we create connected habitats that support wildlife across entire neighborhoods.”

**Trail Features and Accessibility**

The 0.8-mile loop is paved and graded to accommodate wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. Every 200 feet, the trail offers shaded resting points with benches and interpretive panels. Key features include:

- **Native Plant Demonstration Beds**: Ten curated plots showcase Pennsylvania sedge, butterfly weed, purple coneflower, and other species naturally beneficial to local pollinators. Each bed includes a companion guide explaining bloom times, host plant relationships, and soil requirements.

- **Night-Blooming Flower Station**: A dedicated twilight area highlights evening primrose, moonflower, and other nocturnal blooms that support moths and night-flying insects. Subtle low-level lighting allows evening viewing without disrupting pollinator activity.

- **Tactile Signage and Youth Observation Cards**: Braille and raised-text panels accompany each interpretive sign, providing non-visual access to trail content. Visitors ages 5–12 can pick up free observation cards that prompt them to identify pollinator species, note flower shapes, and record behavioral patterns.

- **Volunteer-Led Weekend Walks**: Beginning July 19, trained volunteer educators will lead guided walks every Saturday and Sunday at 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. These 45-minute tours focus on seasonal pollinator activity and offer personalized planting advice.

The trail’s design incorporates research from the Garden’s horticultural team, who consulted peer-reviewed studies on pollinator foraging ranges, native plant phenology, and the impact of habitat fragmentation. The east meadow location was chosen for its existing diversity of wildflowers and its proximity to the Garden’s main entrance, allowing easy access for school groups and families.

**Educational Goals and Community Impact**

Harborleaf Botanical Garden emphasizes that the Pollinator Path is a nonpolitical, science-based educational resource. The trail does not advocate legislation or endorse specific products. Instead, it focuses on ecological principles: plants that provide nectar and larval host material, the importance of bloom succession from early spring to late fall, and the role of pesticide-free gardening practices.

Ortiz noted that the trail’s content is designed to be inclusive of all experience levels. “Whether you are a master gardener or someone who has never planted a seed, you will find something useful here,” she said. “Our goal is not to overwhelm, but to inspire. Every sign, every planting bed, every observation card is a conversation starter about how we share our landscapes with the creatures that keep our food systems and ecosystems healthy.”

The Garden plans to update the trail’s interpretive materials annually to reflect changes in pollinator populations and emerging research. Volunteers will rotate observation station activities each season, including spring “nest-spotting” for solitary bees and fall “seed harvest” demonstrations.

**Opening Day Events**

To mark the opening, the Garden is hosting a family-friendly event from 9 a.m. to 4 p.m. on July 12. Activities include a guided scavenger hunt along the trail, a native plant “swap table” for visitors to exchange seeds and cuttings, and a pollinator-themed story time for young children at 11 a.m. and 1 p.m. Admission to the Garden is free for members and included with regular daytime admission for non-members.

The Pollinator Path is now open daily from dawn to dusk. Weekend volunteer-led walks begin July 19; no advance registration is required.

**About Harborleaf Botanical Garden**

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a public institution dedicated to plant conservation, education, and ecological stewardship. The Garden manages more than 40 acres of curated landscapes, including woodlands, wetlands, and restored meadows, and serves as a research site for native plant ecology. Its learning programs reach more than 15,000 students and community members annually through school field trips, adult workshops, and public events.

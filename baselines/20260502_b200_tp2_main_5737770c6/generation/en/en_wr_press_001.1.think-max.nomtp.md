**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New “Pollinator Path” to Educate Public on Habitat Creation**

**HARBORLEAF, PA – July 1, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will officially open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on July 12, 2026, offering visitors a hands-on, accessible resource for learning how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through native plant choices. The trail, located along the east meadow loop, responds directly to visitor requests for practical, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly habitats at home.

The 0.8-mile accessible trail winds through the Garden’s east meadow, featuring a series of demonstration beds planted exclusively with native species that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host plants for regional pollinators. Key stops along the route include a night-blooming flower station, designed to highlight plants that support moths and other crepuscular pollinators, and tactile signage with raised text and botanical illustrations for visitors with visual impairments. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, encourage children to document the insects and flowers they encounter, while shaded resting points offer respite and opportunities for reflection.

The Pollinator Path will be open daily during regular Garden hours. In addition to self-guided exploration, the Garden will host volunteer-led weekend walks every Saturday and Sunday at 10:00 a.m. beginning July 12. These walks, led by trained volunteer docents, will provide deeper insights into the ecological roles of specific plants and the life cycles of common pollinators.

Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden, emphasized that the trail is designed to bridge the gap between general awareness of pollinator decline and concrete, actionable steps that individuals can take. “We kept hearing from visitors who said, ‘I know I should help pollinators, but I don’t know where to start,’” Ortiz said. “The Pollinator Path gives them a model. It shows that small planting decisions—like choosing a single milkweed over an ornamental grass, or letting a patch of clover bloom—can create connected habitats that run across neighborhoods, from one garden to the next. That cumulative effect is powerful.”

The Garden developed the trail based on feedback collected during visitor surveys and program evaluations conducted over the past two years. Many respondents expressed interest in learning about plants that support specific pollinator species, as well as how to select native alternatives for common ornamental plants. The demonstration beds include species such as butterfly weed, wild bergamot, purple coneflower, and evening primrose, all of which are native to the region and widely adaptable to home gardens.

The night-blooming flower station, a feature unique to the Pollinator Path, will showcase plants such as moonflower, night-blooming jasmine, and evening stock, which open their flowers in the late afternoon and evening to attract moths. Tactile signage at this station includes raised diagrams of moth antennae and flower shapes, providing an inclusive educational experience for visitors with low vision. Youth observation cards, printed on recycled paper, prompt children to identify flower colors, insect visitors, and weather conditions, with space for drawings and notes.

The trail surface is constructed with compacted, permeable material to accommodate wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility devices, and the gradient remains gentle throughout the loop. Benches made from reclaimed wood are positioned at intervals, shaded by existing tree canopy. The Garden has also installed small interpretive panels in braille and large-print text at each station, ensuring that non-sighted and low-vision visitors can access the content independently.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will rotate through different themes each month, including “Moon Moth Monday” orientations at the night-blooming station, “Bee Basics” focusing on solitary and native bee identification, and “Butterfly Life Cycles” highlighting host plants. The walks are free with Garden admission, and no advance registration is required. Groups of ten or more are encouraged to contact the Garden to schedule a private tour.

The Pollinator Path is the most recent addition to Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s public programming, which includes school field trips, adult workshops, and community gardening initiatives. The Garden, which spans 120 acres in eastern Pennsylvania, maintains collections of native plants, woody ornamentals, and heritage gardens.

“We are not advocating for any single approach to gardening,” Ortiz said. “We are simply showing that when you plant with intention—when you choose native flowers and trees that evolved alongside local insects—you create a stepping stone. That stone connects to the next garden, and the next, forming a web of life that benefits both wildlife and people. The Pollinator Path is a teaching tool for that idea.”

The Garden will host a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at the east meadow trailhead on July 12 at 9:30 a.m., followed by the first volunteer-led weekend walk at 10:00 a.m. The event is open to the public.

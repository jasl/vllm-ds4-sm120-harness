**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New Pollinator Path to Educate Visitors on Backyard Habitat Creation**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the official opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile public education trail designed to provide visitors with practical, hands-on guidance for supporting bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The trail, located on the east meadow loop of the garden, is fully accessible and will be free with regular garden admission beginning immediately.

The Pollinator Path addresses a growing demand from Harborleaf’s visitors for concrete, actionable information on how to turn small residential gardens into productive pollinator habitats. Over the past two years, garden staff recorded a significant increase in questions from guests seeking advice on native plant selection and garden design that specifically benefits local pollinator species. The new trail is intended to serve as a living classroom, demonstrating that even modest planting decisions can contribute to a broader network of connected habitats across neighborhoods.

“We heard repeatedly from our visitors that they want to help pollinators but are unsure where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that small planting decisions—choosing one native shrub over a non-native ornamental, or leaving a patch of bare soil for ground-nesting bees—can create connected habitats that stretch from our garden into your backyard and beyond.”

The trail, which winds through the east meadow, features nine distinct demonstration stations. Each station is anchored by native plant demonstration beds that showcase species known to support local pollinator life cycles, including larval host plants and nectar-rich blooms. Among the highlights is a night-blooming flower station, designed to attract moths and other nocturnal pollinators. The station features plants such as evening primrose and moonflower, with interpretive signage noting the unique importance of nighttime pollination.

To ensure accessibility for all visitors, the path incorporates tactile signage at each station. The signs include embossed illustrations of key plant and insect species, as well as braille descriptions, allowing visitors with visual impairments to engage with the content. Youth observation cards are also available at the trailhead, offering children a guided activity to identify pollinators and document their sightings along the route. Shaded resting points, placed at regular intervals, provide respite for visitors, with benches positioned to offer clear views of the meadow and its visiting wildlife.

The garden also announced a volunteer-led weekend walk program, beginning Saturday, July 19. Trained docents will lead small groups along the Pollinator Path every Saturday and Sunday at 10 a.m. and 2 p.m. through October. The walks are free with admission and do not require advance registration, though space may be limited depending on group size.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden designed the Pollinator Path in collaboration with local conservation ecologists and landscape architects, though all funding has been provided by the garden’s existing operating budget. The organization has emphasized that the trail is educational in nature, focused on scientific principles of plant-pollinator relationships, and does not advocate for any specific policy or political position.

“We are not here to tell people what to do with their gardens,” Ortiz added. “We are here to share what the science shows: that when we plant with pollinators in mind, we strengthen the entire ecosystem. The moth that visits your windowsill planter tonight might be flying from a garden five blocks away. That connection matters.”

The opening of the Pollinator Path marks the completion of a years-long effort to revitalize the east meadow as an active educational space. Previously used primarily as a passive green area, the meadow now serves as a demonstration site for sustainable landscaping practices that balance beauty with ecological function. Garden staff note that the trail is designed to be self-guided but that the weekend walks will offer deeper engagement for those seeking it.

For visitors planning to explore the trail, Harborleaf recommends comfortable walking shoes and sun protection. The path is paved and graded to accommodate wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers. Youth observation cards and a simple identification guide are available at the admissions desk.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is open daily from 9 a.m. to 6 p.m. through September. Admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under five. Members of the garden receive unlimited free entry.

**About Harborleaf Botanical Garden**

Founded in 1988, Harborleaf Botanical Garden is a nonprofit public garden dedicated to plant conservation, environmental education, and the preservation of regional flora. Located on 42 acres in central California, the garden features more than 3,000 plant species in curated collections, including a century-old oak grove, a desert succulent house, and the newly opened Pollinator Path. Harborleaf offers year-round programs for schools, families, and adult learners. For more information, visit www.harborleafgarden.org.

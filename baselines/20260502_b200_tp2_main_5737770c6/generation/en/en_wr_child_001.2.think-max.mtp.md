Solomon was small and slow, with a shell that curled like a cinnamon roll. He carried a leaf in his mouth, and on that leaf was a map. Not a map of treasure or faraway lands, but of every single puddle in the whole garden.

Solomon flattened himself. A young sparrow tumbled out of the sky, landing in a puff of feathers right beside his puddle.

“Ow!” chirped the sparrow. She shook her head. “That was so close! I almost touched the clouds that time!”

Her name was Pip. She was small and brown, with a spot of white on her chin like a dab of milk. Her wings were strong, but her landings were—how should Solomon put it?—creative.

“You landed on my map,” said Solomon quietly.

Pip looked down. Her foot was planted right on the Three Sisters Puddles. “Oops! Sorry, slowpoke.”

Solomon didn’t mind being called slow. He was slow. But he did mind when puddles got smudged. “You need to practice your landings,” he said.

“Landings are boring!” Pip hopped from one foot to the other. “Flying is the fun part! Up and up and UP! I want to fly higher than the chimney, higher than the big oak tree!”

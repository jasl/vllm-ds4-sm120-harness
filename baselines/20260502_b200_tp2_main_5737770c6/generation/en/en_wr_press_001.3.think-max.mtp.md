Starting July 13, the garden will also launch volunteer-led weekend walks along the path, providing guided interpretation for families and community groups.

The development of the Pollinator Path was driven by a steady increase in visitor questions about practical steps to support local pollinators. According to garden staff, guests frequently asked for specific plant recommendations and design ideas that could be applied in small urban yards, school gardens, or suburban lots.

“Our visitors have been telling us for years that they want to do more to help pollinators, but they need simple, actionable guidance that fits their own space,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. It shows that even small planting decisions—like choosing a single native milkweed for your balcony pot or leaving a patch of unmowed clover—can create connected habitats that cross neighborhood boundaries and strengthen our local ecosystem.”

The trail’s design emphasizes accessibility and multi-sensory learning. The garden does not endorse any commercial products or political positions related to pollinator health, focusing instead on evidence-based gardening practices.

The east meadow loop is paved and wheelchair-accessible, with gentle slopes and rest areas every 200 feet.

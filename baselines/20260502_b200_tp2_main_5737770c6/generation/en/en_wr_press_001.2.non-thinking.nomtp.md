**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden to Open New ‘Pollinator Path’ Education Trail on July 12**

**PORT KINSEY, N.Y. — June 9, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on July 12, 2026, offering visitors an accessible 0.8-mile loop through the east meadow designed to teach practical, hands-on techniques for supporting bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds.

The trail, which begins at the eastern edge of the garden’s meadow section, aims to address a growing interest among Harborleaf’s visitors: how to translate admiration for pollinators into actionable home gardening strategies. Garden staff developed the path after repeated requests from guests seeking clearer, step-by-step guidance on selecting native plants and designing small-scale habitats.

“Visitors often tell us they want to help pollinators, but they don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is designed to show that small planting decisions — choosing one native species over another, or leaving a patch of bare soil for ground-nesting bees — can create connected habitats that stretch across an entire neighborhood.”

The trail features four primary educational components. Native plant demonstration beds line the route, showcasing species selected for their value to local pollinators at different times of the growing season. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights plants that support moths and other crepuscular insects, with interpretive signs explaining scent and color cues used by nocturnal visitors. Tactile signage along the path allows visitors with visual impairments to feel the texture of leaves, bark, and seedpods, while large-print and braille text describe each plant’s role in the local ecosystem.

Youth observation cards — laminated guides with prompts for counting bee species, sketching flower shapes, and noting flight patterns — will be available free at the trailhead. Shaded resting points with benches are positioned at quarter-mile intervals, each offering a different view of the meadow and interpretive panels on topics such as “Pollen vs. Nectar” and “Why Dead Wood Matters.”

Volunteer-led weekend walks will begin on July 13 and continue every Saturday and Sunday through October, weather permitting. Garden docents trained in pollinator ecology will lead small groups along the trail, demonstrating how to identify common bee genera, distinguish butterfly species, and recognize signs of moth activity at night. Weekend walks are included with regular garden admission and do not require advance registration.

The east meadow loop was chosen for the trail because of its existing diversity of wildflowers, grasses, and edge habitat. Harborleaf staff have augmented the area with additional native plantings over the past two years, focusing on species that provide bloom from early spring through late fall. The trail surface is compacted crushed stone, suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers, with gentle grades and no stairs.

Ortiz emphasized that the trail’s purpose is strictly educational and nonpolitical. “We are not advocating for any particular policy or land-use regulation,” she said. “We are simply giving people the tools they need to make informed choices in their own yards, balconies, or community gardens. A single pot of lavender on an apartment patio can be a refueling stop for a migrating monarch. Our goal is to help people see those opportunities.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has been a nonprofit public garden since its founding in 1947. The organization manages 90 acres of cultivated gardens, woodlands, and meadow, with a mission to inspire environmental stewardship through plant-based education. The Pollinator Path is funded through the garden’s annual operating budget and in-kind donations of plant material from local native-plant nurseries.

The trail opens to the public at 9:00 a.m. on July 12, 2026, with a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at the east meadow trailhead. Regular garden hours are 9:00 a.m. to 5:00 p.m. Tuesday through Sunday, with extended hours until 8:00 p.m. on Thursdays from May through September. Admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under 12 and Harborleaf members.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including a downloadable trail map and the weekend walk schedule, visit the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website or contact the communications office.

# Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New Pollinator Path Education Trail, Opening July 12

**HARBORLEAF, CA – June 10, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open a new hands-on educational trail designed to teach visitors how to support local pollinators, beginning July 12, 2026. The “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile accessible loop through the east meadow, aims to answer a recurring question from guests: how to turn small home gardens into productive habitats for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds.

The trail, which encourages self-guided exploration, features native plant demonstration beds that showcase species specifically selected for their nectar and pollen value across the growing season. A dedicated night-blooming flower station will highlight plants that attract nocturnal pollinators, including moths and bats, with interpretive panels explaining the ecological roles of these often-overlooked species. Tactile signage—incorporating Braille and raised illustrations—ensures the experience is inclusive for visitors with visual impairments. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, invite children to document the insects they spot, turning a simple walk into a citizen-science activity. Shaded resting points are placed every quarter-mile, allowing for reflection and wildlife watching. On weekends, volunteer-led walks will provide guided interpretation led by trained garden docents.

Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden, said the trail was developed directly in response to visitor demand. “Again and again, people come to us after a visit and ask, ‘How can I do this at home?’ They want practical, real-world guidance on planting for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds,” Ortiz said. “The Pollinator Path meets that need by showing, not just telling. Every demonstration bed is a replicable model—a blueprint for a balcony pot, a suburban border, or a community plot. Our goal is to show that small planting decisions, made deliberately, can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods.”

The garden’s education team designed the trail to emphasize that pollinator conservation does not require large landholdings or expensive infrastructure. The demonstration beds include compact groupings of milkweed, lavender, coneflower, salvia, and native grasses—all of which thrive in typical residential conditions. Interpretive signs explain bloom timing, host-plant relationships, and pesticide-free maintenance practices. The night-blooming station, planted with moonflower, evening primrose, and night-blooming jasmine, will be illuminated with soft amber lighting to avoid disrupting insect behavior while allowing after-hours viewing during special summer evening programs.

The trail’s placement in the east meadow loop was chosen for its gentle, level terrain, making it fully accessible to visitors using wheelchairs, strollers, or mobility aids. The route is surfaced with compacted decomposed granite and has no steep grades. Benches constructed from reclaimed wood offer rest stops that double as observation platforms for watching pollinators in action. The garden’s volunteer coordinator has recruited and trained 20 weekend walk leaders to date, with plans to add evening shifts once the night-blooming station is in peak flower.

Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is intentionally nonpolitical, focusing solely on ecological education and practical horticulture. “We stick to the science and the how-to,” she said. “Our message is simple: every flower that a person plants can be a rest stop for a migrating monarch or a foraging bumblebee. When neighbors coordinate their choices—planting milkweed here, asters there—those dots become a lifeline. That’s the story we want to tell.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, a private nonprofit organization, has operated since 1982 on 65 acres of restored coastal valley habitat. The garden maintains more than 3,000 species of plants, with a focus on California natives and climate-adaptive ornamentals. Its education department serves approximately 15,000 schoolchildren annually through field trips and workshops. The Pollinator Path was funded through the garden’s existing operating budget and materials donated by local nurseries; no specific grant or external funding amount has been disclosed.

The trail will open to the public at 9 a.m. on July 12, 2026, with a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at the east meadow trailhead. Regular garden admission applies; members enter free. Weekend volunteer-led walks will begin July 13 and run every Saturday and Sunday through October, weather permitting. The trail will remain open year-round thereafter.

For more information about the Pollinator Path, including downloadable youth observation cards and a schedule of weekend walks, visit the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website or call the visitor services line.

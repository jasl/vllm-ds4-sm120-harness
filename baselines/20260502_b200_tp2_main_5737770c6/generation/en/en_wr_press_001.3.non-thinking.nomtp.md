**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New “Pollinator Path” to Teach Public About Native Plant Habitats**

**HARBORLEAF, CA — July 12, 2026** — Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its newest public education initiative, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated 0.8-mile accessible trail designed to help visitors understand how small planting choices can support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The trail, located on the east meadow loop of the garden’s grounds, officially opened to the public this morning.

The Pollinator Path was developed in direct response to a growing number of visitor inquiries seeking practical, hands-on guidance for creating habitat-friendly gardens at home. Garden staff noted that while many guests were enthusiastic about supporting pollinators, they often lacked clear information about which native plants to use, how to arrange them, or how to maintain year-round resources for these species.

“We kept hearing the same question: ‘What can I actually plant in my yard that will help the butterflies and bees?’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is our answer. It’s a living classroom that shows visitors, step by step, how even small planting decisions—like choosing one native shrub over an exotic ornamental—can create connected habitats across neighborhoods. When we link our gardens, we build corridors for pollinators to travel and thrive.”

The trail meanders through the garden’s east meadow, a site chosen for its existing ecological diversity and open access. Designed with universal accessibility in mind, the path features a smooth, packed-earth surface suitable for strollers, wheelchairs, and mobility aids. Along the route, visitors will encounter a series of interpretive stations and demonstration areas:

- **Native plant demonstration beds** showcase a curated selection of regional wildflowers, grasses, and shrubs that provide nectar, pollen, and larval host material for pollinators throughout the growing season.
- **A night-blooming flower station** highlights species that open after dusk, such as evening primrose and moonflower, which attract moths and other nocturnal pollinators. The station includes subtle low-level lighting to allow evening viewing without disrupting insect activity.
- **Tactile signage** at each station features raised text, braille, and textured plant illustrations, ensuring the trail is informative for visitors with visual impairments.
- **Youth observation cards** are available at the trailhead, encouraging children and families to record sightings of specific pollinators, note flower colors visited, and track seasonal changes. Cards can be returned to the garden’s visitor center for a small reward.
- **Shaded resting points** with benches are spaced every 0.2 miles, offering respite and opportunities for quiet observation.
- **Volunteer-led weekend walks** will be offered on Saturday and Sunday mornings throughout the summer, led by trained garden docents who can answer questions in real time and deepen visitors’ understanding of pollination ecology.

The Pollinator Path is part of Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s broader commitment to environmental education and community engagement. Unlike many commercial or political campaigns, the trail focuses solely on ecological literacy and practical gardening advice. It does not endorse any political candidates, funding sources, or celebrity ambassadors. Instead, it aims to empower individuals with knowledge they can apply in their own backyards, schoolyards, or community spaces.

“We’re not asking anyone to redesign their entire yard overnight,” Ortiz added. “We’re simply showing that one small change—a pot of native milkweed on an apartment balcony, a patch of clover in a sunny corner—can make a real difference. When thousands of people make that small change, the result is a network of tiny habitats that together support regional pollinator populations.”

The trail’s design was informed by input from local ecologists, horticulturists, and accessibility specialists. Harborleaf Botanical Garden staff worked to ensure that the path remains nonpolitical and welcoming to all visitors, regardless of gardening experience or background. The demonstration beds will be maintained by garden volunteers and updated seasonally to reflect the changing availability of blooming plants.

Visitors to the Pollinator Path can access the trail from the east meadow parking area during regular garden hours, 9 a.m. to sunset. The path is free with general garden admission, and weekend walks require no advance registration. Group tours for schools or community organizations may be arranged by contacting the learning programs office.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, established in 1972, is a nonprofit public garden dedicated to the conservation and display of native plants. Its 45-acre site includes multiple themed gardens, a research library, and year-round educational programming.

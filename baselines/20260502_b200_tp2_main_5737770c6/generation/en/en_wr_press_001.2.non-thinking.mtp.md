**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New Pollinator Path to Educate Public on Habitat Creation**

**PORTLAND, OR – June 15, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through simple, native-plant-based choices. The trail will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026, on the garden’s east meadow loop, a 0.8-mile fully accessible route.

The Pollinator Path was developed in direct response to visitor feedback. Over the past two years, Harborleaf staff noted a growing number of questions from guests seeking practical, actionable guidance on attracting and sustaining pollinators in home gardens, community spaces, and urban lots. Rather than offering generic advice, the garden designed a self-guided interpretive experience that demonstrates specific planting strategies, habitat features, and observation techniques.

“Visitors told us they wanted to know exactly which plants to put in the ground, and how to arrange them, so that a single backyard can become a meaningful link in a chain of pollinator-friendly spaces,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that small, deliberate planting decisions—like choosing a native aster over an ornamental hybrid—can create connected habitats that stretch from a windowsill planter across an entire neighborhood.”

The trail features multiple educational stations designed for all ages and abilities. Native plant demonstration beds showcase species native to the region, including Oregon sunshine, showy milkweed, goldenrod, and various penstemons, each labeled with information about its pollinator partners. A night-blooming flower station highlights plants that open after dusk, such as evening primrose and yucca, explaining how moths and bats contribute to nighttime pollination.

Tactile signage along the route provides descriptions in both large-print text and raised graphics, allowing visitors with visual impairments to feel the shape of a flower or the structure of a seed head. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, invite children to document which pollinators they see and to compare notes at stations spaced at regular intervals. Shaded resting points—benches beneath mature oaks and maples—offer places to pause, sketch, or simply watch.

To deepen visitor engagement, Harborleaf will host volunteer-led weekend walks on the Pollinator Path every Saturday and Sunday from 10:00 a.m. to 11:30 a.m., beginning July 12 and continuing through October. Trained docents will lead small groups along the loop, answering questions and demonstrating how to incorporate pollinator-friendly practices into a variety of settings, from apartment balconies to suburban yards.

The entire trail is wheelchair- and stroller-accessible, with a smooth, compacted gravel surface that meets Americans with Disabilities Act standards. Interpretive materials are available in English and Spanish, and trail maps can be downloaded from the garden’s website or picked up at the visitor center.

Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is intended as a resource for anyone—regardless of gardening experience—who wants to make a tangible difference for local wildlife. The trail does not advocate for any political position or policy; rather, it focuses on the ecological role of pollinators and the simple actions individuals can take to support them.

“We are not asking people to change their entire landscape overnight,” Ortiz said. “We are showing them that even a single pot of native sedum on a front step can provide nectar for a migrating monarch. The cumulative effect of many such small efforts is what creates resilient, connected habitats.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden plans to update the demonstration beds seasonally, rotating in spring ephemerals, summer bloomers, and fall nectar sources to reflect the changing needs of pollinators throughout the year. Garden staff will also collect visitor feedback through comment cards and a QR-code-based survey posted at the trail’s midpoint, using the data to refine the educational content over time.

The Pollinator Path is included with general admission to Harborleaf Botanical Garden. Garden members and children under 5 receive free entry. For more information about the trail, weekend walk schedules, or group visits, please contact the garden’s public information office.

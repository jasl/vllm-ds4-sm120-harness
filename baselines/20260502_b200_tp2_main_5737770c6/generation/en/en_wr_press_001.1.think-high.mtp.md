**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New Pollinator Path, Offering Practical Guidance for Home Gardeners**

**HARBORLEAF, MD – June 15, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through thoughtful planting choices. The 0.8-mile accessible trail, located along the east meadow loop, will officially open to the public on July 12, 2026.

Developed in response to repeated requests from garden visitors for more hands-on guidance on planting for pollinators, the trail aims to close the gap between general conservation awareness and actionable home gardening practices. Rather than focusing on broad ecological policy, the Pollinator Path emphasizes small, concrete steps that individuals can take in their own yards and community spaces.

The trail features a series of native plant demonstration beds, each showcasing species that provide nectar, pollen, or host-plant resources for specific pollinator groups. Visitors will find beds designed for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds, with clear labeling explaining which plants serve which species and during which blooming periods. A dedicated night-blooming flower station highlights plants that attract moths and other nocturnal pollinators, with signage describing how light pollution and garden timing can affect these creatures.

“What we hear most from our visitors is not ‘Why should I help pollinators?’ but ‘How do I actually do it in my own backyard?’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “They see the news about declining insect populations and they want to be part of the solution, but they need practical, specific advice. The Pollinator Path gives them exactly that—a living, walkable reference guide they can apply at home.”

To ensure accessibility for all visitors, the trail includes tactile signage with raised text and Braille for visually impaired guests, as well as interpretive panels with large-print descriptions and simple illustrations. Youth observation cards will be available at the trailhead, encouraging children to identify and record the pollinators they spot along the route, with prompts for sketching and note-taking. Shaded resting points, built with sustainably sourced materials, are spaced at regular intervals to accommodate visitors with mobility limitations or those who wish to pause and observe.

The trail’s east meadow loop location was chosen for its existing diversity of wildflowers and its proximity to the garden’s main apiary demonstration area. The path itself is surfaced with compacted crushed stone to support wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers, and the grade does not exceed five percent at any point.

In addition to the self-guided experience, Harborleaf will offer volunteer-led weekend walks each Saturday and Sunday through the end of October. These guided walks, led by trained garden docents, will provide deeper explanations of plant-pollinator relationships, seasonal timing, and habitat connectivity. No registration is required for the weekend walks, which are included with general garden admission.

Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is designed to address a recurring theme in visitor feedback: the desire for practical, neighborhood-level action. “One person planting a pollinator-friendly garden is a good start,” she said. “But when many people make small planting decisions—choosing a native milkweed over an ornamental, or adding a night-blooming vine—those individual choices create connected habitats across neighborhoods. The path shows how that connectivity works, block by block.”

The trail’s demonstration beds feature species native to the Mid-Atlantic region, including butterfly weed (Asclepias tuberosa), blue false indigo (Baptisia australis), cardinal flower (Lobelia cardinalis), and evening primrose (Oenothera biennis). Each bed incorporates seasonal rotation, so visitors returning throughout the year will see different blooms and associated pollinators.

Garden staff note that the Pollinator Path is part of a broader institutional commitment to ecological education that remains nonpolitical and science-based. The trail does not advocate for specific legislation, policy changes, or endorsements, focusing instead on voluntary, personal action and observation.

The July 12 opening day will feature a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony with garden staff at 10 a.m., followed by the first guided walk. Regular garden admission applies, with no additional fee for the Pollinator Path.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is open daily from 9 a.m. to sunset. For more information about the Pollinator Path, including a downloadable map and list of plants featured on the trail, visitors may contact the Garden’s Learning Programs office.

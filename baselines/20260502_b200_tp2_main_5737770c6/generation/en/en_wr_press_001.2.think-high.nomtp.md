**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Unveils New “Pollinator Path” to Connect Visitors with Native Species and Habitat Gardening**

**HARBORLEAF, MD – July 7, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile accessible public education trail designed to help visitors of all ages understand and support local pollinators. The trail opens to the public on Sunday, July 12, 2026, in the garden’s east meadow loop.

Developed in response to growing visitor inquiries about practical home gardening for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds, the Pollinator Path offers a self-guided, immersive experience. The trail features native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments, youth observation activity cards, shaded rest areas, and a weekly volunteer-led weekend walk.

“We have heard from many visitors who want to do more for pollinators but aren’t sure where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “Our new trail shows that small planting decisions—choosing a native milkweed instead of a non-native ornamental, or adding a patch of late-blooming asters—can create connected habitats that stretch across yards, parks, and neighborhoods.”

The Pollinator Path begins at the east meadow loop entrance, near the garden’s main parking area. The trail is fully accessible, with a packed gravel surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers. Interpretive signs are placed at regular intervals, offering clear, nontechnical explanations of pollination ecology, plant–pollinator relationships, and simple steps for establishing pollinator-friendly spaces at home.

A central feature of the trail is the series of native plant demonstration beds. Each bed highlights a different pollinator group: one dedicated to plants that attract bees and solitary wasps, another for butterfly and moth host plants, and a third focused on hummingbird-friendly flowers. Species include purple coneflower, butterfly weed, cardinal flower, and wild bergamot—all native to the mid-Atlantic region and chosen for their proven value to local insects and birds.

The night-blooming flower station, located near the midpoint of the trail, features plants such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-scented stock. This station is designed to illustrate the role of moths and other nocturnal pollinators. Tactile signage with raised botanical illustrations and Braille labels ensures that visitors with visual impairments can also engage with the content.

Youth observation cards are available at a dispenser at the trailhead. The cards prompt children to look for specific pollinator behaviors—such as a bee landing on a flower or a butterfly resting with wings open—and to draw or check off what they see. Garden staff recommend that families use the cards as a conversation starter about local wildlife and habitat conservation.

Shaded resting points, built from sustainably harvested wood and locally sourced stone, occur every quarter-mile along the trail. Each rest point offers seating and a small informational panel about the ecological role of the surrounding plant community.

Beginning July 19, 2026, Harborleaf Botanical Garden will offer a free, volunteer-led weekend walk along the Pollinator Path every Saturday at 10 a.m. and Sunday at 2 p.m. The walks are led by trained garden volunteers who provide additional context about the plants and pollinators observed along the route. No registration is required.

The trail was designed in collaboration with the garden’s horticulture and education teams. No external funding, celebrity endorsers, or political partners were involved in the project.

“The Pollinator Path is a direct response to what our community asked for,” Ortiz added. “It is not about making grand changes. It is about noticing the small, simple choices that add up, and showing people that anyone can create a refuge for pollinators in a flowerpot, a yard, or a community garden.”

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is open daily from 9 a.m. to 6 p.m. throughout the summer. Admission is $8 for adults, $4 for seniors and students, and free for children under 12 and for garden members. The Pollinator Path is included with general admission. For more information, including trail maps and a schedule of volunteer-led walks, visit the garden’s website or contact the visitor services office.

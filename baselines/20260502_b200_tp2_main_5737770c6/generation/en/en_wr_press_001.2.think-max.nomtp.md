**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” to Connect Community with Native Habitat Preservation**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the official opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new 0.8-mile public education trail designed to teach visitors how to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through practical, small-scale planting choices. Located along the east meadow loop, the accessible trail opens to the public at 9:00 a.m. on July 12, 2026, and features a series of demonstration gardens, interpretive signage, and volunteer-led programming.

The Pollinator Path was developed in direct response to a growing number of visitor inquiries seeking guidance on creating pollinator-friendly spaces at home. Garden staff noted that while many guests enjoyed the garden’s formal displays, they frequently asked for actionable advice on which native plants to choose, how to arrange them, and what specific features—such as night-blooming flowers or sheltered resting spots—could make a difference in urban and suburban landscapes.

“Our visitors have been telling us they want to help, but they don’t always know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “We built the Pollinator Path to show that even a single small planting decision—a pot of milkweed on a balcony, a patch of goldenrod in a backyard border—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods and support the insects and birds that sustain our ecosystems.”

The trail is fully accessible, with a smooth, compacted surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. It winds through the east meadow loop, a previously underutilized section of the garden that has been transformed into a living classroom. Key features include:

- **Native plant demonstration beds:** Each bed showcases a distinct pollinator group—bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds—and explains the specific plant species that support each group’s life cycle.
- **Night-blooming flower station:** A dedicated area featuring evening-blooming plants such as evening primrose, moonflower, and night-blooming jasmine, with interpretive signage explaining how moths and bats rely on these flowers after dark.
- **Tactile signage:** Braille and raised-text signs placed at every station, allowing visually impaired visitors to engage with the content through touch.
- **Youth observation cards:** Free, reusable cards available at the trailhead that guide children through activities such as counting pollinators, identifying flower shapes, and noting colors or scents.
- **Shaded resting points:** Three covered benches positioned along the route, each with a small information panel on how shade structures and water sources can benefit pollinators in hot weather.
- **Volunteer-led weekend walk:** Beginning July 13, trained garden volunteers will lead a free, guided walk on Saturday and Sunday mornings at 10:00 a.m., offering hands-on tips for planting and maintaining pollinator-friendly spaces.

The Pollinator Path emphasizes nonpolitical, educational content. It does not advocate for specific policies, agricultural practices, or political positions. Instead, it focuses on observable, science-based information about the relationship between native plants and local pollinator species. The garden’s horticulture team consulted with regional entomologists and native plant societies to ensure that all signage and demonstration beds reflect current ecological best practices.

“We are not telling people what to do in their gardens—we are showing them what is possible,” Ortiz added. “If a family plants one native shrub and sees a hummingbird visit the next morning, that experience can spark a lifelong commitment to habitat stewardship. That is the kind of change we hope to cultivate here.”

The trail’s opening coincides with peak summer bloom season, when the east meadow is alive with wildflowers and insect activity. Harborleaf Botanical Garden staff anticipate that the Pollinator Path will become a year-round attraction, with seasonal rotations of demonstration plants and interpretive materials to reflect changing bloom cycles and pollinator activity from spring through autumn.

The garden invites community members of all ages and abilities to explore the new trail. Admission to the Pollinator Path is included with regular garden entry, and youth observation cards are available free of charge at the visitor center. For those unable to visit in person, the garden plans to release a digital companion guide and a series of short educational videos later this summer.

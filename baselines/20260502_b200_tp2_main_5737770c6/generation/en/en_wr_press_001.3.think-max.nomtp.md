**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ to Teach Visitors How to Build Backyard Habitats**

**HARBORLEAF, MA – June 10, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will open its newest public education trail, the “Pollinator Path,” on July 12, 2026, offering visitors a hands-on guide to creating habitats for bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile accessible loop, located in the east meadow, features native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, tactile signage for all ages, youth observation cards, and shaded resting points. The trail was developed in response to repeated visitor requests for practical, actionable guidance on supporting local pollinators through small-scale planting choices.

The Pollinator Path is designed to bridge the gap between general appreciation for gardens and the specific steps individuals can take at home. Harborleaf staff observed that many visitors left the main garden inspired but unsure how to translate what they saw into their own yards, balconies, or community spaces. The new trail directly addresses that gap by focusing on native plants that provide nectar, pollen, and host materials for key pollinator species. Each demonstration bed is labeled with the plant’s common and scientific names, bloom season, and the specific pollinators it attracts.

A central feature of the trail is the night-blooming flower station, which highlights plants that open after dusk to support moths and other nocturnal pollinators. Tactile signage—including raised lettering and textured images—ensures that visitors with visual impairments can engage with the content. Youth observation cards, available at the trailhead, guide children through simple activities such as identifying flower shapes, counting visiting insects, and noting colors. Shaded resting points, built with recycled materials, are placed at intervals along the loop to accommodate visitors of all mobility levels.

“We heard time and again from our visitors that they want to help pollinators but don’t know where to start,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path shows that even small planting decisions—like choosing a native aster over a non-native annual—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods. A single backyard patch of milkweed can be a stepping stone for monarchs, and a row of bee balm can fuel an entire colony of bumblebees. Our goal is to turn inspiration into action.”

The trail will be open daily during regular garden hours beginning July 12, with volunteer-led weekend walks scheduled each Saturday and Sunday at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. through October 2026. Garden staff and trained docents will lead the walks, pointing out key species, explaining pollination ecology, and answering visitor questions. No advance registration is required for the weekend walks, though groups of ten or more are encouraged to contact the garden’s education office in advance for capacity planning.

Native plant demonstration beds along the path represent a variety of habitat types found in the region, from sunny meadows to shady woodland edges. Plant selections include butterfly weed (Asclepias tuberosa), wild bergamot (Monarda fistulosa), common milkweed (Asclepias syriaca), and cardinal flower (Lobelia cardinalis), each chosen for its proven value to specific pollinators. The night-blooming station features evening primrose (Oenothera biennis) and moonflower (Ipomoea alba), which open in the late afternoon and remain fragrant through the night. All plants used in the trail are sourced from regional nurseries that follow sustainable growing practices.

The Pollinator Path is the latest addition to Harborleaf’s ongoing public education mission, which includes seasonal workshops, school field trip programs, and a community seed library. The trail is fully accessible via a crushed stone surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and walkers, with grade changes held to less than five percent. Braille and large-print versions of the trail guide are available at the visitor center.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden is open year-round, with admission fees supporting its conservation and education work. The east meadow loop is located approximately a quarter-mile from the main parking lot, with a designated accessible drop-off point near the trailhead. For more information about the Pollinator Path, volunteer-led walks, or group visits, contact the garden’s learning programs office or visit the Harborleaf website.

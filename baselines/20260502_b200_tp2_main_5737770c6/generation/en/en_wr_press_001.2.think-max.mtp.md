**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New ‘Pollinator Path’ to Educate Visitors on Supporting Local Wildlife**

**HARBORLEAF, MD – July 12, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of its new “Pollinator Path,” a 0.8-mile accessible education trail designed to help visitors of all ages understand how small-scale planting choices can benefit bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The trail, located within the garden’s east meadow loop, officially opens to the public on July 12, 2026, and will feature a series of interactive learning stations, native plant demonstration beds, and volunteer-led weekend walks.

The Pollinator Path was developed in response to growing visitor interest in practical, do-it-yourself guidance on attracting and sustaining pollinator species. According to garden staff, guests frequently ask how to replicate the planting strategies they see at Harborleaf in their own backyards, community gardens, or balcony containers. The trail is intended to serve as a living classroom, showing which native plants support specific pollinators during different seasons and how to arrange plants for maximum ecological benefit.

“We noticed that many of our visitors leave inspired but unsure about how to apply what they see here at home,” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path is built around the idea that small planting decisions in a single yard, when replicated across neighborhoods, can create connected habitats that sustain entire pollinator populations. We want people to walk away with specific, actionable knowledge.”

The trail begins at the entrance to the east meadow loop, a flat, paved path suitable for strollers, wheelchairs, and mobility aids. Along the route, visitors will encounter five distinct stations. The first station features native plant demonstration beds that showcase species such as milkweed, coneflower, and bee balm, each paired with signage explaining which pollinators they attract and when they bloom. A second station is dedicated to night-blooming flowers, such as evening primrose and moonflower, designed to support moths and other nocturnal pollinators.

Each station includes tactile signage with raised lettering and botanical illustrations, making the experience accessible to visitors with visual impairments. In addition, a set of youth observation cards—available at the garden’s welcome kiosk and along the trail—invites younger visitors to record which insects they spot, encouraging observation and data collection.

Shaded resting points, constructed with locally sourced wood and covered with native climbing vines, are placed at regular intervals along the path. These points offer seating and a quiet space for reflection, as well as additional interpretive panels that describe the life cycles of local pollinator species.

On weekends, beginning July 13, the garden will host volunteer-led walks along the Pollinator Path. These walks are free with general garden admission and are led by trained docents who can answer questions about plant selection, soil preparation, and seasonal care. The garden encourages repeat visits, noting that the demonstration beds will change with the seasons and that different pollinators will be active at different times of year.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden has consistently emphasized educational programming as part of its mission, and the Pollinator Path is the latest in a series of initiatives aimed at making ecological science accessible to a broad audience. The garden’s existing programs include school field trips, adult horticulture workshops, and a community seed exchange. The Pollinator Path expands this mission by offering a self-guided, year-round resource that visitors can use at their own pace.

“This is not about political advocacy or large-scale land management,” Ortiz added. “It is about giving people practical tools. A tomato plant on a balcony, a patch of clover in a lawn, a row of sunflowers along a fence—these are all choices that add up. The Pollinator Path shows the connections in a tangible way, and we hope visitors leave feeling empowered, not overwhelmed.”

The opening of the trail coincides with peak summer bloom in the east meadow, where wildflowers and grasses provide a natural backdrop for the demonstration beds. Staff expect the path to attract both first-time visitors and returning members who wish to deepen their understanding of pollinator ecology.

The Pollinator Path is open daily from 9:00 a.m. to sunset. Admission to Harborleaf Botanical Garden is required, with discounted rates for seniors, students, and children under 12. Weekend walks begin at 10:00 a.m. and 2:00 p.m. and do not require advance registration. Groups of ten or more are encouraged to contact the garden in advance to ensure adequate docent availability.

For more information, including a map of the trail and a list of native plant species featured in the demonstration beds, visitors may visit the Harborleaf Botanical Garden website or contact the garden’s visitor services office.

**FOR IMMEDIATE RELEASE**

**Harborleaf Botanical Garden to Open New “Pollinator Path” Educational Trail on July 12**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – May 15, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden announced today the official opening of its newest public education attraction, the “Pollinator Path,” a dedicated trail designed to teach visitors how to support local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds through thoughtful planting. The 0.8-mile accessible loop, located in the garden’s east meadow, will open to the public on July 12, 2026, featuring a series of hands-on learning stations, native plant demonstration beds, and volunteer-led weekend programming.

The trail was developed in direct response to growing visitor demand for practical, actionable guidance on creating pollinator-friendly habitats at home. Over the past two seasons, garden staff documented a sharp increase in questions from guests about which plants attract specific pollinators, how to design small-scale gardens for maximum ecological benefit, and what steps individuals can take to support declining insect populations. The Pollinator Path aims to answer those questions through immersive, self-guided education.

“We kept hearing the same thing from visitors: ‘I want to help the bees and butterflies, but I don’t know where to start,’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “What started as a simple interpretive sign idea grew into a full loop with multiple teaching stations, because we realized that people need to see, touch, and smell the plants that make a difference. The Pollinator Path is designed to show that even small planting decisions—like swapping one ornamental shrub for a native milkweed or adding a patch of evening primrose—can create connected habitats that stretch across neighborhoods.”

The trail begins at the east meadow entrance and winds through a series of themed zones. The “Night-Blooming Flower Station” features plants such as evening stock, moonflower, and four-o’clocks, which are specifically chosen to attract moths and nocturnal pollinators. Nearby, the “Native Plant Demonstration Beds” showcase regional species including California fuchsia, coyote mint, and buckwheat, each labeled with tactile signage that allows visually impaired visitors to identify plants by touch. The signage includes raised text and braille descriptions, as well as QR codes linking to audio guides narrated by garden educators.

In addition to signage, the trail offers youth observation cards—laminated, reusable sheets that guide children through activities such as counting flower visits per minute and identifying insect body parts. These cards are available at a kiosk near the trailhead and can be returned after use. Shaded resting points with benches and interpretive panels are placed at roughly quarter-mile intervals, allowing visitors to pause and read about specific topics like solitary bee nesting habits or the relationship between flower color and pollinator preference.

Volunteer-led weekend walks will begin the morning of July 12 and continue every Saturday and Sunday through October. Led by trained garden docents, these 45-minute walks will focus on seasonal highlights, such as which nectar sources are most active during a given week. No registration is required, and walks are free with garden admission.

The location of the trail—the east meadow loop—was chosen for its existing diversity of native grasses and wildflowers, which garden staff have supplemented with additional plantings over the past 18 months. The loop is fully accessible via paved, level pathways, meeting Americans with Disabilities Act standards. Benches are designed with armrests and back support, and the tactile signage is positioned at a height accessible to wheelchair users and children alike.

Maya Ortiz emphasized that the Pollinator Path is not a political statement but an educational resource grounded in horticultural science. “Pollinators don’t care about state lines or property boundaries. A single backyard garden can serve as a vital rest stop for a migrating monarch or a nesting site for a solitary bee. We want visitors to leave with a clear, practical plan—three plants to add, one thing to stop doing, and a sense that their own small space matters in a larger network.”

To celebrate the opening, the garden will host a ribbon-cutting ceremony at 9:00 a.m. on July 12, followed by the first guided walk at 10:00 a.m. Regular garden hours apply; admission is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under five. Garden members receive free entry year-round.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, established in 1972, is a nonprofit horticultural institution dedicated to plant conservation, education, and public enjoyment. The garden maintains more than 50 acres of themed collections, including a desert garden, a redwood grove, and seasonal butterfly enclosures. For more information about the Pollinator Path or other programs, visit the garden’s website or contact the media relations team.

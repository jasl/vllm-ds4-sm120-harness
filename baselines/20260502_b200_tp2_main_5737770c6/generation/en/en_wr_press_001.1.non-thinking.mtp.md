**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens ‘Pollinator Path’ to Guide Visitors on Creating Wildlife-Friendly Habitats**

**HARBORLEAF, CA – July 12, 2026** – The Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the official launch of the “Pollinator Path,” a new public education trail designed to provide practical, hands-on guidance for planting to support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile accessible trail, located along the east meadow loop, opens to the public on July 12, 2026, and aims to address a growing demand from visitors for clear, actionable steps to transform private gardens and community spaces into connected pollinator corridors.

Developed in response to frequent inquiries from garden guests seeking more than just aesthetic advice, the Pollinator Path offers a self-guided educational experience that demonstrates how small planting choices can have a measurable impact on local ecosystems. The trail features a series of native plant demonstration beds, each highlighting species known to attract and sustain specific pollinators, from drought-tolerant manzanita for early-season bees to showy milkweed for monarch butterflies. A dedicated night-blooming flower station showcases plants such as evening primrose and jasmine, which open after dusk to support moths and nocturnal pollinators. All trail components are designed to be fully accessible, with smooth surfaces suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids.

“The single most common question we hear from visitors is, ‘What can I plant in my own yard that will actually help the bees and butterflies?’” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path answers that question directly. It shows that a single pot on a balcony or a small strip of native flowers along a fence line can be a vital stepping stone. By linking these small planting decisions across neighborhoods, we create connected habitats that support the entire life cycle of pollinators.”

The trail is punctuated by tactile signage, which uses raised images and Braille labels to describe plant characteristics and pollinator behaviors, making the experience accessible to visitors with visual impairments. For younger explorers, the garden has introduced youth observation cards—laminated guides that encourage children to identify specific pollinators, record their sightings, and note which plants they visit. Shaded resting points, constructed from sustainably sourced materials and equipped with seating, are positioned at regular intervals along the loop, allowing visitors to pause, observe, and reflect.

In addition to self-guided exploration, the Pollinator Path will host a volunteer-led weekend walk program beginning Saturday, July 13. Trained garden volunteers will lead small groups through the trail on Saturday and Sunday mornings from 9:00 a.m. to 11:00 a.m., offering commentary on plant-pollinator relationships and answering questions in real time. The walks are free with general garden admission, and no advance registration is required.

The design and content of the Pollinator Path were informed by visitor feedback collected over the past two years. According to garden staff, many guests expressed a desire to move beyond passive enjoyment of the garden’s beauty and toward active participation in conservation efforts. The path emphasizes practical application rather than political advocacy, focusing entirely on ecological science and horticultural best practices. No corporate or celebrity endorsements are associated with the project, and funding was provided solely through the garden’s existing operational budget and member contributions.

“This is not about making a statement—it’s about making a difference where people live,” added Ortiz. “Whether you have a window box or an acre, the same principles apply: choose native plants, provide water, avoid pesticides, and plant for continuous bloom from early spring to late fall. The Pollinator Path gives everyone the tools to start.”

The east meadow loop, which previously served as a less-trafficked walking trail, was selected for its natural transition between open meadow and woodland edge—habitat types that are particularly valuable for pollinator diversity. The garden’s horticulture team worked over the past winter to install the demonstration beds, signage, and seating, with an emphasis on species that are both regionally appropriate and low-maintenance. Species along the trail include California lilac, yarrow, goldenrod, and various salvias, all of which require minimal supplemental water once established.

The Pollinator Path is open during regular garden hours: daily from 8:00 a.m. to sunset. Admission to the garden is $12 for adults, $8 for seniors and students, and free for children under 12 and Harborleaf members. The trail is also open to school groups and community organizations by appointment.

For more information, including a downloadable observation card and a full list of plant species along the trail, visitors are encouraged to visit the garden’s website or call the main office.

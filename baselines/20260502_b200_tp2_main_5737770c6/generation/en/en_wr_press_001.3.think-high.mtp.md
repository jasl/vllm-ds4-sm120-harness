**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens New Pollinator Path to Inspire Community Habitat Efforts**

**HARBORLEAF, PA – June 2, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden today announced the opening of the “Pollinator Path,” a new public education trail designed to demonstrate how small-scale planting choices can support bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The trail will officially open to visitors on July 12, 2026, along the east meadow loop, a 0.8-mile accessible route that winds through native plant demonstration beds, a night-blooming flower station, and multiple shaded resting points.

The project was developed in direct response to visitor feedback. Over the past two years, garden staff noted a recurring theme in guest comments and program evaluations: many visitors expressed a desire for more practical, hands-on guidance about which plants to grow at home to attract and sustain local pollinators. Harborleaf’s horticulture and education teams collaborated to design a trail that translates scientific best practices into an immediate, sensory experience.

“We kept hearing people say, ‘I want to help the bees, but I don’t know where to start,'” said Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden. “The Pollinator Path gives visitors a real-world look at how small planting decisions—choosing one native plant over a non-native, or leaving a patch of bare ground for ground-nesting bees—can create connected habitats across entire neighborhoods. Our goal is to show that every garden, no matter how small, can be part of a larger pollinator corridor.”

The trail’s design emphasizes accessibility and multi-sensory learning. The 0.8-mile loop features a smooth, compacted surface suitable for wheelchairs, strollers, and mobility aids. Along the route, tactile signage provides raised illustrations and braille descriptions of key plants and insect life cycles, allowing visitors who are blind or have low vision to engage with the content. Youth observation cards will be available at the trailhead; children can use them to record sightings of pollinators and note specific plant preferences, turning a casual walk into a structured scientific observation.

One of the trail’s signature features is a night-blooming flower station, which will showcase species that attract moths and nocturnal pollinators. This station will be illuminated with low-level moonlight-colored lighting that does not interfere with the flowers’ natural cycles or disorient night-flying insects. Information panels at this station explain the ecological role of moths and highlight the contrast between day and night pollination strategies.

Native plant demonstration beds are planted in clusters that mirror natural habitat types: a dry, sandy patch for species like butterfly milkweed and purple coneflower; a moist, shaded area for cardinal flower and blue lobelia; and a sun-drenched section for goldenrod, asters, and bee balm. Each cluster is labeled with common and scientific names, bloom periods, and the specific pollinators it supports, giving visitors a clear reference for replicating similar groupings at home.

Shaded resting points are spaced at regular intervals along the trail, each equipped with a bench and a small interpretive panel that offers a “habitat tip of the spot”—a simple suggestion such as leaving leaf litter for overwintering insects or providing a shallow water source for bees. The trail also includes a designated “pause zone” where volunteers from Harborleaf’s docent program will lead weekend walks starting in late July. These free, volunteer-led walks will run each Saturday morning through the end of September, offering visitors the chance to ask questions in real time while observing active pollinators.

The Pollinator Path was funded through Harborleaf Botanical Garden’s general operating budget and visitor support programs. No external grants or partner organizations were involved in the trail’s development. The garden plans to maintain and update the trail’s interpretive content on an annual basis to reflect shifts in plant performance and emerging pollinator research.

Harborleaf Botanical Garden, located at 240 Meadow Lane, has served the region for more than 40 years as a public space for conservation education, plant research, and community recreation. The garden’s 65 acres include native woodlands, a sensory garden, a greenhouse complex, and more than 500 labeled plant species. The institution operates year-round and does not receive state or federal funding for its educational programming.

“This trail is not about telling people what not to do,” Ortiz added. “It is about showing what can be done, easily and beautifully, to make a measurable difference in pollinator health. When someone plants a small patch of phlox or leaves a caterpillar on a milkweed, they are joining a larger network of stewards. That is a powerful thought, and we hope the Pollinator Path helps people see how close that power lies to their own backyards.”

The Pollinator Path will open at 9 a.m. on July 12, 2026. Regular garden admission applies. Youth observation cards will be available free of charge at the visitor center. No separate reservation is required for the trail, but weekend walks will have a limit of 20 participants and may fill quickly.

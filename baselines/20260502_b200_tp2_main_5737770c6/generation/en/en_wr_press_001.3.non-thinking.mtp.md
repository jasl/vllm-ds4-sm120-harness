**Harborleaf Botanical Garden Opens “Pollinator Path” Educational Trail July 12**

**PORTSMOUTH, NH – June 22, 2026** – Harborleaf Botanical Garden will officially open its new “Pollinator Path” public education trail on Sunday, July 12, 2026, offering visitors a hands-on, accessible learning experience focused on supporting local bees, butterflies, moths, and hummingbirds. The 0.8-mile loop through the east meadow is designed to address a growing demand from garden visitors for practical, actionable information on how to create pollinator-friendly habitats at home.

The trail, which begins at the eastern edge of the meadow near the old oak grove, is fully accessible and features a series of interactive educational stations. Highlights include native plant demonstration beds showcasing species that provide nectar and host plants for regional pollinators, a dedicated night-blooming flower station to illustrate the role of moths and nocturnal insects, and tactile signage for visitors with visual impairments. Shaded resting points are spaced along the route, and youth observation cards—laminated guides with prompts for sketching and noting insect visitors—are available for young explorers.

A volunteer-led weekend walk will begin at 10:00 a.m. every Saturday and Sunday from the trailhead, with trained docents offering informal commentary on the plants and animals seen along the path. The trail is open during regular garden hours, from 8:00 a.m. to dusk.

The Pollinator Path is the direct result of visitor feedback surveys conducted over the past two years, which consistently identified a need for more concrete guidance on planting for pollinators. Harborleaf staff analyzed more than 1,200 comment cards and noted the same question recurring: “What can I do in my own yard to help bees and butterflies?” Maya Ortiz, Director of Learning Programs at Harborleaf Botanical Garden, said the trail was designed to answer that question in an immersive, low-barrier way.

“Our visitors have told us they want to help, but they often don’t know where to start,” Ortiz said. “The Pollinator Path shows that small planting decisions—choosing a single native milkweed instead of an ornamental, letting a patch of clover grow, or planting a night-blooming jasmine—can create connected habitats across an entire neighborhood. A window box or a small backyard garden can become a stepping stone for a migrating monarch or a local bumblebee colony.”

The trail’s sequence of stations mirrors a logical progression for home gardeners. The first demonstration bed features early-season bloomers such as Virginia bluebells and wild columbine, which provide critical food for queen bumblebees emerging from hibernation. Subsequent beds highlight mid-summer and late-season plants like goldenrod, asters, and showy tick-trefoil, emphasizing the importance of having blooming plants from spring through fall. The night-blooming station, open after dusk during extended summer hours, showcases evening primrose, four-o’clocks, and moonflower, with interpretive panels explaining how moths use scent and pale colors to navigate in low light.

Tactile signage, installed at each station, features raised botanical illustrations, braille descriptions, and text in large-print font. The signs are mounted on angled posts to accommodate wheelchair users and visitors of varying heights. Youth observation cards, printed on waterproof paper, contain prompts such as “Count how many bees visit this flower in one minute” and “Draw the shape of a moth you see here.” Garden staff will collect completed cards each week and post observations on a community board at the trailhead.

The Pollinator Path is a permanent addition to the garden’s east meadow. No construction was required for the trail surface, which follows an existing gravel path that was widened and stabilized to meet accessibility standards. Harborleaf’s horticulture team spent the spring installing the demonstration beds, using only plants native to the coastal Northeast region. No pesticides or synthetic fertilizers have been applied to the trail area.

In addition to the weekend walks, the garden plans to host a monthly “Pollinator Power Hour” at the trailhead on the second Wednesday of each month from July through October, where visitors can meet with a garden horticulturist and ask questions about their own planting projects. The first session will take place July 14 at 5:30 p.m.

The garden will hold a brief ribbon-cutting ceremony at 9:30 a.m. on July 12 at the trailhead, followed by a guided inaugural walk led by Ortiz and members of the volunteer docent team. The event is free with regular garden admission.

The opening comes at a time when public interest in pollinator conservation has grown steadily. Harborleaf staff note that while large-scale agricultural and policy changes are important, the trail’s educational approach focuses on what individuals can control: the plants they choose, the chemicals they avoid, and the spaces they share with non-human neighbors.

“We are not making policy recommendations or lobbying anyone,” Ortiz said. “We are simply providing information and inspiration. If a family leaves here and plants one aster in a pot on their balcony, that is a meaningful act. Connect a thousand such acts across a city, and you have a network. That is the idea the Pollinator Path is meant to bring to life.”

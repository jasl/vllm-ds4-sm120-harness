The Map of Every Puddle

The summer rain had stopped. The whole world smelled like wet dirt and washed-clean leaves. Drops still hung from the bluebell stems, sparkling like tiny glass beads in the returning sun.

At the base of the old stone wall, a small snail named Silas was very, very busy. He inched his careful way across the garden path, his shell a neat spiral of brown and cream. Underneath his soft belly, he was pushing a rolled-up leaf. It was his most important possession: his Map of Every Puddle.

Silas was not fast. He was never fast. But he was thorough. He stopped at a shiny, silver dime-sized pool near a crack in the wall. “Puddle Number Six,” he murmured, unrolling his leaf. With a tiny leg, he traced a wet mark onto his map. “Shaped like a bean. Depth: one and a half snail-bodies. Sun will reach it by mid-afternoon.”

Every puddle in the garden was different. The deep, dark one under the rhubarb leaf (Puddle Number Three) lasted for days. The skinny, wiggly one between the paving stones (Puddle Number Eleven) was the best for a cool sip. Silas knew them all. Knowing the map kept him safe, and knowing when a puddle would dry was, to a careful snail, very fine bravery.

Suddenly, a shadow flickered over him. *Whoosh!*

A small sparrow tumbled out of the sky, landing not in a graceful swoop, but in a clumsy flop that sent a splash of muddy water all over Silas’s map.

“Oh! Bother and feathers!” cheeped the sparrow. She was young, with a fluffy chest and wings that seemed a little too big for her body. Her name was Pip.

Silas sighed a long, slow snail sigh. “That was my best puddle corner,” he said quietly.

Pip shook herself, sending more droplets flying. “Sorry! I was trying to fly. I mean, I *was* flying. Then I tried to turn, and the wind tickled me, and— *plop.*” She looked at Silas with bright, sorry eyes. “You’re awfully slow. How do you ever get anywhere?”

“Slow is how I arrive safely,” said Silas, carefully wiping mud from his leaf map. “I need to know where I am. Especially now, after the rain. Have you seen Puddle Number One? It’s the biggest.”

“Oh,” said Pip, looking sad. “So my favorite splashing spot is disappearing?”

Pip fluffed her feathers. “I don’t like patience. I want to fly *now.* I want to loop and zoom and feel the wind under my belly. But coming down is so hard! The ground comes up so fast!”
